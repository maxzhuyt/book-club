# Love in the Limelight

The bell above the door had a particular tone—flat, unimpressed, like a teacher who had lived long enough to stop being surprised by anything—and Mara had always liked it for precisely that quality. It sounded now, and she did not look up.

"We're not buying today," she said, from behind a tower of Penguins she was cataloguing. "Sign's on the door."

"I'm not selling anything."

She looked up.

The man standing inside her bookshop with his collar turned up and his hands in his pockets was Declan Reeve, whom fourteen million people on Instagram followed, whose face had recently appeared on the side of a bus on the A470, and who had, if the entertainment press was to be believed, dated three different women simultaneously without any of them knowing, which Mara privately considered logistically impressive if nothing else.

"I know," she said, and returned to the Penguins.

A pause. She could feel him recalibrate.

"The street's a bit—there's a situation out there," he said. "Paparazzi. I just need five minutes. I'll buy something."

"The sign says closed."

"You're clearly open."

"I'm clearly doing inventory."

"Could I—" He tried a smile. She could hear it in his voice, the warmth deployed like a searchlight sweeping professionally across the dark. "Could I possibly wait it out? Five minutes at most. I'm very quiet."

"You're already talking."

"I could stop."

She looked at him again over the top of the stack. He was, she admitted to herself privately and without pleasure, extraordinarily good-looking, in the way that certain rocks were good-looking—all drama and no apology, formed by forces that had nothing to do with accommodation.

"Over there," she said, nodding at the corner by the Welsh literature shelf. "Don't touch the map drawer."

---

He was not quiet. He lasted perhaps ninety seconds before drifting toward the shelf and pulling out a collection of R.S. Thomas with the manner of a man who had learned that showing interest in objects was a good way to seem interesting himself.

"Do people actually read him?" he said.

"People who read."

"That's—yes, fair." He opened it, and she expected him to close it again immediately and put it back and perhaps say something about how he'd always meant to read more poetry. He didn't. He read something, his brow doing something complicated that she didn't quite intend to notice. "He's brutal," he said, not performing this time. Just saying it, to the page.

"He's honest. The two look similar from a distance."

Declan glanced at her. She was back to the catalogue sheet. He put the Thomas down without putting it back and looked at the shelf.

"Do you get many people in here wanting actual books," he said, "or mostly wanting the Wales experience? Little independent bookshop, bit of rain outside, bit of melancholy inside, photograph for the story they tell about themselves?"

"Is that why you came in?"

"I came in," he said, "because there were four cameras and a man with a microphone."

"Right." She made a note. "You get many of those?"

"Enough." He leaned against the shelving with the ease of someone who had learned to make every surface a stage—the lean saying, I am at home everywhere, which she suspected was the loneliest possible thing to have learned. "Occupational hazard. I'd say you get used to it, but that's what people say about things you don't actually get used to."

She continued writing.

He moved along the shelf. She could track him by sound: the soft drag of a spine withdrawn, replaced; the slight creak of the floorboard she had been meaning to fix for three years; the pause by the poetry that lasted longer than she expected. When she looked up, he was holding a different book, a thin thing with a green cover, and he was frowning at it with what appeared to be genuine puzzlement.

"Gwenallt," she said, before he asked.

"Welsh?"

"Entirely."

"Do you read Welsh?"

"I do."

He looked at her. Then at the book. Then back at her, with a quality of attention that was different from the quality he'd arrived with—less directed, more actual. "Is it better in Welsh?"

"Most things that are Welsh are better in Welsh. The language was built for a particular kind of grief." She paused. "And for weather."

He set the Gwenallt down carefully and picked up the Thomas again. "He writes about God like he's furious at him for existing."

"He writes about God like he's furious at himself for needing him. It's different."

Declan was quiet for a moment. "That is different," he said, and she had the sense he was thinking about something other than the poem.

---

"There was a piece about you," she said, eventually. She hadn't intended to say it; it arrived on its own, the way things did when you'd been nearly alone for too many hours and someone said something that unlocked a door you'd meant to leave locked. "Last month. In one of the supplements. You said something about the camera."

He didn't tense, exactly. But something behind his expression stood up straighter.

"I say a lot of things for pieces," he said.

"You said you didn't entirely exist when it wasn't pointing at you."

A beat.

"They loved that," he said. His voice was easy. The ease was constructed; she could see the joints of it. "Very quotable. Tortured artist, the whole performance."

"Was it true?"

The question sat between them for a moment, on the counter like an object set down.

He opened his mouth, and she watched him consider the charming answer, the self-aware answer, the answer that would make her think well of him for having given it, and then she watched him put it down.

"Sometimes," he said. "When I was about twenty-two, I had six months where I wasn't working at all. Completely unknown at that point. Nobody wanted me for anything. And I'd wake up in the morning and I genuinely wasn't sure, for a moment, whether I was—" He stopped. Looked at the cover of the Thomas. "This is very strange to tell a stranger."

"I'm a bookseller," she said. "We hear an enormous amount of strange. It's a kind of confessional, a good bookshop. People think it's the books they're talking to."

He looked at her for a moment, then back at the shelf, and she understood that the looking-at-the-shelf was the version of what she'd just done with the catalogue—something to do with the face.

"I wasn't sure whether I existed when no one was watching," he said. He said it faster now, getting it out whole rather than letting it sit too long. "Like a tree falling in a forest. That whole thing. It passed. Obviously." He made a small and very controlled gesture. "I compensated. Have compensated. Enthusiastically and at some scale."

She was quiet.

Outside, a car moved through a puddle on the street, a long slow sound like something being erased.

"I had a cat," she said, without quite meaning to.

He waited.

"When I first opened this place, there was a cat. She used to sleep on top of the biography section, which I thought was fitting. She died—" Mara set her pen down, though she hadn't been writing. "I keep thinking I should get another one. I keep saying I will. But I keep not doing it, because." She stopped. "I don't know what that has to do with anything."

"I think you're saying," he said, carefully, as though handling something that might break, "that sometimes you don't go back to the thing that helped because losing it again would be worse than not having had it."

She looked at him.

He looked back at her with an expression she couldn't quite categorize—not triumphant, not even particularly pleased. Just present. She found it difficult to look at directly, like a word in a language you've studied for years that you finally hear spoken by a native: the thing itself, after a long time of approximations.

"You're more perceptive than I expected," she said.

"You expected an idiot."

"I expected someone who needed everyone to like him."

"And?"

"Still gathering data." She picked up her pen. "It's provisional."

He laughed. She had braced for the famous laugh—had heard it in interviews, that warm, well-timed thing that audiences received like a gift—and this was not it. This was slightly too loud and a half-second longer than it should have been and it surprised her entirely out of her composure so that she was smiling before she had the opportunity to decide not to.

"What's your name?" he said, when he'd recovered himself.

"Mara."

"I'm—"

"I know who you are."

"Everyone knows who I am," he said, and she heard what was underneath it, the thing the entertainment piece hadn't quite gotten to. "It's a peculiar kind of loneliness, actually. Everyone knows the same version of you. The one they decided on."

She didn't say anything, because she was thinking about it instead, really thinking about it rather than supplying the thing that sounded like having thought about it.

"The Thomas is twelve pounds," she said, after a moment. "If you want it."

---

He brought it to the counter.

She wrapped it in brown paper without being asked—an old habit, automatic, and she was already doing it before she noticed she was doing it, which was its own small piece of information—and pushed it across the counter toward him. Their hands did not touch. She was aware, with some precision, of the distance between his hand resting on the package and hers on the counter's edge.

"You didn't have to do that," he said, about the paper.

"Old habit."

He looked down at it. Something in his expression was careful in a way that hadn't been there when he'd arrived.

"The street should be clear by now," she said.

"Probably." He tucked the book under his arm. Didn't move immediately. Outside she could hear nothing specific—no raised voices, no camera shutters, just the ordinary sounds of a Welsh afternoon sorting itself out in the rain. "If it isn't—"

"It'll be clear," she said.

He nodded. She had the impression of something being put back into a pocket. He went to the door, and his hand was on it, and he paused and turned back in the way that she had seen—everyone had seen—in those films: the turn in the doorway that meant something was about to be said that couldn't be left unsaid.

She looked up, not because she'd forgotten to but because she allowed herself to.

"Mara," he said.

She waited.

"Good shop," he said.

The bell rang its flat, judicious note. The door closed. Through the window she watched him cross the street, collar still up, the brown-paper parcel visible under his arm, and she watched him until he turned the corner and wasn't there, and she sat for a moment with her pen not quite touching the paper, looking at the door and the grey ordinary light coming through it. Then she looked down and wrote something in the wrong column, and paused, and looked at it, and crossed it out, and did not immediately write the correct thing in the correct place.

She sat with the mistake for a moment before she fixed it.
