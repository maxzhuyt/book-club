# Love in the Limelight

The bell didn't ring; it clattered, a harsh, broken sound that made Elara look up from her ledger. 

The man who shoved his way inside was soaked, his trench coat plastered to his shoulders. He threw his weight against the door, pressing his forehead against the frosted glass. Outside, the flash of cameras strobed through the rain, turning the quiet Welsh street into a silent, frantic disco.

"Sorry," the man said, breathless. 

"We're closed."

"Just five minutes." He didn't turn around. He was still watching the street, his shoulders tense. 

Elara didn't move from the counter. She watched a drop of water fall from the hem of his coat and soak into the threadbare rug. "You're dripping on the folios."

He finally turned. The ambient light from the streetlamp caught his face. It was a face built for fifty-foot screens, all sharp angles and calculated symmetry, currently ruined by exhaustion and the frantic energy of a hunted animal. He reached up to push his wet hair back and stopped. 

His fingers came away red. He stared at the blood, then touched a scratch on his cheek, right below his eye. 

"It's raining," he said, as if that explained everything.

"I hadn't noticed."

He let out a short, hollow laugh and wiped the blood on his coat sleeve. He started to pace the narrow aisle, running a hand over the spines of the books. His movements were too large for the space, too dramatic, as if he were trying to hit a mark on a stage.

"Do you have any poetry? Contemporary?"

"Second aisle, back wall."

He stopped pacing and pulled a slim volume from the shelf. He didn't open it. He just held it, staring at the cover. 

"I don't read poetry," he said to the spine of a Doris Lessing novel. "But someone told me I should start."

"Usually people start with the classics."

"I don't have time for the classics."

"I don't think they mind."

He looked at her then, really looked at her. The manic energy drained out of him, leaving something brittle in its place. 

"Do you know what it's like," he said, "to spend six months pretending to be someone else, and then when you stop, you aren't sure if you exist when no one is watching?"

The silence stretched. Outside, the flashes stopped. The paparazzi were moving on. 

Elara closed her ledger. She picked up a pen and began to trace the grain of the wood on the counter. 

"I had a cat," she said. "A ginger tom. He died three years ago. Sometimes I still step over the place where he used to sleep on the rug."

He didn't move. He didn't say anything. He just watched her trace the wood grain. 

"Did you get another cat?"

"No."

He nodded, slowly. He walked to the counter and set the book down. "I'll take the poetry."

"That's 4.50 pounds."

He pulled a damp ten-pound note from his pocket and laid it on the wood. "Keep the change."

"We don't do tips." She slid the change across the counter. 

He didn't take it immediately. His hand hovered over the coins, close to hers. The air in the shop felt suddenly thin. Then he scooped the money up, put the book in his pocket, and walked out into the rain. 

Elara watched him go. She picked up her pen and opened the ledger. She wrote the sale in the wrong column, putting the 4.50 pounds under expenses instead of income. 

She sat with the mistake for a moment before she fixed it.
