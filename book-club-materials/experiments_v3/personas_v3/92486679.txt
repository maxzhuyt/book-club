REVIEW 1 (for "The Parisian Chapter"):
Readers who thrilled to the war-time story of Odile in Janet Skeslien Charles’s earlier novel THE PARIS LIBRARY will now be entranced by her protegé Lily’s own time at the same library half a century later. In this follow-up to that massive global sensation, THE PARISIAN CHAPTER reunites us with aspiring writer Lily, who takes a programs manager job at the American Library in Paris (ALP) when she loses both her room-mate Mary Louise and much-needed rent money. Plunged into a dysfunctional workplace on the brink of financial disaster, Lily must navigate a ragtag group of staff, donors and patrons while trying—and mostly failing—to book famous authors for reading events and dealing with her toxic boss. As Lily uncovers more of the library’s past, she also finds clues to Odile’s time there sixty years earlier, including an estrangement which forces Lily to face the reality and rupture of her own friendship with Mary Louise. Skeslien Charles, an American living in Paris, has written a wonderfully Austen-like novel of manners, infusing her depiction of modern Parisian life with wit and insight. As the narrative moves between different characters, Skeslien Charles gives each one tremendous emotional and psychological depth; as with all of her books, reading about these very different people repairing past relationships, while reviving their own dreams in life, is tremendously heartening, winning and inspirational.
