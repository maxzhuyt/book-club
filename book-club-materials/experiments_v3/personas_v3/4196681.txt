REVIEW 2 (for "Little Mouse"):
Little Mouse is the nickname Doktor Theodore Goldstein and his wife Lisl give their son Theo when they are forced to hide him in the cellar of their Berlin house in the days following Kristallnacht. Theo is not quite four and he is our eyes and ears for the first part of this novella which begins on Kristallnacht itself. He is woken by tinkling noises and, in his excitement, mistakes them for fairy bells. The author does an admirable job here of entering into the perceptions, at once lucid and muddled, of a young child. We experience the horror of the Nazi terror campaign through the response of Theo. A few days later, when all the house’s broken windows are boarded up a Mephistophelian character named Schmeling arrives with an offer of help. Theo instinctively recoils from the self-proclaimed Saviour. Thus the family are constrained to forge a pact in order to save themselves and the Faustian theme of the novella takes shape. I don’t want to give away the plot but suffice to say Mephistopheles always returns demanding his due and the security of the Goldstein family will once more be under threat. It has been a constant mystery to many people how passively the Jews sometimes submitted to Nazi persecution. Little Mouse tells a different story. This is the tale of an uprising. The most successful part of this novella is unquestionably the voice of young Theo. The author has pitched his childish wonder and fear on a beautifully crafted resounding note. When Theo is at the helm the novella reads like a poignant fable. Later in the book, when bureaucracy is a key factor in the book’s plot, the narrative voice takes on a more realist register and, once or twice, suspension of disbelief becomes an issue. However, the denouement is genuinely exciting as is the development of young Theo from terrified child to protector of his faith.
