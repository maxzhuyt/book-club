REVIEW 1 (for "Frogsong"):
I loved Frogsong and its vivid exploration of grief and its various forms. Melissa Manning captures this in a raw and realistic way. The story follows Caro and Danny – best friends and lovers, who grew up together at the waterhole. It’s a place that feels like a character itself, and where they spend countless hours across the years. 
  “Born within weeks of one another, the two of them were entwined, inevitable as the mingling collusion of hydrogen and oxygen.”
I found myself thinking about these characters away from the book – even analysing my own relationships at their respective ages, along with the complexities of grief and the ways it can impact different individuals. It was quite thought-provoking.I felt like a fly on the wall to Caro and Danny’s relationship, one that I couldn’t look away from. It was hard to put down. Grief was often present, or lurking in shadows, and yet the theme of metamorphosis was life-affirming and beautifully depicted alongside the life cycle of frogs – something reinforced with excellent illustrations by Dale Gilkes.
  “Escaping the confines of the waterhole and yet always drawn back.”
Frogsong is an incredibly sensual read, immersive in its descriptions of both addiction and sexuality. I loved how transportive it was too – from Tasmania to Portugal and back. Distinctively Tasmanian, it’s literally set in my backyard, and I found many aspects close to home or relatable. These could easily have been people I know, and this helped ensure I formed a deep connection to the characters.
  “Sometimes on a Sunday, they’d catch a bus to Blackmans Bay. Spend the day at the beach - towels and packets of crisps, goon bags pulled from their boxes. Fruity Lexia poured into plastic cups from someone’s backpack.”
Melissa doesn’t waste her words and writes in a style that feels minimalist yet profound, slotting into a hypnotic rhythm that meant chapters flowed effortlessly into the next. I kept wanting to know how things would work out for those involved. I’ll forever have a place in my heart for Angelo and his dog, Derek – the best neighbours. REVIEW 2 (for "Cured: The Tale of Two Imaginary Boys"):
Being a fellow drummer, I jumped at the chance to read Lol Tolhurst’s memoir - The Cure are one of my favourite bands.Unfortunately, the book turned into a series of we did this, then did that. Lol gets drunk, urinates often and describes various outfits he wore. I would’ve loved greater focus on songwriting and recording. Instead, such moments were vague - crashing a jeep while drink-driving is the only thing mentioned about the making of Kiss Me, Kiss Me, Kiss Me. There were too many disconnected ramblings and unnecessary details. I started to lose interest due to mundane stories with zero relevance except for those who lived them. More interesting parts were mere snapshots of the world - the Berlin Wall, paying bribes in Europe, working class suburbs of Thatcher’s England. Sadly, these reflections were typically followed by diva tales - like forcing Glastonbury fans to wait while they watched soccer on TV. Lol seemed to relish in describing himself as a “hard man” that people avoided getting into fights with. He was difficult to like.Lol briefly touched on vulnerability when describing his mother’s passing and how it contributed to his alcoholism. But this was overshadowed by other flaws in the book. As a tale of rock n roll - it’s poor. As a tale of overcoming alcoholism - it’s also poor. Both subjects have been told countless times previously and usually better than here.I considered abandoning this but persisted. I’m glad the book ended on a somewhat upbeat note. I was pleased to learn Lol has been enjoying sobriety and repairing friendships with The Cure. Despite not liking this memoir, I wish him plenty of success and happiness and will continue to enjoy his drumming for years to come.
  “…the real purpose, if there ever was one, of the Cure: to serve as the template for a kind of emotional therapy”
