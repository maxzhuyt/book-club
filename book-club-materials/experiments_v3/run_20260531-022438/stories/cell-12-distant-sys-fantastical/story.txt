# The Weight of Bronze Leaves

The Sibylline keeper's hands trembled as he held the transcription, and Marcus Porcius Cato watched those hands with the particular disgust he reserved for weakness in public men. The keeper was a priest of adequate lineage and inadequate spine, and the parchment shook as though the words themselves carried fever.

"Read it again," Cato said. "Clearly, this time."

The keeper glanced toward the presiding consul, Metellus Celer, who offered a curt nod from the curule chair. Around the chamber, three hundred senators arranged themselves in postures of attention that ranged from genuine alarm to performed solemnity. Cato could distinguish between them easily. Twenty years in this chamber had taught him which men feared the gods and which men merely feared appearing not to.

"The consultation was performed at dawn, according to proper ritual," the keeper began, his voice steadying into the drone of procedural recitation. "The question posed concerned the proposed grain distribution in the eastern provinces, as requested by this body fourteen days prior. The leaves were drawn, interpreted by the full college, and verified against the index of Tarquinius."

"The instruction," Cato said. "Not the methodology."

The keeper swallowed. "The sibyl's words, as recorded: *Let Rome return the lion to his den, or Rome shall learn what lions eat.*"

Silence held the chamber for a precisely calculated moment—long enough for piety, short enough for politics. Then Publius Clodius Pulcher laughed from the back benches, a sound like breaking pottery.

"A lion," Clodius said, rising without recognition from the consul. "We ask about grain shipments to Syria, and the dead priestess speaks of lions. Perhaps the college might consult a bestiary rather than bronze leaves."

"You will address the chair," Metellus Celer said, "or you will not address the chamber at all."

Clodius offered an elaborate gesture of deference so theatrical it bordered on mockery. Cato marked it, added it to the long accounting he kept of Clodius's offenses against dignity and republic alike. The man had dressed as a woman to infiltrate the Bona Dea rites not two years past. That he still held his seat was evidence of how deeply rot had penetrated Roman virtue.

"The chair recognizes Publius Clodius," Metellus said, his tone suggesting he would rather recognize a case of plague.

"I merely observe," Clodius said, spreading his hands as though offering gifts, "that divine guidance proves remarkably consistent in its obscurity. When the guidance favors our interests, we find it clear as spring water. When it does not—" He gestured toward the keeper. "Lions. Dens. Poetry suited to a Greek stage."

Marcus Licinius Crassus shifted on his bench, the movement drawing eyes as intended. He did not rise, did not request recognition, merely shifted—and conversation paused around him like water around a stone. His fortune could purchase half the men in this chamber and lease the other half. When Crassus moved, men attended.

"The keeper will perhaps clarify," Crassus said, still seated, his voice carrying the particular softness of a man who had never needed to shout. "The college's interpretation. Not merely the words, but their application."

The keeper looked again to Metellus, received another nod, and consulted a second document. "The college's preliminary interpretation suggests the lion refers to a king or ruler currently held from his proper throne. The phrase 'return to his den' indicates restoration. The consequence clause—what lions eat—carries standard formulaic weight indicating military or civic disaster."

"A king," said Lucius Lucceius from the middle benches. He was a historian by temperament, a senator by inheritance, and he leaned forward now with the particular hunger of a man scenting footnotes. "Which king? Egypt's Ptolemy remains in Alexandria. Pontus is settled. Armenia—"

"Ptolemy is not in Alexandria."

The voice came from near the entrance, and heads turned toward Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus as he strode into the chamber. Pompey the Great, hero of the eastern campaigns, conqueror of pirates and kings, dressed today in a toga of such pristine white it seemed to generate its own light. His timing, Cato noted, was impeccable. It always was. The man staged his entrances like a Greek dramatist.

"Ptolemy Auletes fled Alexandria six months ago," Pompey continued, not waiting for recognition, knowing no consul would dare demand it. "A popular uprising. He's been residing in Ephesus, spending borrowed money and awaiting Roman assistance." Pompey paused at the center of the floor, allowing the chamber to arrange itself around him like iron filings around a lodestone. "I have had correspondence."

Of course he had. Cato felt his jaw tighten. Pompey had correspondence the way other men had meals—constantly, enormously, from every corner of a world he had personally rearranged.

"The chair recognizes Gnaeus Pompeius," Metellus said, the words carrying a slight edge. He was Pompey's brother-in-law and no fonder of him for it.

"If the sibyl speaks of lions," Pompey said, "she speaks of Egypt. The lion is Ptolemy's symbol. The den is his throne. The instruction is unambiguous—Rome must restore him, or suffer divine consequence." He turned slowly, letting his gaze touch each faction of the chamber. "I propose we debate the method, not the mandate."

"A moment." Cato stood, feeling his knees protest the cold marble. He was not young anymore, but neither was he yet willing to let younger men drive the republic off cliffs. "Before we accept this interpretation as settled fact, I would hear from the augurs present. Gaius Claudius—you observed the morning signs?"

An elderly senator near the front stirred, rousing from what had appeared to be sleep but was more likely strategic withdrawal. Gaius Claudius Marcellus had read birds for forty years. His eyes, milky with incipient cataracts, turned toward the high windows where winter light fell weak and gray.

"The signs were troubled," he said. "A crow flew against the sun's path during the third hour. Two eagles circled the Capitol without landing. The sacred chickens ate, but without enthusiasm."

"Without enthusiasm," Clodius repeated. "The chickens were melancholy. Shall we postpone the republic's business until poultry morale improves?"

"You mock what you don't understand," Gaius Claudius said, and there was iron beneath the age in his voice. "I have seen men die for less reverence than you show. I have seen cities burn."

"We have all seen cities burn," Clodius said. "Generally because men with armies decided to burn them, not because chickens ate sadly."

"Enough." Metellus struck his staff against the floor. "The question before this body is not whether the gods speak—this is settled law and settled fact. The question is whether this particular instruction compels Roman action, and if so, what form that action should take."

A hand rose from the benches nearest the door—Gaius Julius Caesar, praetor, debtor, and schemer, his handsome face arranged in an expression of thoughtful concern that Cato trusted not at all. The man smiled too easily and owed too much money to too many of the wrong people. Crassus had purchased his debts entire. What Crassus purchased, Crassus owned.

"The chair recognizes Gaius Julius Caesar."

"I wonder," Caesar said, rising with fluid economy, "if we might acknowledge the practical dimensions alongside the sacred. Ptolemy Auletes is not merely a foreign king. He is a client of Rome, bound by treaties my own uncle helped negotiate. He pays tribute. He provides grain. Egypt's stability serves Roman interests regardless of divine instruction."

"Egypt's instability serves some interests as well," Cato said. "A restoration would require troops. Troops require a commander. I notice certain parties have shown great eagerness to interpret this oracle in ways that might require their particular services."

He did not look at Pompey. He did not need to.

"You suggest," Pompey said, his voice cooling by several degrees, "that I have manufactured divine guidance for personal advancement?"

"I suggest nothing about manufacture. I observe only that when the gods speak, their words somehow consistently align with the ambitions of men who have legions."

The chamber stirred, that particular rustle of three hundred men recalculating their positions. Cato had spoken the unspeakable—not that anyone doubted it, but that it had now been said aloud, recorded by the scribes, entered into the permanent memory of Roman debate.

"Marcus Porcius Cato impugns my piety." Pompey's hand moved to his chest, touching the fabric over his heart in a gesture of wounded honor. "I, who have restored more temples than any man living. I, who have triumphed three times with full divine sanction. I, who—"

"Who have conquered half the known world and hunger still," Cato said. "Yes, your piety is well documented. As is your appetite."

"Gentlemen." Crassus rose at last, and both men fell silent. His interventions were rare enough to carry weight and wealthy enough to carry threat. "We lose sight of the central question. The sibyl has spoken. The interpretation offered is plausible, perhaps even likely. But plausibility is not certainty, and Rome has erred before in reading divine will."

He walked slowly toward the center of the chamber, his footsteps deliberately measured. Cato watched him with the wariness one reserved for venomous things—Crassus was dangerous precisely because he never seemed to be.

"I propose," Crassus continued, "a further consultation. Let us send to Delphi. The Pythia may clarify what the sibyl has obscured. If both oracles align, we may act with confidence. If they conflict—" He spread his hands. "Then we will know that divine will is not yet settled, and mortal caution is warranted."

"Delphi," Pompey said. "A journey of weeks. Months, perhaps, if winter seas delay the return. Meanwhile, Ptolemy languishes, Egypt festers, and Roman credibility erodes."

"Roman credibility erodes when we act rashly on ambiguous guidance," Crassus said. "The gods respect deliberation. They are immortal. They understand patience."

"Do they?" The voice came from above, from the gallery where observers sometimes gathered, and every head in the chamber turned upward.

A young woman stood at the gallery's edge, dressed in a simple white tunic that seemed wrong for the winter cold. Her feet were bare. Her hair was dark and unbound, moving slightly in a breeze that touched nothing else in the chamber. Cato felt the temperature drop, felt the hair rise on his forearms, felt something ancient and terrible in his bones that his rational mind tried to reject and could not.

"Do the gods understand patience?" she asked. Her voice carried without effort, filling the space the way light fills a room. "Do the gods respect deliberation? You speak of us as though we were your servants, waiting on your convenience."

No one moved. No one breathed. The scribes' styluses hung frozen above their tablets.

"Who—" Metellus began.

"I am who is sent," the woman said. "I am the message and the messenger. I am the patience of heaven reaching its end." She looked down at the chamber with eyes that held no pupil, no iris, only a golden luminescence that hurt to meet. "The sibyl spoke. You quibble. The augurs warn. You mock. The signs accumulate. You deliberate."

She raised one hand, and the light through the high windows dimmed, as though clouds had suddenly thickened—but there was no sound of wind, no natural explanation for the darkness that pressed against the chamber's edges.

"Return the lion to his den," she said, and her voice was no longer singular but layered, harmonic, as though a chorus spoke through one throat. "You have until the moon completes her cycle. What follows, if you fail, will not be poetry."

The light returned in a rush that left spots dancing across Cato's vision. The gallery was empty. The cold lingered.

For a long moment, no one spoke.

Then Clodius, because he was Clodius and incapable of appropriate silence, said: "Well. That was theatrical."

"Silence." Metellus's voice shook. His face had gone the color of old marble. "This session is—this session will recess. The college of pontiffs will convene immediately. The augurs will take new signs. This body will reconvene when—when proper consultation has been completed."

"Consul." Pompey's voice cut through the rising murmur of terrified senators. "Before we adjourn. Let the record show that divine instruction has been delivered directly to this chamber. Let the record show that Rome has been warned, specifically and with deadline, by power beyond mortal question. Let the record show—" His eyes found Cato. "—who argued for delay."

Cato felt the weight of those words, felt the political ground shifting beneath him. A month ago, he could have blocked Pompey's Egyptian ambitions through procedural obstruction and factional alliance. Now the gods themselves had intervened—or appeared to—and opposition would mark him not as a defender of republican principle but as an enemy of heaven.

Unless the intervention itself was manufactured. Unless Pompey's resources extended to women who could disappear from galleries and speak in choruses. Unless—

But he had felt the cold. He had seen those eyes. Thirty years of skepticism told him there must be explanation, mechanism, trick. Thirty years of Roman piety, bred into his bones before skepticism had taken root, told him otherwise.

"Let the record show," Cato said slowly, "that Rome has always honored the gods. Let the record show also that Rome has always demanded proof against deception, even—especially—when deception wears divine masks. I do not oppose the gods. I oppose those who would use their names for earthly gain."

"You saw what I saw," Pompey said.

"I saw something," Cato said. "What I saw, I do not yet know."

The session dissolved into chaos—senators clustering, arguing, praying, a few simply fleeing toward the doors. Cato remained in his seat, watching Pompey and Crassus exchange glances across the chamber, watching Caesar slip toward the exits with the particular haste of a man who had somewhere else to be, watching the keeper of the Sibylline books clutch his documents like a child clutching a blanket against the dark.

Outside, somewhere in the city, a dog began to howl. Then another. Then dozens, their voices rising in a chorus that might have been fear or might have been greeting.

The gods were not subtle, Cato thought. They never had been.

But men who wanted to seem like gods learned subtlety first.

He would need proof before he acted—in either direction. The moon's cycle gave him twenty-nine days. In a republic rotting from within, surrounded by ambitious men and uncertain signs, twenty-nine days might be enough.

Or it might be twenty-nine days too few.

The session of the Roman Senate stood adjourned, and Rome stood waiting, and somewhere in Ephesus a deposed king dreamed of lions while the Mediterranean winter gathered its storms.
