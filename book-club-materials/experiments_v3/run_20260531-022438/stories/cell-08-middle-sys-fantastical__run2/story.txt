# The Mammoth Question

## Chapter One

The Imperial Society for Megafaunal Studies held its monthly meetings in a chamber that smelled, perpetually and despite all efforts, of preservation salts. This was perhaps inevitable given that the room shared a wall with the Society's lesser preparation laboratory, where at this very moment two assistants were engaged in the delicate work of mounting a juvenile ground sloth skeleton acquired from the Buenos Aires consignment. The odor had, over the years, worked itself so thoroughly into the oak paneling and leather chairs that several members had simply ceased to notice it. Others had never managed this adjustment and sat with handkerchiefs pressed delicately to their noses, giving the appearance of men in chronic states of grief.

Sir Edward Carrow, who presided over the Society's meetings from behind a desk grotesquely carved with scenes of mammoth hunts (a gift from a Siberian fur merchant seeking favorable mention in the *Proceedings*), called the meeting to order at half past seven. The usual matters were dispatched with: the treasurer's report, the election of a corresponding member from Edinburgh, a brief dispute about whether the late Professor Whitstone's collection of megatherium teeth ought to pass to his nephew or to the Society itself.

It was only when these preliminaries had concluded that the room's atmosphere shifted.

"We come now," said Sir Edward, "to the matter of the proposed expedition."

In the third row of seats, a young man with an unruly shock of ginger hair and the weathered complexion of someone who had spent more time in the field than was strictly respectable for a gentleman naturalist leaned forward. This was Thomas Marchmont, and he had waited six months for this moment.

"Mr Marchmont," Sir Edward continued, with the tone of a man announcing an irregular verb, "you may present your proposal."

Marchmont rose, gathering the leather portfolio that contained eighteen months of correspondence, three hand-drawn maps, and what he privately considered to be the most thorough budget ever prepared for a scientific expedition. He made his way to the front of the room and unrolled the largest of his maps upon the lectern, weighing its corners with brass paperweights shaped like tiny woolly rhinoceroses—another gift from the Siberian merchant, who had evidently cornered the market in megafauna-themed desk accessories.

"Gentlemen," Marchmont began, "I propose that this Society mount an expedition to the Yeniseisk mammoth grounds, departing no later than April of next year, with the aim of securing for Britain's collections specimens that will restore our nation to its rightful place at the forefront of megafaunal science."

He paused, allowing this to settle. Several members exchanged glances.

"I use the word 'restore' deliberately," Marchmont continued. "I would ask the members to consider our present holdings. Our finest mammoth—the bull in the Great Hall—was collected in 1849. Twenty-three years ago, gentlemen. Its hide has faded to the color of dirty straw. Two of its tusks have been replaced with plaster reconstructions after the originals were damaged in the flooding of '61. Meanwhile—"

"We are all aware of the Society's inventory, Mr Marchmont," interrupted a voice from the back of the room. This belonged to Horace Pembury, a stout man of sixty who had not personally participated in a collection expedition since the reign of William IV but who nevertheless considered himself the final authority on all such matters. "There is no need for theatrical recitations."

"I mean no theatrics, Mr Pembury. Only to establish the necessity. The Prussians acquired three adult mammoths in their Kamchatka expedition of '69—three, including a pregnant cow whose preserved calf now draws ten thousand visitors a year to the Berlin museum. The French have a breeding pair of ground sloths in their Jardin des Plantes. Living specimens, gentlemen. Meanwhile we display our faded bull and speak of past glories."

"The Society's finances—" began the treasurer, a nervous man named Coggins who seemed perpetually alarmed by any sentence that might conclude with a figure.

"Are sufficient," Marchmont said. He opened his portfolio and produced a sheaf of papers covered in dense columns of numbers. "I have corresponded extensively with the Russian-American Trading Company regarding transport costs. I have secured preliminary agreements with three experienced guides who worked the Yeniseisk grounds in the Prussian expedition. I have obtained estimates for preservation supplies, shipping, and insurance against loss. The total expenditure, including a contingency of fifteen percent, would be eight thousand, four hundred pounds."

A murmur passed through the room. It was not, Marchmont noted with some satisfaction, a murmur of outrage. It was the murmur of men who had expected a larger number.

"The matter of expense is secondary," said another voice. This was Dr. James Ashworth, who occupied a peculiar position within the Society: he was its most published member, its most frequently cited authority, and also its most reliable opponent of any proposed action whatsoever. He had perfected the art of raising objections so thoroughly researched that they could not be dismissed, yet so narrowly focused that they never quite amounted to a coherent counter-position. "The matter of diplomatic relations is primary. I need hardly remind this body that our relations with the Russian government remain... delicate."

"Delicate," repeated Pembury, with evident relish. "Indeed. After the Ashford affair."

Several members shifted uncomfortably. The Ashford affair was not typically mentioned in open session. Three years prior, a Society expedition to the Lena River delta had gone spectacularly wrong when its leader, one Colonel Ashford, had gotten into a dispute with a local administrator over collection permits. The dispute had escalated. Rifles had been brandished. The Colonel had been briefly imprisoned. The Foreign Office had been forced to intervene. The incident had provided ammunition to every parliamentary critic who believed that natural history expeditions were merely pretexts for imperial meddling, and the Society had been quietly blacklisted from Russian territories ever since.

"The Ashford affair," Marchmont said carefully, "was a failure of leadership, not of concept. Colonel Ashford was a military man who approached specimen collection as though it were a cavalry exercise. I am proposing something quite different: a small party, no more than six, traveling under the auspices of legitimate scientific exchange. I have drafted a letter to the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg proposing a collaborative arrangement. British funding, Russian oversight, shared publication rights. It offers them a face-saving mechanism."

"You have drafted a letter," said Ashworth, "to a foreign government. Without authorization from this body."

"A draft only. Unsent. I would not presume—"

"And yet you have presumed a great deal, it seems."

Marchmont felt his color rising but forced himself to remain steady. "I have prepared thoroughly, Dr. Ashworth. As I believed this Society would wish me to do."

Sir Edward raised a hand, forestalling further exchange. "Let us hear the proposal in full before we debate its particulars. Mr Marchmont, you may continue."

Marchmont smoothed the map before him, running his finger along the blue thread of the Yenisei River as it wound northward through the Siberian taiga. "The Yeniseisk grounds represent the largest remaining concentration of woolly mammoths in Eurasia. Current estimates—and I have corresponded with both Nordenskiöld and Middendorff on this point—suggest a population of between eight hundred and twelve hundred animals. The herds migrate seasonally between the river lowlands and the upland plateaus, following patterns that have been well documented since the 1840s. A summer expedition, properly timed, could intercept the herds during their July grazing period, when the animals are most accessible and the weather most favorable for preservation work."

"Preservation work," said a younger member near the front, speaking for the first time. This was William Holt, who served as the Society's assistant curator and who had, Marchmont knew, his own reasons for supporting the expedition. "You intend to collect specimens, then. Not to observe merely."

"I intend," Marchmont said, "to collect at minimum one adult specimen suitable for mounting. Ideally a mature bull, though I would defer to circumstances in the field. Additionally, I would hope to secure skeletal material, tissue samples for the anatomical collection, and—if opportunity presents—juvenile specimens or preserved embryonic material of the sort the Prussians obtained."

"You speak of a mature bull," said Pembury. "You speak of it as though one might simply walk up to the creature and ask it to kindly lie down and be preserved."

Scattered laughter. Marchmont waited for it to subside.

"The Siberian hunters have refined their techniques considerably since the last British expedition. The preferred method now employs a combination of driven corrals and—" he hesitated, knowing this would be controversial, "—and targeted culling using modern rifles. The animal can be dispatched cleanly, with minimal damage to the hide or skeletal structure. Preservation can begin within hours rather than days."

"Culling," said Ashworth, loading the word with decades of academic disapproval. "Let us be plain, Mr Marchmont. You propose to shoot a mammoth."

"I propose to collect a specimen, Dr. Ashworth. By the most efficient means available."

"And what of the ethical considerations?"

This was the objection Marchmont had anticipated most, and dreaded most, because it had no clean answer. The debate over megafauna collection had grown increasingly heated in recent years. The great beasts were not, after all, infinite in number. The Siberian mammoths had declined sharply from their populations of a century ago; the South American ground sloths more sharply still. There were voices—not yet dominant, but growing louder—who argued that collection should cease entirely, that the surviving populations should be left unmolested, that the age of natural history cabinets stuffed with exotic specimens was giving way to something else, something not yet clearly defined.

Marchmont had prepared his response to this objection with great care.

"The ethical considerations," he said, "are precisely why we must act now rather than later. The mammoth population is declining, Dr. Ashworth. You know this as well as I do. The Siberian herds have contracted by an estimated thirty percent since reliable counts began. If present trends continue—"

"If present trends continue," Ashworth interrupted, "it is in part because of collection expeditions. Ours and others'. We are contributing to the very decline we claim to lament."

"The primary causes of decline are habitat loss due to agricultural expansion and hunting by local populations for ivory and meat. Scientific collection accounts for a tiny fraction of annual mortality. And more to the point: the specimens we collect serve a purpose that simple preservation cannot. They educate. They inspire. They advance human knowledge. If the mammoth is destined to diminish—and I fear it may be—then surely it is better that future generations should have some record of its magnificence than that it should vanish entirely unmemorialized."

It was a well-rehearsed argument, and Marchmont could see that it had landed with some of the members. But Ashworth was shaking his head.

"You speak of memorializing. But a stuffed specimen in a museum hall is not a memorial, Mr Marchmont. It is a trophy. It tells future generations not what the mammoth was, but what we did to it."

The room had grown very quiet. Marchmont felt the familiar sensation of a debate slipping away from him, tilting toward the comfortable inaction that was the Society's default state. He opened his mouth to respond—

And was interrupted by a knock at the chamber door.

Sir Edward frowned. The Society's meetings were not to be disturbed; this was a point of long-standing protocol. "Yes?"

The door opened to reveal a young clerk from the Society's administrative office, looking deeply uncomfortable. "Apologies, Sir Edward. An urgent message has arrived. From the Foreign Office."

A ripple of unease passed through the room. Messages from the Foreign Office were rarely welcome. Sir Edward extended his hand and the clerk scurried forward, depositing a cream-colored envelope before retreating hastily.

Sir Edward examined the envelope, broke its seal, and read in silence. His expression, never particularly animated, grew stonier still.

"Gentlemen," he said finally, "it appears Mr Marchmont's proposal has become rather more urgent than any of us anticipated."

He set the letter down on the desk.

"The Foreign Office has received intelligence—I am not told through what channels—that a German expedition departed Hamburg three weeks ago, bound for Vladivostok and thence to the Yeniseisk grounds. The expedition is privately funded but travels with official sanction. Its stated purpose is to collect breeding stock for a proposed megafauna preserve in Bavaria."

"Breeding stock," Holt breathed. "They mean to take live animals."

"So it would appear."

The room erupted. Marchmont stood at the lectern, momentarily forgotten, watching as decades of institutional caution collided with something more primal: the fear of being beaten, of being second, of watching a rival nation claim a prize that ought by rights to have been British.

"We cannot permit—"

"The logistics of live transport are impossible, surely—"

"The Prussians managed it with the ground sloths—"

"That was South America, entirely different conditions—"

"Gentlemen." Sir Edward's voice cut through the noise. "Gentlemen, please."

Order gradually reasserted itself. Sir Edward turned to Marchmont.

"Mr Marchmont. Your proposed timeline. You spoke of an April departure."

"Yes, Sir Edward. To allow sufficient time for—"

"Could it be accelerated?"

Marchmont's heart was pounding. "I... with sufficient resources and authorization, yes. A February departure would be possible. Difficult, but possible. We would arrive at the grounds by late May, ahead of the German party if they are indeed traveling via Vladivostok."

"And the diplomatic obstacles? The Russian situation?"

"The letter I drafted. It could be sent immediately. Or—" Marchmont hesitated, then committed to the gamble, "—or the Foreign Office might be persuaded to intervene directly. If they are sufficiently concerned about German ambitions in the region, they may find it convenient to have a British scientific presence as well."

Sir Edward was silent for a long moment. Then he turned to face the room.

"I propose," he said, "that we vote on Mr Marchmont's expedition proposal, amended as follows: the expedition shall depart no later than the fifteenth of February; it shall operate under Foreign Office guidance regarding diplomatic protocols; and its objectives shall be expanded to include, where feasible, the acquisition of live juvenile specimens suitable for transport."

"Live specimens?" Ashworth's voice was sharp with disbelief. "Sir Edward, you cannot be serious. The mortality rate alone—"

"I am entirely serious, Dr. Ashworth. If the Germans mean to establish a breeding population, we cannot afford to return with mere carcasses. The game has changed." He paused. "All in favor?"

The vote was not close. When it was done, Marchmont stood at the lectern with his maps and his careful budgets and his eighteen months of preparation, feeling as though he had been handed something far heavier than he had asked to carry.

"Congratulations, Mr Marchmont," Sir Edward said, with no warmth whatsoever. "You have your expedition. I suggest you begin preparations immediately."

The meeting adjourned. Members filed out, some clapping Marchmont on the shoulder, others avoiding his eye. Ashworth departed without a word, his disapproval radiating from him like heat from a stove.

Marchmont remained at the lectern, looking down at his map, at the blue thread of the Yenisei winding through territory he had never seen, toward animals he had only studied in books and museum halls.

Live specimens. Breeding stock. A race against the Germans.

He had wanted an expedition. He had been handed something else entirely: a test he had not studied for, with consequences he could not yet imagine.

Through the wall, faintly, he could hear the sounds of the preparation laboratory: the tap of small hammers, the scrape of wire against bone. The assistants were still at work on the ground sloth, building an armature to hold its skeleton upright, arranging its bones into a posture of frozen motion.

Marchmont rolled up his maps, gathered his papers, and went out into the London night to begin.
