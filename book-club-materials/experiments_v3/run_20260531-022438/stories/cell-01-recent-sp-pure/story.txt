# The Directive

## Berlin, December 1942

---

The radiator clanked twice and fell silent. Kriminalinspektor Walther Brack lifted his head from the burglary report and waited, but the heat did not return. He pulled his coat tighter and continued writing, his pen scratching across paper that had grown coarse and gray since the war began. Even paper felt the rationing now.

Outside his window, the Alexanderplatz lay under a thin crust of frozen snow. Office workers hurried past with their collars raised. A woman pushed a pram, her breath trailing white behind her. Ordinary people conducting ordinary business on an ordinary Thursday afternoon. Brack had learned to treasure such ordinariness. It meant no one was looking at you.

He was noting the estimated value of stolen copper pipes—a crime so tedious it felt almost peaceful—when Feldmann appeared in his doorway.

"Something for you." Feldmann held out an envelope as if it might ignite. "Courier just brought it."

Brack set down his pen. The envelope was heavy cream stock, the kind that hadn't existed for civilians in two years. In the upper left corner, an eagle clutched a wreathed swastika above two words in Gothic script: *Reichsführer-SS*.

He did not reach for it.

"The courier is waiting," Feldmann added. "Said you're expected within the hour."

"Expected where?"

"He didn't specify. I assume—" Feldmann's eyes moved to the envelope and then quickly away, as if he'd glimpsed something indecent. "I assume you'll find out."

Brack took the envelope. The paper was smooth and cold against his fingertips. He slit it open with his thumbnail and withdrew a single sheet bearing three typed sentences and a signature he did not recognize. Some functionary. Some deputy's deputy.

*Kriminalinspektor Brack is directed to present himself at Prinz-Albrecht-Straße 8 at 15:00 hours today. He will ask for Sturmbannführer Lange. This matter requires discretion.*

Discretion. In the vocabulary of the Reich, the word carried weight that pressed against the chest.

Feldmann was still standing in the doorway, not quite looking at him. They had worked together for six years. They had never discussed politics. They had never needed to.

"The copper pipe case," Brack said, folding the letter back into its envelope. "Reassign it to Drescher."

"For how long?"

Brack buttoned his coat. "I don't know."

---

The cold outside was bitter and immediate. Brack walked rather than requisitioning a car. He needed the time, and he needed to think, and perhaps some part of him hoped the walk would never end—that he could keep moving through the frozen streets forever, suspended in the space before whatever awaited him.

Prinz-Albrecht-Straße. Everyone in Berlin knew what those words meant. The Gestapo had made their home there for a decade. And now Heydrich had claimed it entirely, consolidating his empire of information and fear into a single address. Since Prague, since surviving the Czechs' grenades with nothing worse than a shattered spleen and three months of recovery, Heydrich had become something more than a man in the public imagination. The newspapers called him iron-willed. The whispered jokes—fewer every month—called him something else. The Hangman. The Blond Beast. Himmler's brain and now Himmler's heir, since the Reichsführer's heart attack in October had left him diminished and largely ceremonial.

Brack passed a newsstand. The headlines announced victories in Stalingrad that felt increasingly like the kind of victories one stopped questioning. He turned south on Friedrichstraße and let the crowd carry him.

He had been a policeman for eighteen years. He had joined the Kripo in 1924, when the Republic still seemed like it might mean something, and he had stayed because the work was what he knew. Investigating. Following evidence. Building cases that held together when you pressed them. For a time, that had been enough. The Party had come, and the laws had changed, and he had kept his head down and worked his burglaries and his assaults and his occasional murder. He had not joined. He had not refused. He had existed in the gray space where most people existed, telling himself that ordinary crimes still mattered, that someone had to care about stolen copper pipes while the world arranged itself into shapes he did not recognize.

Now an envelope with Heydrich's seal sat in his coat pocket, and the gray space felt very small.

The first checkpoint materialized at the corner of Prinz-Albrecht-Straße. Two SS men in long coats examined his identification with the blank efficiency of men who had examined ten thousand identifications before his. One of them made a telephone call. Brack stood in the cold and watched his breath dissolve.

"Proceed to the main entrance. You'll be met."

He proceeded. The building rose before him, gray stone and barred windows and all the charm of a mausoleum. A second checkpoint at the door. A third in the lobby. At each one, men examined his papers and made notations and spoke into telephones, and at each one, Brack felt himself diminishing slightly, becoming less a person and more a unit being processed through a system designed for processing units.

A young Unterscharführer finally led him up a staircase and down a corridor lined with doors that revealed nothing. Their footsteps echoed on marble. Somewhere behind one of those doors, Brack knew, people were having conversations they would not survive. He had heard the stories. Everyone had heard the stories. You simply chose how much of your mind to give them.

The Unterscharführer stopped before an unmarked door and knocked twice.

"Enter."

The office beyond was modest by the standards Brack had expected. A desk, two chairs, a filing cabinet, a portrait of the Führer mounted on the wall behind. The man behind the desk wore the black uniform of an SS-Sturmbannführer and possessed the kind of face that would vanish from memory the moment you looked away—thin lips, pale eyes, hair the color of nothing in particular.

"Kriminalinspektor Brack. Sit."

Brack sat.

The Sturmbannführer—Lange, presumably—did not introduce himself. He opened a folder on his desk and studied its contents for a long moment while Brack waited. The performance of authority. Brack had seen it countless times in countless offices. It meant nothing except that the man wielding it wanted you to feel small.

"You investigated the Kranzler matter in September," Lange said finally. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"The jeweler found dead in his shop. You concluded suicide."

"The evidence supported that conclusion."

"The evidence." Lange closed the folder. "You have a reputation, Brack. Methodical. Thorough. Not inclined to see what you are expected to see." He paused. "Some might consider that a liability."

"Some might."

"But the Reichsführer-SS considers it an asset. In certain circumstances."

Brack said nothing. There was nothing safe to say.

Lange rose and moved to the window. Outside, the gray afternoon was already darkening toward evening. Berlin in December offered perhaps six hours of genuine daylight, and most of those hours were the color of dishwater.

"Early this morning, a body was discovered in the Grunewald. A man in civilian clothes. No identification. He had been shot once in the back of the head." Lange turned from the window. "The death itself is not unusual. What is unusual is that the body was found by a unit conducting a training exercise, and that unit reported it through military channels before anyone could intervene. The report has already reached three different offices. It cannot be unseen."

"You want me to investigate the death."

"I want you to investigate the death and reach the correct conclusion."

"Which is?"

Lange returned to his desk and extracted a photograph from the folder. He slid it across to Brack. The image showed a man's face, slack in death but still recognizable. Late fifties. Heavy features. A scar along the jawline.

"Do you recognize him?"

Brack studied the photograph. Something tugged at his memory—a name glimpsed in a file, a face seen across a crowded room—but it would not resolve.

"No."

"His name is Werner Kessler. Until three weeks ago, he was a senior administrator in the Reich Main Security Office. He retired quietly for health reasons. He had a wife in Potsdam and a hunting lodge in Bavaria and every reason to live out his days in comfortable obscurity." Lange took back the photograph. "And yet someone put a bullet in his brain and left him in the forest like a discarded cigarette."

"What was his position exactly?"

"That is not your concern."

"If I'm to investigate—"

"You are to investigate the circumstances of his death. Not his life. Not his work. Not the contents of his office or his files or his correspondence. You will determine who killed him and you will do so without asking questions that do not require answers."

Brack felt the trap closing around him, elegant and inescapable. A man from Heydrich's own apparatus murdered in the woods. An investigation that must exist because reports had already been filed, because too many people knew. But an investigation with walls built around it, walls he must not climb over.

"And when I determine who killed him?"

"You will report your findings directly to this office. To me. To no one else." Lange leaned forward slightly. "You will commit nothing to writing. You will keep no notes. You will discuss this matter with no one in your department. As far as the Kripo is concerned, you have been seconded to assist with administrative matters. Nothing more."

"And if my investigation leads somewhere inconvenient?"

The question hung in the air between them. Lange's expression did not change, but something shifted behind those pale eyes—a flicker of what might have been amusement or might have been warning.

"Then you will remember who sent you. And you will remember that inconvenient conclusions are a luxury afforded to men who do not wish to continue breathing."

Brack looked down at his hands. They were steady. He was distantly surprised.

"I will need access to the body. And to the location where it was found."

"Arrangements have been made. A car will collect you tomorrow morning at seven." Lange pushed a slim envelope across the desk. "Your authorization. It will open most doors. But not all doors, Kriminalinspektor. You would do well to remember which ones remain closed."

Brack took the envelope and stood. There was nothing else to say. The directive had been delivered, and he had received it, and now he walked back out into the corridor with its echoing marble and its silent doors. The Unterscharführer was waiting to escort him out.

At the final checkpoint, the guards returned his identification without comment. He stepped through the doors and into the December evening. Full dark now, the streetlamps casting pale circles on the frozen pavement. Somewhere in the distance, a siren wailed and faded.

He had been given an impossible task. Investigate a murder but ask no questions. Find a killer but see nothing inconvenient. Report to Heydrich's office and trust that his usefulness would outweigh his knowledge. 

Three paths lay before him. He could obey completely, follow the boundaries Lange had drawn, and deliver whatever neutered conclusion they required—and live with the knowledge that he had become an instrument of something he did not understand. He could investigate honestly, follow the evidence wherever it led, and accept that the moment his findings became troublesome, he would join Werner Kessler in some frozen forest. Or he could run, tonight, disappear into the chaos of wartime Europe and hope that Heydrich's reach had limits.

None of these paths led anywhere good.

Brack turned up his collar and began walking north, back toward the Alexanderplatz, back toward an office where a burglary report sat unfinished on his desk. Tomorrow at seven, a car would collect him. Tonight, he would go home to his empty apartment and sit in the dark and try to remember the man he had been before today—the man who believed that ordinary crimes still mattered, that copper pipes were worth investigating, that the gray space offered safety.

That man had received an envelope. That man no longer existed.

The city swallowed him, and he walked on through the frozen dark, carrying his dangerous knowledge like a stone in his chest, wondering which of the paths before him led to morning and which led only to the forest.
