# The Mammoth Question

## Chapter One

The gentlemen of the Sub-Committee for Vertebrate Acquisition had been arguing for two hours, and the fire had died to embers without anyone noticing.

"We shall be a laughingstock," said Mr. Hadley, for the fourth time. He had arranged before him on the mahogany table a series of newspaper clippings, foreign and domestic, which he periodically rearranged as though constructing a winning hand at whist. "Absolutely a laughingstock. The Berlin expedition departs in March. *March*, gentlemen."

"You have mentioned this," said Professor Ainsworth, who had closed his eyes some time ago and had not reopened them.

"I mention it because it bears mentioning. The Prussians mean to establish a breeding program. A *breeding program*. While we sit here debating."

The committee room of the Zoological Society occupied a cramped corner of the administrative building, its windows giving onto Regent's Park, where the last grey light of a November afternoon was surrendering to fog. The fog had crept into the room itself, or so it seemed; the gas lamps produced more shadow than illumination, and the portraits of former Society presidents gazed down from the walls with expressions of accumulating disappointment.

Mr. Cornelius Blackwood, who had been taking minutes in increasingly illegible shorthand, paused to massage the cramp from his writing hand. At thirty-four, he was the youngest man present by some two decades, and had spoken precisely once, to confirm that yes, the tea service had been arranged, and no, there would not be sandwiches.

"The breeding question is immaterial," said Sir Edmund Foyle. He occupied the chairman's seat with the settled permanence of a man who had occupied many chairman's seats and saw no reason this should ever change. "We are a scientific institution. We collect. We preserve. We document. We do not operate a menagerie for the amusement of the public."

"The Zoological Gardens," Professor Ainsworth observed, without opening his eyes, "are in fact precisely a menagerie for the amusement of the public."

"The Gardens are an *educational enterprise*."

"Then educationally amused. The distinction is noted."

Mr. Hadley swept up his clippings and deposited them in a new configuration. "The French," he announced, "are sending two ships. I have it on good authority. Two ships, sixteen naturalists, and a veterinary surgeon who trained under Bouley himself."

"The French," said Sir Edmund, "may do as they like. The question before this committee is not what the French are doing but what *we* ought to do."

"We ought to do it faster, whatever it is."

The door opened, admitting a gust of cold air and Mr. Josiah Pemberton, Secretary to the Acquisitions Committee, who murmured apologies as he took his seat. His collar was wet and his whiskers disordered; he had evidently been caught in some weather between here and wherever he had been, which, from the ink stains on his cuffs, Blackwood surmised was the Society's archive.

"You have the reports?" Sir Edmund asked.

"I have the reports." Pemberton produced from his leather case a sheaf of papers so substantial that their arrival on the table caused the tea things to rattle. "Dobrynin's survey of the Lena River basin, 1869. Karlsson's census of the Yakutsk herds, 1871. And—" He paused for effect, a habit Blackwood found tiresome. "The field notes of Lieutenant Marchetti, Royal Geographical Society, submitted six weeks ago and not yet published anywhere."

This produced the intended stir. Even Professor Ainsworth opened his eyes.

"Marchetti has returned?" Sir Edmund reached for the papers. "I had heard nothing."

"Returned and recuperating, I understand. Frostbite. Lost three toes and a portion of his left ear." Pemberton permitted himself a small, satisfied smile. "But his notes survived intact. The Society has given us a week's exclusive review before they go to the general membership."

"And what does Marchetti report?"

"Three viable herds within two hundred miles of Yakutsk. The largest numbering perhaps forty animals, including—" Another pause. "—seven calves born within the last two seasons."

Mr. Hadley made a sound of vindication. "Breeding. You see? The population is recovering. If we don't act now—"

"The population," Pemberton continued, raising his voice slightly, "is also being hunted. The Yakut have traditionally taken one or two bulls per year. Sustainable, or so Marchetti believes. But he encountered a Cossack trading party offering rifles in exchange for ivory. Modern rifles, gentlemen. The Yakut are not fools. They understand the mathematics."

The room fell silent. Outside, a keeper's whistle sounded, sharp and distant, calling some creature in from the growing dark.

"Then we must move quickly," Sir Edmund said at last. "Not to compete with Berlin or Paris, but because the opportunity may not exist in five years' time. In ten years we may be discussing fossils rather than specimens."

"Specimens." Professor Ainsworth spoke the word as though testing its weight. "We return to the central question. What exactly do we propose to bring back?"

"A breeding pair," said Mr. Hadley immediately. "Male and female, young and healthy. Establish a program here. The Gardens have space, if we clear the eastern enclosure—"

"The eastern enclosure houses the Indian rhinoceros."

"The rhinoceros can be relocated."

"To where? The refreshment pavilion?"

"Gentlemen." Sir Edmund raised a hand. "We are not here to discuss rhinoceros logistics. Professor Ainsworth, you had an alternative proposal?"

Ainsworth straightened in his chair with the careful dignity of a man whose back troubled him in cold weather. "I propose we focus on scientific collection. Skeletal specimens, preserved tissue, detailed anatomical studies. A complete juvenile specimen, if one can be ethically obtained. The Hunterian Museum has offered collaborative support and funding."

"Ethically obtained," Pemberton repeated. "You mean wait for one to die."

"I mean we need not participate in killing one. The Yakut hunt continues regardless of our presence. We might acquire—"

"A carcass." Mr. Hadley's contempt was palpable. "Bones and pickled organs. While Berlin parades a living mammoth through the Tiergarten."

"Berlin may find that living mammoths are rather more difficult to parade than they imagine."

Blackwood, who had resumed his shorthand, found himself writing a note in the margin that was not part of the official minutes: *They speak of the creature as though it were already theirs to dispose of.* He crossed it out immediately, but the thought persisted.

"There is another consideration." The voice belonged to Dr. Harold Coombes, who had been silent so long that Blackwood had half-forgotten his presence. Coombes served on the committee as representative of the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals, a position that seemed designed to ensure his opinions were heard and then systematically disregarded. He was a small man with a neat grey beard and spectacles that magnified his eyes to an unsettling degree.

"Dr. Coombes," Sir Edmund said, in the tone of a man acknowledging an unavoidable unpleasantness.

"The transport alone would constitute considerable suffering. We are discussing an animal of some eight to ten tons, accustomed to temperatures of forty below zero, to be confined in a ship's hold for—what? Three months? Four? Through Baltic waters in spring, the North Sea, the Channel?"

"Arrangements can be made. Refrigerated compartments. The Americans have developed techniques—"

"The Americans have developed techniques for shipping beef carcasses. Not living creatures of significant intelligence and social complexity."

"Social complexity." Mr. Hadley laughed without humor. "It's an animal, Dr. Coombes. A very large animal. We are not proposing to kidnap a Russian nobleman."

"The evidence suggests," Coombes continued, as though Hadley had not spoken, "that these creatures live in family groups, that they mourn their dead, that they demonstrate behaviors we might reasonably describe as grief, affection, and play. Marchetti's own notes observe a female attending a dying calf for three days, refusing food, producing vocalizations of apparent distress. We propose to tear a young animal from such a group and transport it alone across half the world."

"For science," said Sir Edmund.

"Science has many methods. Not all of them require cruelty."

The fire, unattended, had gone entirely cold. Blackwood noticed that his fingers had grown stiff around his pen. The fog beyond the windows had thickened to the point where the park had vanished entirely; they might have been suspended in grey nothing, these eight men in their wood-paneled room, debating the fate of creatures most of them had never seen.

"The permits," Pemberton said, breaking the silence. "We have not discussed the permits."

Sir Edmund nodded, seeming grateful for the change of subject. "The Tsar's government has grown... cautious regarding foreign expeditions. The Alaska sale has made them wary of appearing to give away Russian natural resources."

"They sold Alaska willingly enough."

"And have regretted it loudly ever since. Our embassy in St. Petersburg advises that any permit application will require considerable diplomatic preparation. Personal assurances at the highest levels. Gifts, possibly." Pemberton consulted his notes. "They suggest beginning with the Governor-General of Eastern Siberia. A man named Sinelnikov. By all accounts both corrupt and corruptible, which is convenient."

"And the Yakut themselves?" Dr. Coombes asked. "Have we considered that these animals might hold significance for them beyond the commercial?"

"The Yakut are subjects of the Russian Empire. Their considerations are subsumed by imperial permits."

"How efficient."

Mr. Hadley gathered his clippings with an air of finality. "We are going in circles. I move that we authorize preliminary planning for a collection expedition, departing no later than autumn of next year, with the specific objectives to be determined by a technical sub-committee. The hour is late. I should like to go home."

"Seconded," said Professor Ainsworth, who had closed his eyes again.

Sir Edmund looked around the table. "Discussion?"

"The cost," Pemberton said. "We have not discussed the cost. I have prepared estimates." He produced another sheaf of papers, thinner this time but no less daunting. "Ship charter, equipment, personnel, permits, bribes—I list them separately, as they constitute a significant line item—supplies, native guides, veterinary support if we pursue live capture, preservation materials if we do not. The minimum figure, assuming everything proceeds without complication, which nothing ever does, is eleven thousand pounds."

The number settled over the room like a physical weight.

"Eleven thousand," Sir Edmund repeated.

"Minimum. More realistically, fifteen. If we encounter serious difficulties, twenty or more."

"The Society's discretionary fund contains—"

"Three thousand, four hundred pounds. Approximately."

Mr. Hadley, who had been halfway to his feet, sat down again. "Then we require patrons."

"We require patrons," Pemberton agreed. "Wealthy, enthusiastic, and preferably not too interested in the details of how their money is spent."

"I may know someone," Sir Edmund said slowly. "Lord Ashworth has expressed interest in the Society's work. His collection at Hartfield is considerable, if somewhat eccentric. He might be persuaded to see this as an opportunity for... legacy."

"Ashworth." Professor Ainsworth's eyes opened once more. "The man who tried to purchase a polar bear for his garden party?"

"The same."

"And was subsequently bitten."

"He has recovered. And his enthusiasm, I am told, remains undimmed."

Dr. Coombes removed his spectacles and polished them with a handkerchief, a gesture Blackwood recognized as preparatory to saying something he knew would be unwelcome. "If we are to proceed—and I recognize that my objections carry limited weight in this company—I would ask that the committee formally consider the establishment of ethical guidelines for the expedition. Standards for capture, transport, and care that prioritize the welfare of the animals involved."

"Guidelines," Mr. Hadley said.

"Written standards. With consequences for violation."

"You wish us to court-martial a mammoth hunter for excessive roughness?"

"I wish us to behave as though we were civilized men engaged in scientific inquiry, rather than trophy hunters with institutional funding."

The silence that followed was of a different quality than before—uncomfortable, charged with something that might have been shame or might have been merely irritation at having one's motives questioned.

"Guidelines," Sir Edmund said at last, "can be discussed. I will not have it said that the Zoological Society of London conducted itself without proper consideration for... for the subjects of its research."

"Then I will draft a proposal."

"Do so. We will review it at the next meeting." Sir Edmund consulted his pocket watch, a gesture that signaled the end of all further discussion. "I believe we have a motion on the table. Preliminary authorization, technical sub-committee, objectives to be determined. All in favor?"

The hands rose, some eagerly, some reluctantly, Dr. Coombes's not at all.

"Opposed?"

Coombes raised his hand, alone.

"The motion carries. Mr. Blackwood, please ensure the minutes reflect the vote and the various considerations discussed. Gentlemen, I thank you for your time."

Chairs scraped. Coats were gathered. Pemberton collected his papers with the efficiency of long practice. Blackwood remained seated, completing his notes, watching the others file toward the door.

"Mr. Blackwood." Sir Edmund had paused, his hand on the door frame. "A word, if you please."

Blackwood set down his pen. "Sir?"

"You've been very quiet this evening."

"My role is to record, Sir Edmund. Not to contribute."

"Indeed. And yet I wonder." The chairman's eyes, pale blue and rather cold, studied him with an attention Blackwood found uncomfortable. "You've read the Marchetti notes? Pemberton shared them with you?"

"He did, sir. For context."

"And what did you make of them? Off the record."

Blackwood considered the question, and the man asking it, and the empty room with its dead fire and its portraits of dead men. "I thought," he said carefully, "that Lieutenant Marchetti seemed to have been affected by what he saw. His language becomes less scientific toward the end. More... personal."

"Personal."

"He describes watching a herd cross a frozen river at dawn. The light on their breath. The sound they made—he compares it to singing, though he acknowledges the comparison is fanciful. He writes that he stood watching for nearly an hour and forgot to take measurements or notes. He writes that he understood, in that moment, why the Yakut consider them sacred."

Sir Edmund was silent for a long moment. "Marchetti has always been given to romanticism. It's a weakness in a field naturalist."

"Perhaps, sir."

"Science requires objectivity. Detachment. We cannot preserve what we do not collect, and we cannot collect if we allow ourselves to be paralyzed by sentiment."

"No, sir."

Sir Edmund nodded, as though Blackwood had agreed with something rather than merely acknowledged it. "Good evening, Mr. Blackwood. Please have the minutes on my desk by Thursday."

He left. The door closed. Blackwood sat alone in the darkened room, his notes before him, the cold seeping into his bones.

In the margin of the final page, where no one would read it, he wrote: *The singing. Someone should have asked about the singing.*

Then he gathered his papers and went out into the fog.

---

*The morning post, two days later, would bring a telegram from Irkutsk. The Society's advance scout, a young man named Foley, had made contact with a Yakut hunting party willing to serve as guides. He had also witnessed something he struggled to describe—a gathering of herds, dozens of animals, perhaps hundreds, converging on a valley whose location the Yakut refused to disclose.*

*The gathering happened every seven years, they told him. It was not for outsiders. The mammoths came to mourn their dead.*

*Foley had tried to follow. He had been turned back, politely but firmly, and warned not to try again.*

*His telegram ended with a question that the Sub-Committee for Vertebrate Acquisition would debate for the better part of the following spring:*

*PLEASE ADVISE: DO WE RESPECT THEIR SACRED PLACES, OR DO WE NOT?*
