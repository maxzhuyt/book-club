# The Weight of Paper

The dispatch had traveled four hundred li to reach Chen Weiyan's desk, and it smelled of river water and horse sweat and something else—smoke, perhaps, or the particular mustiness of a city that had been closed against the world for five years.

He did not open it immediately.

Autumn light fell through the latticed windows of his study in the Bureau of Military Affairs, casting diamond patterns across the lacquered desk, across the bronze paperweight shaped like a tortoise, across his hands which he noticed were not quite steady. Outside, Lin'an continued its business: the calls of vendors in the canal streets below, the distant bell of a temple marking the hour, a woman's laughter from somewhere in the residence compound. The capital had learned to conduct its affairs while Xiangyang died slowly in the north. One could not suspend all pleasure, all commerce, all life for five years. People adjusted. They had to.

Chen Weiyan was sixty-three years old. He had served four emperors and buried two wives and sent three sons to provincial postings far from the intrigues of court. He had learned that paper carried weight far beyond its physical substance. A single sheet could end a career, a family, a dynasty.

This particular bundle—wrapped in oiled silk, sealed with the garrison commander's chop—could end his ability to sleep through the night, one way or another.

He had prepared himself for the fall. They all had, in the Bureau, though no one spoke of it directly. For three years the memoranda had grown increasingly grim: supply boats burned on the Han River, relief forces turned back, the Mongol siege lines tightening like a slow fist. Chen Weiyan had written eleven reports to the Emperor detailing the strategic consequences of Xiangyang's loss. He had drafted contingency plans for the defense of the Yangtze crossings. He had, in his private moments, begun to wonder whether his grandchildren would grow up speaking the language of the steppes.

Now his hands hesitated over the oiled silk because he had learned something else in forty years of imperial service: hope was more dangerous than despair. Despair was stable. One could build on despair, plan around it, accommodate its weight. Hope was unstable. Hope could crack a man open when it proved unfounded.

The seal broke with a small sound, like a bone settling.

*To the Bureau of Military Affairs, from Garrison Commander Lü Wenhuan, written on the nineteenth day of the eighth month in the ninth year of Xianchun:*

*The barbarian forces have withdrawn from their siege positions. I am ordered to report that Xiangyang stands.*

Chen Weiyan read the line three times. The characters remained the same. He became aware that he had stopped breathing and forced himself to draw air, slowly, as his physician had taught him after the palpitations began last spring.

*Xiangyang stands.*

He set the first page aside and continued reading.

The withdrawal had begun twelve days ago, Lü Wenhuan reported. The great trebuchets—those terrible machines that had reduced the walls to rubble and broken the spirit of the defenders more than any cavalry charge—had been dismantled and carried north on the backs of ten thousand laborers. The siege lines, maintained for five years with a patience that spoke of absolute confidence, had emptied overnight like water draining from a basin. Mongol outriders still patrolled the northern approaches, but the army—that vast instrument of conquest that had swallowed kingdoms from the western sea to the forests of Goryeo—had turned away.

*We do not yet understand the cause of this reversal,* Lü Wenhuan wrote, and Chen Weiyan could hear the exhaustion beneath the formal phrasing, the careful bewilderment of a man who had expected to die and now had to contemplate living. *Intelligence from our remaining agents suggests a dispute among the barbarian princes concerning succession. The Great Khan is said to be ill. Other reports speak of rebellion in the western territories requiring redeployment of forces. We cannot confirm these accounts.*

*Our casualties over the duration of the siege: fourteen thousand military dead, an estimated twenty-six thousand civilian deaths from starvation and disease. Current garrison strength: eight thousand combat-effective soldiers. Rice stores sufficient for forty days. The walls require extensive repair before winter. I am requesting immediate resupply and reinforcement.*

*I am also requesting permission to execute seventeen individuals accused of collaboration with the enemy during the siege. Evidence has been gathered. Names attached.*

Chen Weiyan turned to the attached list. His eye caught the third name, and he stopped reading.

Zhou Bingwen. Deputy Commissioner of Granaries, Xiangyang.

He sat back in his chair. The autumn light had shifted, the diamond patterns now climbing the far wall. The temple bell rang again—how long had he been reading? His tea had gone cold in its cup, a skin forming on the surface.

Zhou Bingwen. Chen Weiyan had sponsored his appointment six years ago, had written the memorial praising his efficiency and integrity, had dined with him twice during inspections of the northern defenses. A capable man. A man with a wife and four children who had presumably shared the starvation of the siege while their father managed whatever rice remained.

Collaboration could mean many things. It could mean smuggling information to the enemy, negotiating secret surrenders, opening gates in the night. It could also mean trading food for survival, speaking with Mongol officers under flag of truce, making the thousand small compromises that allowed a man to keep his family alive while the world collapsed around him. Chen Weiyan had never been besieged. He did not know what he would have done, faced with five years of slowly dwindling hope and the screaming of hungry children.

The evidence would be in a separate dispatch, if Lü Wenhuan followed procedure. Chen Weiyan would have to read it. He would have to make a recommendation. The Emperor would want to celebrate this miracle—the siege broken, the dynasty preserved, heaven's mandate affirmed—and executions made for poor celebrations. But Lü Wenhuan had held Xiangyang for five years through some alchemy of will and logistics and luck, and he would need to be honored, his recommendations supported, his authority maintained for the rebuilding to come.

Seventeen names. Seventeen families who would beg, would petition, would call upon whatever connections they possessed in Lin'an. Some of those petitions would reach Chen Weiyan's desk. He would have to weigh evidence, consider politics, calculate the costs of mercy against the costs of severity.

This, too, was victory. This was what Xiangyang's survival meant: not an ending but a continuation, not resolution but further complication. The Mongols would return—everyone in the Bureau understood this. The succession dispute or rebellion or illness that had drawn them away was temporary. Kublai Khan had invested too much in this campaign to abandon it permanently. He would stabilize his northern territories, settle his family's quarrels, and turn south again with that terrible patience that had already consumed half the world.

They had been given time. A year, perhaps two, perhaps five if heaven continued its improbable favor. Time to repair the walls of Xiangyang, to rebuild the garrison, to strengthen the river defenses. Time to train soldiers and stockpile rice and forge weapons. Time, also, to indulge in court factionalism, to squander resources on palace construction and religious ceremonies, to assume that one miracle guaranteed another.

Chen Weiyan had served four emperors. He knew which path was more likely.

He set the dispatches aside and reached for fresh paper, for his inkstone, for the brush that had written so many memoranda recommending things that were never done. The Bureau would need to meet before the evening gong. The Emperor would need to be informed—carefully, in a manner that allowed for appropriate celebration while emphasizing the need for continued vigilance. The Grand Councilor would need to be consulted, and the Grand Councilor's rivals would need to be managed, and the seventeen names would need to be considered, and somewhere in the cascade of necessary actions the actual work of defending the dynasty would need to find a place.

He began to write, his brush moving in the familiar patterns of formal address, and outside his window Lin'an continued its business, unaware that it had been granted a reprieve whose duration no one could measure.

The afternoon light faded. Servants lit lamps in the corridor. Chen Weiyan wrote on, pausing occasionally to consult the dispatches, to check a figure, to consider a phrase. When he finished, his back ached and his eyes burned and the temple bells had rung four more times.

He read over what he had written. It was competent work, clear and properly organized, making the necessary recommendations in the necessary order. It would move through the bureaucracy and generate responses and those responses would generate further actions and somewhere at the end of that long chain, walls might actually be repaired and soldiers might actually be trained and rice might actually be stockpiled.

Or the chain would break, as it so often did, snagged on some courtier's pride or some faction's scheming or simple institutional inertia, and the time they had been given would slip away unmarked.

Chen Weiyan sealed his memorandum and summoned a clerk to carry it to the Grand Councilor's office. Then he sat alone in his study, watching the lamp flame shiver in some imperceptible draft, and allowed himself to feel—just for a moment, before duty resumed its claims—the terrible weight of hope.

*Xiangyang stands.*

For now. For this autumn evening in Lin'an, with the canal water catching lamplight below his window and the dynasty's future balanced on events no one in this city could control.

He thought of Zhou Bingwen, who had managed the granaries, who had perhaps done desperate things to survive. He thought of Lü Wenhuan, who had held a city for five years and now wanted blood to mark the cost. He thought of the Mongol princes quarreling in the north, and the Great Khan whose illness might or might not be real, and the vast machinery of conquest that had paused but not stopped.

He was sixty-three years old. He might live to see the next siege, or he might die peacefully in this city before the Mongols returned. He found, somewhat to his surprise, that he hoped for the former. He wanted to know how the story ended, even if the ending was the one he had prepared himself for, the one that had seemed inevitable for so long.

But that was for later. For now, there was work to be done, and the work would not wait for an old man's reflections on mortality and history and the strange gifts of heaven.

Chen Weiyan rose from his desk, his joints protesting, and went to inform his colleagues that Xiangyang stood, and that everything had changed, and that nothing had changed at all.
