# The Weight of Olives

The seal was Cordoban, unmistakably so. Khalid ibn Musa held it at arm's length where the light from the high window could catch the impression properly. The wax had traveled four months to reach him and had softened somewhere along the journey—Ifriqiya, probably, where the coastal road bent close enough to the sea that salt air crept into everything. But the impression remained legible: the provincial diwan's geometric knotwork surrounding the governor's personal mark, a stylized pomegranate that the al-Rashid family had used for six generations now.

He set the seal aside and unrolled the first of the twelve scrolls that comprised the annual return from al-Andalus.

The treasury hall was quiet at this hour. Most of the other kuttab preferred to work after the zuhr prayer when the building filled with purposeful noise, but Khalid had long ago claimed the early morning for the western provinces. Something about matching his hours to theirs, he told himself, though he had never seen al-Andalus and likely never would. A man might serve the same territory for thirty years from behind a desk in Baghdad and know it only through ink and numbers. His father had done exactly this with Egypt. His grandfather with Khorasan. The empire was too vast for any single pair of eyes, so it was absorbed instead through ten thousand functionaries, each tending their particular fragment of the whole.

Olive oil. The first scroll concerned olive oil.

Khalid pulled a fresh sheet toward him and began to compile, his reed pen moving in the automatic rhythms of long practice. The groves of Ishbiliya had produced at expected levels—98,000 qintars assessed, of which the caliphal fifth came to 19,600 qintars. Transport costs deducted according to the established schedule. Spoilage allowance at three percent, which was generous but necessary for goods traveling such distances. Net contribution to the central treasury: 14,200 qintars in oil or specie equivalent at western market rates.

He paused at that last notation. Western market rates. The governor's diwan had included a separate sheet—unusual—explaining that local prices had fluctuated due to a surplus in the Maghreb, and that they had calculated specie equivalent using the five-year rolling average rather than the current depressed figure.

This was technically permissible. The relevant precedent dated to the reign of al-Mu'tasim, when a similar situation with Egyptian grain had created chaos in the treasury's accounts. The Caliph's solution had been elegant: provinces could use averaged rates when current prices deviated more than fifteen percent from historical norms, provided they documented the deviation and its cause.

But Khalid had processed the Andalusian returns for eleven years now, and the governor's diwan had never once invoked this provision. They were fastidious about using current rates, even when it disadvantaged them. He remembered a year—perhaps six years past—when they had actually overpaid because Andalusian prices happened to be high while eastern markets slumped.

He made a small mark in the margin. Not a rejection, not even a query. Simply a mark indicating that he had noticed, that the irregularity had been seen by human eyes in Baghdad. The governor's diwan would never know he had made it. But the mark would remain in the treasury's copy, and if some future pattern emerged—three years hence, five years, ten—someone would be able to trace it backward to this moment.

The second scroll concerned textiles. The third, minerals and metals. The fourth—

A knock at the doorframe. Khalid looked up to find a young man in the plain robes of a petition-carrier standing at the entrance to his alcove.

"Forgive the interruption, sayyid. There is a man asking for the official responsible for al-Andalus."

"A petitioner?"

"He says he is a merchant. Recently arrived from Qurtuba."

Khalid set down his pen. Merchants did occasionally appear at the treasury, usually with complaints about customs assessments or questions about the licensing requirements for various trades. But they typically worked through intermediaries. A man who had traveled from Cordoba to Baghdad and then sought out the specific functionary handling his home province—that suggested something more than a tariff dispute.

"His name?"

"Sulayman ibn Khalaf, sayyid. He says it concerns an estate matter. An inheritance that crosses provincial boundaries."

Inheritance. The word sat awkwardly against the morning's earlier calculations. Oil and textiles could be reduced to qintars and dinars, but inheritance meant families, meant property that someone had worked and someone else now claimed, meant the particular grief of death transmuted into legal argument.

"Tell him I will see him after I complete the current assessment. One hour, perhaps two."

The petition-carrier bowed and withdrew.

Khalid turned back to the fourth scroll. Agricultural levies from the northern districts, the lands that bordered the Christian kingdoms of the mountains. The figures here were more complex—different crops, different assessment schedules, adjustments for the military obligations that frontier populations bore in lieu of certain taxes.

He had seen maps of this region, though maps were expensive and the treasury's collection modest. The caliphate stretched from the Atlantic coast to the Hindu Kush, and its northern edge was irregular, shaped by geography and by the limits of what any army could hold. In al-Andalus, those limits meant the Pyrenees and the Cantabrian mountains, where Christian principalities maintained a precarious independence in the high valleys. They had been there for centuries now. Every few generations, some ambitious governor proposed a campaign to finally absorb them, and every few generations, the central administration explained that the cost would exceed the revenue for approximately four hundred years.

This was how empire actually functioned, Khalid had come to understand. Not through glory or conquest but through calculation. The seal on these scrolls bore the Caliph's name and invoked his divine authority, but the documents themselves concerned drainage assessments and livestock counts and the specific number of bolts of silk that had moved through customs at Malaga in the month of Shawwal.

He worked through the remaining scrolls. Mining output. Port revenues. The special levy on non-Muslims, which in al-Andalus meant primarily Christians and the small Jewish communities that had lived there since Roman times. The figures were unremarkable—slightly elevated, if anything, suggesting the western economy remained healthy despite whatever price fluctuations had prompted the earlier notation.

When he finished, he reviewed his compilation. The province of al-Andalus had contributed approximately 2.3 million dinars to the central treasury this year, plus goods-in-kind valued at another 400,000. This was consistent with recent years. The empire would continue.

He thought of the merchant waiting in the outer hall, Sulayman ibn Khalaf of Cordoba, who had traveled four months over roads that the caliphate maintained, through territories where his safe-conduct bore the same seals that marked these tax returns, all to resolve some family matter of property and inheritance. The man had probably given little thought to the vast machinery that made his journey possible. Few people did. They walked roads and assumed roads would exist. They traded goods across two thousand miles and assumed the currency would be honored, the contracts enforced, the bandits suppressed.

Khalid gathered the scrolls and secured them with a fresh cord. The compilation he would send to the chief treasurer's office, where it would be combined with returns from thirty other provinces and presented to the vizier and ultimately summarized for the Caliph himself. His Excellency the Commander of the Faithful would glance at a single sheet of figures over breakfast, perhaps, and understand that his realm remained solvent for another year.

The merchant would have to wait a little longer. There was one more matter to address—the question of the averaged olive oil prices, which Khalid had marked but not resolved. He could approve it and move forward. He could query it and delay the province's accounts by several months while letters traveled back and forth across the Mediterranean. He could flag it for review at a higher level, passing the decision to someone else's desk.

What he could not do was ignore it. That was the nature of bureaucracy, the quiet tyranny of the noticed irregularity. Once seen, it had to be addressed.

He pulled out a fresh sheet and began to compose a query. Polite, procedural, utterly routine. The governor's diwan would receive it in four months and respond with perfect documentation justifying their calculation. The response would reach Baghdad in another four months. By then, it would be time to process next year's returns, and the whole cycle would begin again.

The empire endured through such cycles. It always had. It always would.

He signed the query, applied his seal, and set it aside for the courier. Then he rose, straightened his robes, and went to hear what Sulayman ibn Khalaf of Cordoba had traveled so far to say.
