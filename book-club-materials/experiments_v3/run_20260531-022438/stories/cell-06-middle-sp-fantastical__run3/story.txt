# The Weight of Ink

The afternoon had turned the color of weak tea by the time Reverend Josiah Alcott noticed he had written the same sentence three times. He stared at the page, at the identical phrases stacked like cordwood, and carefully drew a single line through two of them.

Across the study, Dr. Emil Warehouse shifted in the leather chair that had been Josiah's father's and his grandfather's before that. The theologian from Hartford had arrived two hours ago with a traveling case full of notebooks and a manner of aggressive patience that Josiah recognized from his years at seminary. The man wanted something. They always did now.

"You've gone quiet," Warehouse observed. He had a way of holding his pen suspended over paper, ready to transcribe, that made Josiah feel like a specimen.

"Sermons require quiet."

"Of course. Forgive me." But Warehouse did not look down at his own notes. "I only wonder—do you find the process changed? The composition itself?"

Josiah set his pen in its rest. The nib was splitting; he'd need to trim it soon. Such a small thing, the maintenance of tools. He had not thought about pen nibs at all during the eleven minutes he was dead. He had not thought about anything in the way he understood thinking now.

"I find," Josiah said carefully, "that I am more deliberate."

"Deliberate." Warehouse wrote something. "Meaning you weigh your words more heavily?"

"Meaning I am slower."

The theologian smiled at this, a tight expression that suggested he was cataloging the response for later analysis. Emil Warehouse had published seven articles in the past year touching on Josiah's testimony. Josiah had read four of them. The man wrote well—clearly, precisely—and seemed genuinely torn between fascination and professional obligation to skepticism. This made him more dangerous than the outright debunkers, who could be dismissed, or the true believers, who wanted nothing from him but confirmation.

Warehouse wanted *understanding*. He wanted to take Josiah apart like a clock and see what made him tick differently now.

"May I ask what passage you're treating? For Sunday?"

Josiah glanced at his page. "Lazarus."

"Ah." Warehouse leaned forward slightly. "Bold."

"Is it?"

"You've avoided it. For thirteen months, if my research is accurate, you have not preached on Lazarus, nor on the raising of Jairus's daughter, nor on any of the resurrection accounts in the Gospels. You have preached on charity, on faithfulness, on—" he consulted his notes, "—the lilies of the field. Twice."

"My congregation has many anxieties about material provision."

"No doubt. But they come to hear *you*, Reverend Alcott. They travel considerable distance. Last month a family came from Pennsylvania."

"I know." The Hendersons. The father had waited after the service, hat in hands, and asked if Josiah could tell him whether his son—killed at Antietam six years prior—was at peace. Josiah had said what he could say, which was not enough and was also too much. The man had wept. His wife had wept. Josiah had gone home and not eaten dinner and sat in this room until midnight, unable to pray, unable to do anything but remember the quality of light in a place that had no light source.

He picked up his pen again. The sermon would not write itself. Lazarus, come forth.

But which Lazarus? The one wrapped in grave-clothes, stumbling into the Judean sun? Or the one Our Lord called by name—that specific summons cutting through whatever membrane separates this world from the next?

Josiah had not heard his name. He had heard nothing like language. And yet he had understood that he was seen, known, called back by something that required his presence elsewhere. Here. In this study. With this pen.

The late afternoon light shifted, falling through the window at an angle that caught the dust motes suspended in the air, and for a moment Josiah could not breathe. The light. It was nothing like the light he remembered, and the difference was unbearable, like hearing a child attempt a melody one has heard performed by masters. He gripped the edge of the desk.

"Reverend Alcott?"

The moment passed. The light was simply light. The dust settled.

"I'm well." He was not well. He had not been well in thirteen months. He had been functional, upright, capable of performing the duties of his office, but *well* was a country he had emigrated from and could not return to.

Warehouse was watching him with naked interest. "Does that happen often? These... episodes?"

"I would not call them episodes."

"What would you call them?"

Josiah considered the question seriously. The theologian deserved that much. "Comparisons."

"Between?"

"Between what I see now and what I saw then."

Warehouse wrote rapidly. "And in these comparisons, this world—our world—falls short?"

"That is not what I said."

"But it is what you meant."

Josiah returned to his page. The sentence about Lazarus sat there, singular now, adequate but flat. *Our Lord called Lazarus forth from the tomb, and Lazarus came.* Accurate. Theologically sound. Empty.

He had told the physicians what he remembered. He had told Dr. Warehouse's colleagues, the newspaper men, his own bishop. He had told them about the sense of presence, the overwhelming rightness, the knowledge—not belief, *knowledge*—that he was exactly where he was meant to be. He had told them about the grief of return, which he had not expected and could not adequately explain. How does one grieve a homecoming?

What he had not told them, what he had not told anyone, was that he was no longer certain his sermons mattered. Not because the afterlife made morality irrelevant—if anything, his experience had convinced him that how one lived carried weight, substance, consequence. But because the words he could assemble from the twenty-six letters of the English alphabet were so pathetically inadequate to the task of describing what awaited.

And yet he wrote. Every Saturday, he sat at this desk. Every Sunday, he stood in his pulpit. His congregation looked up at him with their desperate hopeful faces and he gave them what he could.

"May I ask you something directly?" Warehouse said.

"You have been asking me things directly for two hours."

"Something more direct, then." The theologian set down his pen. "In your testimony to Dr. Marsh and Dr. Bellingham—the attending physicians—you described a sense of being... expected. Welcomed. You used the word 'recognized.'"

"Yes."

"Recognized by what?"

Josiah had answered this question before, in various forms. He had said *God* and *the divine presence* and *something beyond my capacity to name*. All true. All insufficient.

"By everything," he said. "By the... fabric of what is. As though the universe itself knew my name. Knows all names. Has always known them."

Warehouse was very still. "That is not orthodox."

"No."

"The Church teaches a personal God. A deity with will, intention, capacity for relationship. What you describe sounds more like..." He searched for the word. "Pantheism. Or something stranger."

"I am telling you what I experienced, Dr. Warehouse. I am not constructing a systematic theology." Josiah heard the edge in his own voice and softened it. "I remain a Christian. I believe Our Lord lived, died, and rose again. I believe the scriptures. But I also know what I know, and I cannot unknow it."

"Even if what you know contradicts what you believe?"

This was the question. The question beneath all the other questions. Josiah had been circling it for thirteen months, approaching and retreating, unable to resolve the tension.

"I do not think it contradicts," he said slowly. "I think it... exceeds. The map is not wrong. But the territory is larger than the map suggested."

Warehouse wrote nothing. He simply looked at Josiah with an expression that might have been sympathy or might have been professional assessment. After a moment, he said, "What will you tell them? On Sunday. About Lazarus."

Josiah looked at his page. The inadequate sentence. The splitting nib. The weak tea light.

He wrote: *Lazarus came forth, but the scriptures do not record what he saw in those four days, nor what it cost him to leave it.*

Then he crossed it out.

"I don't know yet," he said.
