# The Mammoth Question

## Chapter One

The fog had not lifted from Savile Row by half past two, and so the lamps were lit in the map room of the Royal Geographical Society, casting their yellow judgment upon the seven gentlemen who had arranged themselves around the long table. Above them, mounted upon the eastern wall, the skull of a Columbian mammoth regarded the proceedings through empty orbits the size of dinner plates. It had been a gift from the American delegation in 1856, and its curved tusks, each twelve feet long, framed the doorway through which Mr. Edgar Vance now entered, late and wet and carrying a leather satchel dark with rain.

"Gentlemen," said Sir Humphrey Catton from the head of the table. He did not look up from his papers. "Now that Mr. Vance has seen fit to join us, we may proceed."

Vance took his seat beside Dr. Morris Campion, the Society's chief naturalist, whose white whiskers and red-veined nose gave him the appearance of a walrus who had taken up academic pursuits. Campion was examining a map of eastern Siberia that lay unrolled across the mahogany, its corners pinned by brass weights cast in the shape of elephants—a gift from the Viceroy, and quietly resented by those who found the comparison to the Indian species taxonomically imprecise.

"The matter before us," Sir Humphrey continued, "is the proposed expedition to the Kolyma River basin, for the purpose of collection and study of *Mammuthus primigenius* in its native habitat. Dr. Campion, you will summarize the scientific objectives."

Campion cleared his throat with a sound like gravel shifting. "The British Museum's collection of mammoth specimens is, at present, adequate but not comprehensive. We possess three mounted skeletons of varying completeness, numerous tusks and teeth, and perhaps two dozen study skins of inconsistent quality. What we lack—what science lacks—is a complete adult male in prime condition, preserved by modern methods and accompanied by detailed anatomical notes taken in the field."

"The Russians have two such specimens," observed Mr. Ashworth, the youngest member present. He was twenty-six, recently returned from a botanical survey of the Caucasus, and had not yet learned which observations were useful and which were merely inflammatory.

"The Russians," said Sir Humphrey, "have a great many things. That is not the point."

"Is it not?" Ashworth leaned forward. "I only mean to suggest that if comprehensive study is our aim, cooperation with the Imperial Academy at St. Petersburg might prove more efficient than mounting an independent expedition across six thousand miles of increasingly hostile territory."

The silence that followed was of a particular quality known only to committee rooms, in which men who cannot openly express contempt must find other means of conveying it. Dr. Campion's means was to remove his spectacles and polish them slowly. Mr. Vance's was to open his satchel and busy himself with its contents. Sir Humphrey's was to smile.

"Mr. Ashworth raises an interesting notion," Sir Humphrey said. "Perhaps he would also like to suggest that we seek German cooperation on the Tanganyikan surveys, or invite the French to assist in our work in the Punjab."

"That is not what I—"

"The purpose of this expedition," Sir Humphrey continued, "is not merely to add specimens to the British Museum, though that is of course desirable. The purpose is to demonstrate that British natural philosophy remains preeminent in a field that grows more crowded by the year. Last spring the Prussians sent a party to the Lena delta. They returned with two juvenile females, a quantity of hair and tissue samples, and—most gallingly—a living calf."

This last word produced a stir. A living calf was a different matter entirely. Living specimens required transport, feeding, housing, veterinary attention; they were expensive, troublesome, and politically complicated. They were also, undeniably, spectacular.

"The calf survived the journey?" asked Mr. Pemberton, a corpulent gentleman whose interest in megafauna was primarily commercial. His family held investments in the ivory trade, and he attended these meetings with the close attention of a man calculating futures.

"It did. It is now installed at the Zoological Garden in Berlin, where I am told it has become the primary attraction. The Kaiser visited last month."

Pemberton nodded slowly. "And how old is this calf?"

"Approximately fourteen months. A female."

"Then she will not achieve full growth for another fifteen years at minimum. Twenty, if she follows the Indian pattern." Pemberton's tone was carefully neutral. "A considerable investment of resources for a single specimen."

"The scientific value—" Campion began.

"The *symbolic* value," Sir Humphrey interrupted, "is incalculable. Berlin now possesses something London does not. A living representative of the Pleistocene, breathing and feeding and voiding its bowels on Prussian soil. Do you imagine the public does not notice? Do you imagine the Foreign Office does not?"

"The Foreign Office," said a new voice, "notices a great deal."

The speaker was Mr. Lysander Hough, who had thus far contributed nothing to the discussion beyond his presence, which was contribution enough. Hough was attached to the Foreign Office in some capacity that no one could precisely define and everyone understood to be significant. He was a thin man of perhaps fifty, with the complexion of a consumptive and the eyes of a much healthier predator.

"The Foreign Office," Hough continued, "has received certain communications from our ambassador in St. Petersburg. It seems the Tsar's government has grown protective of its eastern megafauna. There is talk of restricting foreign access to known habitats. Talk, I should add, that has intensified since the German expedition's success."

"Restricting access how?" Sir Humphrey asked.

"Permits. Quotas. Requirements that collected specimens be shared with Russian institutions. The usual mechanisms of bureaucratic obstruction." Hough examined his fingernails. "Nothing has been formalized. Nothing may be. But if this expedition is to proceed, it would be wise to proceed soon."

The map of Siberia seemed to grow larger in the lamplight, its vast pale spaces suddenly crowded with invisible barriers. Vance, who had been organizing papers from his satchel, now spoke for the first time.

"There is another consideration." He drew out a letter, much folded and bearing foreign postage. "I received this yesterday from Nils Dorofeyev. Some of you will know him—a fur trader based in Yakutsk, who has assisted previous Society expeditions. He writes that the eastern herds have moved."

"Moved?" Campion frowned. "They migrate seasonally, of course—"

"Not seasonally. Permanently, or so Dorofeyev believes. The herds that formerly ranged along the Kolyma and Indigirka tributaries have withdrawn northward, beyond the usual summer pastures, into terrain considerably more difficult to access. He attributes this to hunting pressure—by which he means not only the pellet rifles of the Yakut and Chukchi, but the organized ivory harvesting operations that have expanded significantly in recent years."

Pemberton shifted in his chair.

"Dorofeyev estimates," Vance continued, consulting the letter, "that the population of the eastern Siberian mammoth has declined by one-third since 1850. He writes—I translate loosely—that his grandfather saw herds that darkened the tundra, moving like slow rivers of flesh, and that his own children may see the last of them."

A longer silence followed this. The skull on the wall offered no comment.

"Fur traders," Pemberton said finally, "are not naturalists. They are prone to exaggeration, particularly when it serves to increase the value of their goods."

"Dorofeyev has no ivory to sell us," Vance replied. "He wrote, I believe, because he is troubled."

"Troubled." Pemberton's tone suggested this was an unserious word for unserious men.

"Perhaps," said Ashworth, "we should be troubled as well."

Sir Humphrey raised a hand before Pemberton could respond. "Mr. Ashworth, your youth is showing. The mammoth has survived the predation of men for ten thousand years—considerably longer if one credits the more speculative geological chronologies. It survived the primitive hunters of the Neolithic with their flint-tipped spears. It survived the horsemen of the steppes. It survived the expansion of Russian settlement across Siberia. It will survive the current commercial interest in its tusks and hide."

"Will it?" Ashworth did not look away from Sir Humphrey's gaze. "The American bison numbered in the millions within living memory. I am told that railway passengers now shoot them from carriage windows for sport, leaving the carcasses to rot where they fall. The great auk is gone entirely. The quagga will follow within the decade. Why should the mammoth prove more resilient than these?"

"Because," said Dr. Campion slowly, "the mammoth occupies territory so remote, so climatically hostile, that no commercial enterprise could pursue it to extinction. The economics would not support it. Long before the last animal fell, the cost of hunting would exceed the value of the product."

"A comforting theory," Ashworth said. "I wonder if the mammoth finds it equally reassuring."

"Gentlemen." Hough's voice cut through the rising tension like a surgeon's knife through inflamed tissue. "This is all very philosophically stimulating. But the question before this committee is not whether the mammoth will persist into the next century. The question is whether British science will document it comprehensively while documentation remains possible—or whether we will cede that honor to Berlin, to St. Petersburg, to whatever American institution eventually bestirs itself to compete."

"Precisely," said Sir Humphrey. "Thank you, Mr. Hough. Now. Let us consider the practical requirements."

For the next hour, the committee discussed routes, provisions, personnel, and equipment. The preferred approach was by sea to Vladivostok, then overland via established trading posts to the collection area—a journey of some four months in favorable conditions. The party would require sledges, draught horses, a substantial quantity of salt and arsenical soap for preservation, specialized tools for the skinning and disarticulation of animals weighing upwards of six tons, and the services of native guides whose cooperation could not be assumed.

"The Yakut will work for us if we pay fairly," Vance said. "The Chukchi are more difficult. They consider the mammoth sacred—or something like sacred. I am told they will not assist in killing, though they may be persuaded to help with transport of already-dead specimens."

"Superstition," Pemberton muttered.

"Belief," Vance corrected. "Which amounts to the same thing for our purposes, but which we would do well to respect if we wish to return with our specimens and our lives."

The estimate of costs grew steadily as the afternoon wore on. By four o'clock, the figure had reached twelve thousand pounds—a sum that produced visible discomfort in several faces around the table.

"The Society's discretionary funds will not support such an outlay," Sir Humphrey admitted. "We will require subscriptions from private donors and institutional partners. The British Museum has indicated willingness to contribute, contingent upon their receiving the primary skeletal specimen and any cranial material of significant scientific interest."

"And the living specimens?" Campion asked. "If we are successful in obtaining a calf, as the Germans did?"

"The Zoological Society of London has expressed interest. They would assume responsibility for transport from the nearest suitable port—Vladivostok or Yokohama—and for subsequent housing and care."

"At what cost?"

"That remains to be negotiated. But their interest is genuine. A living mammoth in Regent's Park would be a considerable attraction. Considerable enough, I should think, to justify considerable expense."

Ashworth had been silent for some time, his attention apparently fixed on the map before him. Now he traced a line with his finger from the Kolyma delta southward to the Sea of Okhotsk.

"If we take a calf," he said, "we must first separate it from its mother."

No one responded.

"Mammoth cows are protective. The literature on Indian elephants suggests that the maternal bond is exceptionally strong, and that cows will defend their young against predators at considerable risk to themselves. I expect the same is true of the northern species." Ashworth looked up. "To obtain a living calf, we will almost certainly have to kill its mother. Possibly other members of the herd as well, if they interfere."

"That is," Campion said carefully, "a reasonable inference."

"So we propose to reduce a declining population further, in order to bring a single specimen to London, where it will live out its days in a paddock designed for Indian elephants and African plains animals. Where it will never see tundra, never migrate, never encounter another of its kind except in the form of mounted bones and stuffed hides. This is our contribution to science?"

"Our contribution to science," Sir Humphrey said, his patience audibly thinning, "is the accumulation and preservation of knowledge. The mammoth exists now. It may not exist in a hundred years—you yourself have raised that possibility. Would you prefer that future generations have nothing to study but bones and secondhand accounts? Or would you prefer that they have detailed anatomical surveys, preserved tissue, a complete record of behavior and physiology observed under controlled conditions?"

"I would prefer," Ashworth said, "that they have living mammoths."

"Then you should take up a different vocation. Conservation is not the purpose of this Society. Natural philosophy is."

"Is there a difference?"

"Yes." Sir Humphrey's voice had hardened. "The difference is that natural philosophy produces knowledge, while conservation produces sentiment. One advances civilization. The other merely impedes it."

The lamps flickered. Outside, the fog pressed against the windows like something alive and curious.

"I think," said Hough mildly, "that we have reached the point of diminishing returns in this particular discussion. Shall we call the question?"

Sir Humphrey nodded. "All in favor of authorizing the Siberian expedition, subject to successful subscription and the approval of our institutional partners, signify by raising your hand."

Five hands rose: Sir Humphrey's, Campion's, Pemberton's, Hough's, and—after a moment's hesitation—Vance's.

"Opposed?"

Ashworth's hand rose alone.

"Abstaining?"

The seventh member, an elderly baronet named Chalcroft who had slept through most of the proceedings, blinked and raised a tentative palm.

"The motion carries. Dr. Campion will prepare a detailed prospectus for potential subscribers. Mr. Vance will write to his contact in Yakutsk requesting current intelligence on herd locations and native disposition. Mr. Hough will keep us informed of diplomatic developments. We reconvene in a fortnight." Sir Humphrey gathered his papers. "Thank you, gentlemen."

Chairs scraped. Voices murmured. The committee dispersed into the fog-bound afternoon, leaving behind the smell of lamp oil and damp wool and something older—the faint, persistent odor that emanated from the mammoth skull whenever the weather turned wet, a smell like ancient ice and cold earth and the memory of distances no railway could ever cross.

Ashworth was the last to leave. He stood for a moment beneath the skull, looking up into those hollow eyes, those orbits that had once contained organs capable of seeing across miles of frozen steppe, of recognizing kin, of watching the sun set over a world that had no names for itself.

"I'm sorry," he said quietly. "I don't expect it helps."

The skull said nothing. The skull had been saying nothing for sixteen years, and would say nothing for centuries to come, long after the question of living mammoths had resolved itself one way or another, long after the men who had debated their fate were themselves bones and memory and dust.

Ashworth turned up his collar and walked out into the fog.
