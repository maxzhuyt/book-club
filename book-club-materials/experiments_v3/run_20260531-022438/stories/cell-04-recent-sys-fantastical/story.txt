# Third Shift

The chill off the inspection floor cut through Margaret's coat despite the blast furnaces roaring two buildings over. She pulled her clipboard closer and began at the first pallet, same as always.

"Batch 7-41-N," she read aloud, though no one was listening. The night shift had clocked out twenty minutes ago, leaving behind their work like an examination paper waiting to be marked.

She lifted an artillery shell from the crate, turning it beneath the caged bulb overhead. The metallurgy looked sound—no pitting, no visible impurities clouding the brass. She ran her caliper along the casing and noted the measurement. Within tolerance. Greaves was adequate at the lathe work, she would give him that much.

Margaret rotated the shell to examine the inscription band. Here the night shift's real work declared itself. The engraved characters spiraled around the circumference in tight procession, each sigil no wider than a match head. She retrieved her loupe and bent close.

The Kindling sequence was cleanly cut. The Binding sequence, acceptable. She followed the spiral with practiced patience until she reached the Unmaking sequence and stopped.

"Damn," she muttered.

The third sigil in the sequence—the one the training manuals called the Mouth, though Margaret had learned it as something older and less pleasant—had been cut with a tremor. The vertical stroke wandered slightly left at its terminus. To an untrained eye, nothing. To a German soldier trusting his cover while this shell sailed overhead and failed to detonate with the proper sympathetic resonance, everything.

She set the shell aside and checked the next. Same flaw. The next, same again.

Margaret worked through the entire pallet, sixteen shells in all. Eleven showed the identical error on the Mouth, all consistent with a single engraver whose hand had grown unsteady. Fatigue, most likely. The night shift ran eleven hours now that the Coventry works had been bombed out of production.

She looked at the eleven shells lined up on her inspection table like unexploded accusations.

Rejecting them was correct procedure. These would be pulled, melted down, recast, re-engraved. Two days of labor from a dozen workers negated. Quotas missed. Questions from the Ministry about why Birmingham's output had dropped again. Explanations required in triplicate.

Margaret thought of the official language she would use. *Deviation in Unmaking sequence, third sigil, consistent with engraver fatigue.* She thought of Davies, the night shift supervisor, who would receive the report. He had three children and a wife in hospital with her nerves. The engraver responsible—she checked the batch manifest and found the stamp belonging to a woman named Colquitt—would face a formal inquiry.

The safe choice was rejection. Nobody had ever been disciplined for being too cautious with ritual munitions. The stories from the last war had made certain of that. Margaret had heard about the Somme shells that detonated in their own guns, the ## at Verdun that had stayed inert until French soldiers retrieved them as souvenirs and brought them home. War Office pamphlets still circulated those photographs as a warning.

But she had also seen the updated casualty projections from North Africa. She had heard the whispers about what the Germans were fielding now, the things their own program had produced in the decades since magic had crept back into the world through whatever crack the Great War had opened.

Eleven shells. Eleven flawed Mouths. Eleven small chances that the resonance would scatter instead of focus, that the Unmaking would unmake improperly or not at all.

Margaret picked up one of the rejected shells and examined the sigil again. The deviation was perhaps a tenth of a millimeter. Perhaps less.

She thought about what a tenth of a millimeter meant when you multiplied it by distance and velocity and the strange mathematics of ritual sympathy. She thought about the technically permissible range that the Ministry guidelines allowed for minor deviation. She thought about the difference between the letter of her training and its spirit.

The floor was silent except for the distant furnace roar and the building's settling groans. Night inspection was solitary work. That was by design. No distractions, the regulations said. Clear judgment.

Margaret looked at the eleven shells for a long moment.

Then she pulled a fresh form from her clipboard and began to write.

---

She was still writing when she heard footsteps on the iron stairs leading down to the inspection floor. The clock on the wall read quarter past four. No one should be in the building at this hour except security, and security didn't wear hard-soled shoes that rang with that particular administrative cadence.

Margaret kept her eyes on her form as the footsteps approached. Only when they stopped directly beside her table did she look up.

The man wore a Ministry coat over a suit that had been good quality before the war and merely adequate since. He was perhaps fifty, with the kind of face that suggested he had spent decades learning how to make it unreadable. He looked at the eleven shells arranged on her table, then at her clipboard, then at her.

"Mrs. Linwood," he said. It was not a question.

"Inspector Linwood," she corrected.

"Quite." He picked up one of the rejected shells and examined it with an expertise that surprised her. His eyes went directly to the flawed Mouth. "Third sigil. Engraver tremor."

"Yes."

"Eleven of sixteen in the batch."

"Yes."

He set the shell down with care that spoke of long experience handling objects whose power lay in their markings rather than their material. "I've read your file, Inspector. Seventeen months on third shift inspection. Not a single batch passed that later showed ritual failure in the field."

Margaret waited.

"Also not a single batch rejected that, upon secondary review, was found to actually require rejection."

"Quality and efficiency both," Margaret said carefully. "As the Ministry requires."

"Indeed." The man reached into his coat and produced a photograph. He laid it on the table beside the shells.

Margaret looked at it. A city street, Continental by the architecture. The buildings on both sides had been—she searched for the right word and found nothing adequate. They had been *negated*. Not bombed, not burned. Simply made absent, as if something had eaten the concept of their existence and left only ash-gray impressions where structure had been.

"Stalingrad," the man said. "Last week. German ritual artillery. We don't know the sigil sequences they're using yet, but our people estimate the yield at approximately six times what our current shells produce."

He let that sit for a moment.

"We need more shells, Inspector. And we need them faster. And we need them to work." He gestured at the eleven rejects. "What were you going to recommend?"

Margaret looked at her half-finished form. She had been writing the rejection order, same as always. Correct procedure. Safe choice.

But she had not yet signed it.

"I was still deciding," she said.

The man smiled, and Margaret understood that this had been a test and that she had passed it in some fashion she did not yet fully understand.

"Good," he said. "I was hoping you would say that." He produced a second document from his coat and laid it beside the photograph. "There's an opening in the Ministry's new quality assessment division. Field work. Reviewing production at facilities across the country and making... judgment calls."

Margaret read the document's heading. It named a department she had never heard of and carried a classification stamp she had only seen twice before.

"The pay is unchanged," the man continued. "The hours are worse. The decisions are harder." He looked at the eleven shells again. "But someone with your record of knowing precisely where the line is—and precisely how close one can get to it without crossing—would be valuable."

Margaret thought about the flawed Mouths. She thought about the Stalingrad photograph. She thought about Davies and Colquitt and the quotas and the casualty projections and the tenth of a millimeter that might mean nothing or might mean everything.

"The eleven shells," she said. "What would you do with them?"

The man's expression didn't change. "What would you recommend?"

Margaret looked at them for a long moment. Then she picked up her pen and finished the rejection order, signing her name at the bottom with the careful clarity the Ministry required.

"Reject them," she said. "Properly flawed is still flawed. But I would also recommend a reduced engraver shift length and a secondary review of the Mouth specifications. The tolerances may be tighter than they need to be."

The man took the rejection order and read it. His unreadable face showed something that might have been approval.

"When can you start?" he asked.

Margaret looked at the clock. Quarter to five. First shift would arrive in two hours. She had three more pallets to inspect, a report to file, and a whole set of certainties she had carried into this building that no longer seemed quite as solid as they had been.

"I haven't finished my inspection," she said.

"No," the man agreed. "You haven't."

He left the photograph and the classified document on her table and walked back toward the iron stairs. At the bottom he paused and looked back.

"Finish your shift, Inspector. Then go home and sleep. Then decide." His shoes rang on the first step. "But I think you already know what you're going to choose."

Margaret listened to his footsteps recede until the building fell silent again. She looked at the photograph of the negated street. She looked at the eleven rejected shells with their flawed Mouths, their trembling Unmakings.

Then she set them aside, pulled the next pallet forward, and continued her inspection. There was still work to do, and the night was not yet over, and somewhere out in the dark the war was waiting to see what she would build for it.
