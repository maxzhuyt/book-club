# The Last Bridge

The rain fell on Dejima as it had fallen for three hundred years, dripping from the same curved eaves onto the same flagstones worn smooth by generations of Dutch boots. Willem van der Berg stood at his window watching it pool in the familiar depressions, thinking that a man could measure his life by such stones. His father had walked them. His grandfather too. The van der Bergs had been Dejima men since the VOC days, back when the Company meant something, before it collapsed and left only this—a fan-shaped island smaller than an Amsterdam city block, connected to Nagasaki by a single guarded bridge.

Twenty-six years he had lived here. He knew every warped beam, every rat hole, every guard who watched the bridge with eyes that had learned patience across centuries of watching.

Today they had summoned him.

Willem turned from the window and examined himself in the small mirror he'd inherited from his father. His coat was twenty years out of fashion by European standards, but it remained the finest wool on Dejima. He straightened his cravat. The face looking back at him had gone grey at the temples, the skin weathered by decades of salt air and confinement. He looked, he thought, like a man who had been pickled.

The knock came precisely at the fourth hour. He opened the door to find two guards flanking a young interpreter named Ogawa, who bowed with mechanical precision.

"Van der Berg-san. The honorable Inspector Matsudaira will receive you."

Willem nodded and followed them across the bridge. He had crossed it perhaps five thousand times in his life, always with guards, always with permission documented in triplicate. The bridge was forty paces long. He had counted. A man learned to count things when his world could be measured in forty paces.

The rain intensified as they entered the inspection hall, a building Willem knew as well as his own warehouse. The same tatami smell, the same smoke-darkened beams, the same low table where generations of his family had knelt and negotiated and occasionally begged. He lowered himself onto the cushion provided—thinner than the one offered to Japanese merchants, he had noticed long ago—and waited.

Inspector Matsudaira entered without ceremony, attended by two scribes who settled behind him like nesting birds. He was perhaps fifty, his face carrying the particular stillness Willem had learned to associate with men who had survived shogunate politics. His topknot was immaculate. His robes were the color of wet slate.

"Van der Berg-san." Matsudaira settled onto his cushion with fluid grace. "You requested this meeting."

"I am grateful for the Inspector's time." Willem kept his Japanese formal, careful. Three decades and he still felt the language like a garment that did not quite fit.

"You wish to discuss the matter of the machinery."

"Yes."

The scribes' brushes whispered against paper. Willem had learned long ago that everything said in this room became permanent, became part of archives that stretched back centuries, an unbroken record of every transaction, every conversation, every foreign word spoken on Japanese soil.

"The machinery," Matsudaira repeated. "Explain again what this machinery does."

Willem had explained it four times already, to four different officials. He suspected they compared his explanations afterward, searching for inconsistencies.

"It sends words across great distances using lightning. Captured lightning, conducted through wires of copper. A message sent from Nagasaki could reach Edo in minutes rather than days."

"Lightning." Matsudaira's expression revealed nothing. "The Dutch have captured lightning."

"Not the Dutch alone. The English. The Americans. The French. All Western nations now use this technology. They call it telegraph."

"And the Dutch East India Company wishes to sell us this captured lightning."

"The Company no longer exists, Inspector. As I have explained. I represent the trading house of van der Berg and Sons, operating under license from the Kingdom of the Netherlands."

"Yes. Your Company died." Matsudaira paused. "Yet you remain."

"Dejima remains. Trade remains. The relationship between our nations remains."

"The relationship." Matsudaira lifted a small ceramic cup and examined it as though searching for flaws. "Tell me, van der Berg-san. This telegraph. It exists in your homeland?"

"It exists across all of Europe. Across America. Lines connect London to Calcutta. Messages cross the Atlantic Ocean floor."

"The ocean floor."

"Yes."

The scribes wrote. Rain drummed against the roof tiles. Willem felt the familiar sensation of speaking into a void, of offering information that would be weighed and measured and filed away in archives he would never see.

"And ships," Matsudaira said. "Your ships have also changed."

"Steam power. Yes. Ships no longer depend on wind. They cross oceans on schedules, arriving within days of their predicted—"

"We have seen smoke on the horizon. Black smoke from ships that move against the wind." Matsudaira set down his cup. "Twenty-six years ago, American ships came to Uraga. Black ships belching smoke. They demanded we open our ports."

"I am aware of that incident, Inspector."

"The Shogun refused. The Americans threatened fire and war. The Shogun refused again. The Americans departed." Matsudaira's voice remained level. "They said they would return with a greater fleet. They have not returned."

"No."

"Why?"

Willem hesitated. This was the question beneath all questions, the gap in his knowledge that had troubled him for years. The American expedition had failed—that much was known. Commodore Perry had returned home in disgrace. But why the Western powers had not simply sent larger fleets, had not forced the issue as they had forced China to its knees, Willem could not say with certainty. He had theories. The American civil war had consumed that nation's attention. European powers had been occupied with colonial struggles elsewhere. Japan was far, and Japan had made clear it would fight rather than submit.

But these were guesses. The truth was that he knew less about the outside world with each passing year. Ships from Batavia brought goods and fragmentary news, but Dejima had become an eddy in the stream of history, and Willem van der Berg had become a man preserved in amber, increasingly uncertain what century he belonged to.

"The Western nations have other concerns," he said finally. "Wars among themselves. Territories in Africa and Asia that occupy their attention. Japan is distant. Japan has shown it will resist."

"Japan has shown it will remain Japan." Matsudaira's tone carried a weight that Willem recognized as dangerous. "For three hundred years, the Tokugawa have maintained order. Peace. Stability. Your ancestors came here seeking profit. Our ancestors permitted them to stay, within limits. The limits have held."

"They have."

"And now you offer us captured lightning. Steam engines. The tools of nations that tried to force our doors open at cannon-point."

"I offer trade, Inspector. Only trade. As my family has always offered."

Matsudaira studied him for a long moment. The scribes had stopped writing, their brushes suspended.

"There is a faction in Edo," Matsudaira said quietly, "that believes we must acquire Western weapons. Study Western sciences. Transform ourselves to resist Western aggression."

Willem said nothing.

"There is another faction that believes such transformation would destroy what it seeks to preserve. That Japan cannot adopt the tools of barbarians without becoming barbarian itself."

"And the Inspector? What does he believe?"

It was too direct a question. Willem knew it as soon as he spoke. But he was tired, tired of circling, tired of decades of careful indirection that had led nowhere.

Matsudaira's expression flickered—surprise, perhaps, or something colder.

"I believe," he said slowly, "that a man who has lived on Dejima for twenty-six years should understand patience."

"I understand patience, Inspector. I also understand that my wife is buried on this island because she was not permitted to leave it. I understand that my son lives in Batavia because he chose a larger world, and I have not seen his face in eleven years. I understand patience very well."

The silence that followed was absolute. Even the rain seemed to pause.

"What do you want, van der Berg-san?" Matsudaira's voice had changed, dropping the formal register. "Truly. Beneath the telegraphs and the steam engines. What do you want?"

Willem closed his eyes. The question was impossible. He wanted to walk more than forty paces without permission. He wanted to see his son. He wanted to know what the world had become while he sat in his warehouse cataloging copper and camphor. He wanted to matter.

"I want to send a message," he said. "To my son. If you permitted a telegraph line from Dejima to the Dutch station in Shanghai, I could speak to him. Words across the ocean. Before I die, I would like to speak to my son."

Matsudaira was quiet for a long time.

"One line," he said finally. "From Dejima to Shanghai."

"Yes."

"Operated by Dutch hands. Observed by Japanese eyes. Every message recorded and translated."

"I would expect nothing less."

"And in exchange?"

Willem opened his eyes. "In exchange, I will teach your engineers how it works. Every component. Every principle. The captured lightning will belong to Japan."

Matsudaira rose. The meeting, apparently, was over. Willem struggled to his feet, his knees aching from the tatami.

"I will present your proposal to the appropriate authorities." Matsudaira moved toward the door, then paused. "Van der Berg-san. You have lived here longer than most Japanese officials remain in their posts. You have seen inspectors come and go. Policies shift and shift again."

"I have."

"Then you understand that what is forbidden today may be permitted tomorrow. And what is permitted today..." He let the sentence hang unfinished.

"May be forbidden tomorrow. Yes. I understand."

"The bridge will remain, van der Berg-san. For now, the bridge remains."

The guards escorted Willem back across the forty paces of worn wood. The rain had stopped, and weak sunlight was breaking through the clouds, illuminating the puddles on Dejima's flagstones. He stood in the doorway of his warehouse and watched a Dutch ship riding at anchor in the harbor, flying colors he barely recognized anymore.

Somewhere beyond the horizon, steam engines were reshaping the world. Telegraphs were annihilating distance. Empires were being born and dying. And here on his island, Willem van der Berg waited for permission to speak to his son, as his father had waited, as his grandfather had waited, as the stones beneath his feet had waited for three hundred years.

The bridge remained.

For now, the bridge remained.
