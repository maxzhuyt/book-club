# The Matter of the Ligurian Eagle

The Senate house stank of sweat and olives. Marcus Scribonius had been speaking for nearly an hour about grain shipments to Hispania, and Gaius Cassius Nerva felt his left leg going numb beneath him. He shifted on the bench, drawing a sharp look from old Publius Cotta, who believed suffering built character and had once delivered a four-hour speech on harbor tariffs while passing a kidney stone.

"—and furthermore, the merchants of Ostia have presented documentation suggesting—"

"The chair recognizes that Senator Scribonius has exhausted his allotted time," interrupted the presiding consul, Quintus Caecilius Metellus Celer, with evident relief. "We shall table the Hispanian matter until the next session."

A murmur of gratitude rippled through the chamber. Nerva straightened, preparing to rise, when the great bronze doors groaned open.

Every head turned.

A figure in white robes edged with saffron stood at the threshold. Nerva recognized him immediately: Lucius Manlius Torquatus, one of the quindecimviri sacris faciundis, keeper of the Sibylline Books. The priest's face was pale as temple marble.

"Honored senators," Torquatus said, and his voice cracked on the second word. He cleared his throat. "Honored senators, I bring instruction from Delphi."

The chamber went silent. Not the polite quiet of attention, but the absolute stillness of held breath. Nerva watched Metellus Celer grip the arms of the curule chair.

"The oracle has spoken," Torquatus continued. He produced a scroll from his robes with trembling hands. "Three days past, the Pythia entered her trance before witnesses including two Roman observers of equestrian rank. She spoke in the voice of Apollo himself."

"Read it," someone called from the back benches.

Torquatus unrolled the scroll. His eyes moved across the text once, twice, as though confirming what he already knew. Then he looked up.

"The golden eagle must not cross the Ligurian water. Let Rome remember Drusus."

The silence shattered into chaos.

Nerva closed his eyes. The Ligurian matter. Of course. For three months the Senate had debated whether to send legions across the gulf to crush the coastal tribes raiding Roman shipping. Pompeius Magnus had been quietly lobbying for the command. Crassus had been quietly lobbying against him. Caesar had been quietly watching both.

And now Apollo had spoken.

"Order!" Metellus Celer was shouting. "Order, or I will clear this chamber!"

The noise subsided reluctantly. Senator Gaius Aurelius Cotta—no relation to Publius, and possessed of a far more flexible spine—was already on his feet.

"The chair recognizes Senator Aurelius Cotta."

"Brothers," Cotta said, spreading his hands in a gesture of measured reason, "let us not be hasty. The Pythia's words require interpretation. She speaks of the golden eagle—but which eagle? Rome has many legions, many standards. Perhaps Apollo means only that a specific legion should remain behind while others proceed."

"The golden eagle is Rome itself," snapped a voice from across the chamber. Nerva turned to see Marcus Porcius Cato standing rigid as a pillar. "Every schoolchild knows this. Apollo commands that Rome must not send military force across the Ligurian gulf. The instruction is clear as spring water."

"With respect to my distinguished colleague," Cotta replied smoothly, "clarity is rarely the province of divine speech. The gods communicate in layers. Consider: the Pythia also says 'let Rome remember Drusus.' Which Drusus? The great general who died in Germania? His son? His grandson? Each interpretation suggests different—"

"Drusus Libo." The voice belonged to Publius Servilius Vatia Isauricus, ancient and wizened, who had held every major office the Republic could offer. He spoke rarely now, which meant men listened when he did. "She means Drusus Libo. I was seventeen when he sailed against the Ligurians in the consulship of Lepidus and Catulus. I watched his fleet leave Ostia."

The old man paused. His eyes seemed focused on something beyond the chamber walls.

"Forty-three ships. He had forty-three ships. The weather was clear. The auguries were favorable. Every priest in Rome had blessed the venture." Servilius drew a rattling breath. "They found wreckage for six months. Bodies washed up in Sardinia. In Corsica. In places that had no names. Three hundred sailors survived of six thousand."

"A storm," someone murmured.

Servilius turned his ancient gaze toward the voice. "No storm. The survivors spoke of calm seas. They spoke of something rising from beneath the water. Something with many arms. Something that sang."

Nerva felt cold despite the press of bodies.

"A Titan," Cato said quietly. "The Ligurian waters belong to one of the old powers. Everyone knows this."

"Everyone suspects this," Cotta corrected. "There has been no confirmation, no direct divine communication indicating—"

"What do you imagine we are discussing at this very moment?" Cato's voice rose. "Apollo himself, speaking through his ordained vessel, warns us against this action. What further confirmation do you require? Must Poseidon himself appear in this chamber and drown the opposition benches before you acknowledge divine will?"

Scattered laughter, quickly suppressed.

Nerva found himself studying the faces around him. Pompeius Magnus sat in careful stillness, his expression betraying nothing, though his political position had just been demolished. Crassus appeared equally blank, though a hint of satisfaction lurked at the corners of his mouth. And Caesar—

Caesar was watching Torquatus.

The priest still stood at the chamber's entrance, scroll clutched to his chest. He looked like a man who had expected to deliver a message and leave, not to stand witness to the machinery of Roman politics grinding against divine mandate.

"The chair recognizes Senator Julius Caesar."

Nerva straightened. The gallery grew quiet.

Caesar rose with his customary unhurried grace. "Honored colleagues, I find myself in rare agreement with both the distinguished Cato and the distinguished Aurelius Cotta. A remarkable position, and I suspect it will not last beyond this speech."

Light laughter. Caesar smiled.

"The oracle's words are clear, as Cato says. Rome must not send military force across the Ligurian water. But Cotta is also correct that divine speech operates in layers. Consider what Apollo does not say. He does not forbid Romans from entering Ligurian territory. He forbids the eagle—the military standard, the organized legion. What then of other approaches?"

"Explain yourself," Metellus Celer said.

"Gladly. The Ligurian pirates harry our shipping because they have no reason to stop. We have offered them no alternative. But what if Rome were to send not soldiers but merchants? Diplomats? Priests of Apollo himself, bearing gifts and offers of alliance?" Caesar's voice carried without apparent effort, filling the chamber. "Let us transform enemies into clients, as our ancestors did with such success throughout Italia. The eagle need not cross the water if Roman influence crosses instead."

Nerva watched the senators absorbing this. It was clever. Perhaps too clever.

"You propose to negotiate with sea-raiders," Cato said flatly.

"I propose to obey Apollo while solving our problem. Unless you believe the god would object to Rome extending its hand in peace rather than war?"

That was the trap, and Cato saw it clearly. His jaw tightened. Opposing Caesar's proposal meant implying that Apollo wanted the Ligurians left entirely alone—an interpretation the oracle's words did not support.

"The god's words say nothing about diplomacy or merchants," Cato replied carefully. "I will concede that they do not explicitly forbid such approaches. But neither do they endorse them. We must be cautious of constructing permissions from silence."

"Then let us consult the quindecimviri," Caesar said. "Let them examine the Sibylline Books for guidance. If the books counsel against diplomatic engagement, I will withdraw my proposal. If they are silent or favorable, we proceed."

All eyes turned to Torquatus, who had gone even paler.

"The books," the priest said slowly, "are not to be consulted lightly. Each consultation requires preparation, purification, sacrifice—"

"Then prepare," Caesar said. "Purify. Sacrifice. Rome will wait."

The vote came at dusk. The Senate authorized a diplomatic mission to Liguria, with military action explicitly suspended pending further divine consultation. Pompeius Magnus voted in favor, his face revealing nothing. Crassus abstained, citing uncertainty. Cato voted against, recorded his formal dissent, and swept out of the chamber before the session formally closed.

Nerva lingered as the other senators filed out, watching slaves light the oil lamps against the gathering darkness. He found himself standing near the chamber's small shrine to Jupiter, where a flame burned eternally, tended by priests who worked in shifts throughout the day and night.

"You look troubled, Senator."

He turned. Caesar stood a few feet away, still wearing his senatorial toga, showing no signs of fatigue despite the long session.

"A successful day for you," Nerva said.

"A successful day for Rome, I would prefer to say."

"And if the Sibylline Books counsel against your diplomatic mission?"

Caesar's smile did not reach his eyes. "Then I shall bow to divine will, of course. As any pious Roman would."

He walked away, his footsteps echoing in the emptying chamber. Nerva stood alone before the eternal flame, watching it flicker and dance.

Somewhere across the sea, in waters Rome's legions would not cross, something old and vast waited in the darkness below the waves. The god at Delphi had spoken a warning. Rome had listened—after a fashion.

Nerva wondered if that would be enough.

He thought of the sailors who had washed up on distant shores, bodies broken and waterlogged, and he thought of the thing that sang beneath calm seas. He thought of how Servilius Vatia's voice had trembled, and how even now, forty years later, the old man could not speak of what the survivors had described without his hands beginning to shake.

The flame burned on.

Rome had remembered Drusus, in the end. Rome had listened to Apollo's warning.

But Rome had also found a way around it.

Nerva pulled his toga tighter against a chill that had nothing to do with the evening air, and walked out into a city that had learned long ago to negotiate with gods the same way it negotiated with foreign kings: carefully, respectfully, and with one hand always reaching for advantage.

The stars were coming out overhead. Somewhere among them, the gods watched.

Nerva hoped they were satisfied.

He suspected they were not.
