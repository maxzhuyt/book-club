# The Morning Edition

The church bells started at midnight and hadn't stopped. From the fifth floor of the Herald building, Gerald Wise could see the crowds on Washington Street, the ticker tape already falling from office windows though the war had ended six hours ago and the paper still hadn't gone to press.

"We need a decision." Art Callahan stood in the doorway of the conference room, his sleeves rolled past his elbows, a proof sheet in his hand. "Composing's been holding for forty minutes."

Gerald didn't turn from the window. Below, a woman in a red dress was kissing a stranger. Somewhere a trumpet was playing, badly.

"Gerry."

"I heard you."

The conference table was littered with the debris of argument: coffee cups, ashtrays mounded with cigarette ends, three competing mock-ups of the front page. Franklin Morse sat at the far end, his bow tie loosened for the first time in the eleven years Gerald had known him. Across from him, Nina Cho pressed her fingertips against her temples, her eyes closed.

"Read them again," Gerald said, finally turning.

Art sighed but lifted the first proof. "'VICTORY IN EUROPE: Nazi Germany Surrenders Unconditionally to Allied Forces.' Clean, direct, what every other paper from here to San Francisco is running."

"Next."

"'THEIR VICTORY: Europe Celebrates End of Six-Year War.' Franklin's version."

Franklin straightened. "It's accurate. The British fought. The Soviets fought. The French resistance fought. We sold them trucks."

"We sold them trucks on credit," Nina said without opening her eyes. "Which they'll be paying off for the next fifty years. Don't make us sound generous."

"Next," Gerald said.

Art hesitated. "'THE WAR IS OVER: Now the Questions Begin.' Nina's."

"Questions." Franklin laughed, a dry sound. "What questions? The question was whether fascism would swallow the continent. It didn't. That's the story."

"The story," Nina said, her eyes opening now, "is that we watched. Six years, Franklin. Six years of watching."

"We weren't at war."

"No. We weren't."

Gerald returned to the window. A conga line had formed on the street below, snaking between the stalled cars. He wondered how many of the dancers had sons or brothers who'd gone to Canada to enlist, who'd joined the RAF or the merchant marine before the Neutrality Acts tightened. He wondered how many of those boys were dead.

"The readers want to celebrate," Art said. "I've got Donovan down in the lobby with photographs from London. Churchill on a balcony. The King and Queen waving. People dancing in Piccadilly. Beautiful stuff."

"Then let the photographs do the celebrating." Nina stood, walking to the mock-ups. "The headline can do something else."

"Such as?"

"Tell the truth."

Franklin slapped his palm on the table. "The truth is that this country stayed out of another European slaughter, exactly as the people wanted, exactly as they voted for. Three times they voted for it. Lindbergh, then Taft, then Lindbergh again. That's democracy, Nina. I'm sorry if it offends your sensibilities."

"My sensibilities." She turned to face him. "I got a letter last week from a friend in California. A woman I knew at Radcliffe. Her family relocated to Manzanar in '42 because Lindbergh decided Japanese-Americans were a security risk, even though we weren't at war with Japan either. She's been in a camp for three years, Franklin. Her father died there. So please, tell me more about democracy."

The room went silent. From outside, a cheer rose up, probably another false rumor of Lindbergh's motorcade approaching the Common.

"That's not—" Franklin started.

"Not what? Not relevant? Not fit to print?" Nina's voice had gone quiet, which Gerald knew was more dangerous than shouting. "What exactly are we in the business of, Franklin? What do you think a newspaper is for?"

Art cleared his throat. "We're not going to relitigate five years of editorial policy at eleven-thirty at night."

"Why not? When would be more convenient?"

The phone rang. Art grabbed it, listened, spoke a few words Gerald couldn't hear, then hung up.

"Wire service," he said. "They've got more details coming out of Buchenwald. The camps. The—" He stopped, swallowed. "The numbers are getting worse. They're saying it might be millions."

Gerald closed his eyes. He'd seen the first photographs two weeks ago, smuggled in through London. Skeletal figures in striped uniforms. Mass graves. He'd run them on page six, below the fold, because the managing editor had worried about breakfast tables.

"Run them," Nina said.

"What?"

"The photographs. Tonight. Front page."

Franklin stood so quickly his chair scraped back. "You cannot be serious. This is V-E Day. People want to feel good. They want to believe the right side won."

"Did it?" Nina asked. "Did we?"

"We weren't on a side. That was the whole point."

"Yes." She picked up one of the mock-ups, the clean one, the one that matched every other front page in America. "That's exactly my point."

Gerald walked to the table. He looked at the three versions, each one a different story about who Americans were and what they'd done with the last six years of history. The trumpet outside had stopped. The bells continued.

"What if we're wrong?" Art asked, his voice lower now. "What if people don't want to think about this tonight? What if they just want one day, one single day, to feel like the nightmare is over?"

"The nightmare is over," Gerald said. "For Europe. For the survivors. Whoever's left."

"But not for us?"

Gerald thought about the question. He thought about the letters he'd received over the years, the ones calling him a warmonger for running stories about the London Blitz, the ones calling him a coward for not running more. He thought about his nephew Daniel, nineteen years old, healthy and alive and safe in his dormitory at Amherst because there'd been no draft, no war, no reason for American boys to die on foreign beaches.

He thought about the people in the photographs from Buchenwald and wondered if any of them had relatives in Boston who'd been writing letters to newspapers for years, begging someone to do something, anything at all.

"Run Franklin's headline," he said.

Nina's face went slack.

"Run Franklin's headline," he repeated, "with Nina's photographs. The camps. Front page, above the fold. And below that, the celebrations. London, Paris, the crowds right outside our window."

"That's—" Art frowned. "That's going to be a hell of a jarring layout."

"Yes."

"People won't know how to feel."

"No. They won't."

Franklin sank back into his chair. "The calls we're going to get tomorrow—"

"I'll take them." Gerald picked up the proof with Franklin's headline and handed it to Art. "Make sure the photographs are captioned accurately. Names if we have them. Locations. Numbers. Everything we know."

Art nodded slowly, then headed for the door. He paused at the threshold. "For what it's worth, I don't think there's a right answer here."

"No," Gerald said. "There isn't."

After Art left, the three of them stood in silence, listening to the celebration below. The conga line had dissolved into smaller clusters of dancers. Someone had started singing "God Bless America," and others were joining in, the words drifting up through the open window.

"It's not enough," Nina said quietly. "A few photographs. It doesn't change anything."

"No."

"But it's something."

Gerald didn't answer. He watched the crowds below, the joy and the relief and the strange hollowness underneath, and he wondered what tomorrow's readers would see when they looked at the front page. Victory and horror, side by side. The war that had ended and the questions that were only beginning.

Franklin stood, straightening his bow tie. "I'm going to get a drink. Possibly several." He paused at the door. "For what it's worth, I hope you're right. I hope people want the truth more than they want to feel good." He shook his head. "I just don't believe it."

After he left, Nina moved to stand beside Gerald at the window.

"Do you?" she asked. "Believe it?"

Gerald watched the woman in the red dress, still dancing with her stranger, and thought about all the things Americans had chosen not to see over the last six years. The cables from ambassadors. The refugee ships turned back. The photographs buried on page six because breakfast tables were sacred.

"No," he said. "But I believe it's our job to try."

The bells rang on. The presses, at last, began to roll.
