# The Agrarian Question

Dmitri Alexandrovich Volkonsky dipped his pen and held it suspended above the paper, watching a single drop of ink tremble at the nib before falling back into the well. Outside the frosted windows of his third-floor office, St. Petersburg lay buried under February snow, the Neva frozen solid enough to bear sleighs. Inside, the porcelain stove ticked and settled, radiating insufficient heat.

He was forty-three years old. He had served in the Ministry of Internal Affairs for nineteen years. He had drafted perhaps two thousand memoranda in that time, documents that had traveled upward through the layered machinery of imperial governance like prayers ascending toward an indifferent heaven. Most had vanished without consequence. A few had returned bearing marginalia in various hands, queries and objections and occasionally—rarely—approval.

This one, he suspected, would not vanish.

*On the Question of Extending Redemption Payment Relief to the Black Earth Provinces*, he wrote at the top of the fresh sheet. His handwriting was precise, a schoolmaster's hand, each letter formed with the mechanical consistency that nineteen years of bureaucratic labor had instilled.

He paused. The title itself was a political act. *Relief* suggested that the current payments were a burden. The reactionaries in the State Council would read that single word as an indictment of the original emancipation terms, an admission that the Tsar-Liberator's great reform had been flawed from its inception. And yet *adjustment* was too bloodless, too technical. It failed to capture what Volkonsky had seen with his own eyes during his inspection tour of Voronezh province the previous autumn: the hollowed faces, the children with distended bellies, the peasants selling their allotment shares back to the very landlords from whom they had supposedly been freed.

He let the title stand.

*The undersigned respectfully submits for consideration the following observations regarding the present condition of the formerly enserfed population in the central agricultural provinces, with particular attention to the districts of Voronezh, Tambov, and Kursk.*

The formulaic opening settled his nerves somewhat. There was comfort in bureaucratic language, in its measured rhythms and deliberate evasions. One did not write that peasants were starving. One wrote that *nutritional inadequacies had been observed among certain segments of the rural population*. One did not write that the redemption payments were crushing families into dust. One wrote that *the current amortization schedule may warrant reconsideration in light of prevailing agricultural conditions*.

And yet.

Volkonsky set down his pen and pressed his palms flat against the desk. Through the wall he could hear the muffled voice of his colleague Petrov dictating correspondence to a clerk. The Ministry hummed with this constant low noise of administration, the scratching of pens and shuffling of papers and murmured consultations that somehow, in aggregate, kept the empire functioning. Or gave the appearance of function. Volkonsky was no longer certain which.

Four years ago, everything had nearly ended. He remembered that March Sunday with the vividness of nightmare: the explosion on the Catherine Canal, the Tsar's carriage destroyed, the screaming horses, the blood on the snow. He had been at home when the news came, eating a late breakfast with his wife, and she had dropped her teacup when the servant burst in with the report. The cup had shattered against the parquet floor, and Volkonsky had stared at the spreading pool of tea and thought, with strange clarity, *This is how empires fall. One broken vessel at a time.*

But the Tsar had lived. The second bomb had maimed him terribly—he walked now with a cane, and his left hand was a ruin of scar tissue—but he had lived. And in the aftermath, rather than retreating into reaction as so many had urged, Alexander had pressed forward. The constitutional proposals that had been sitting on his desk that very morning, awaiting his signature when the assassins struck, had been signed from his hospital bed. The Loris-Melikov reforms had proceeded. Censorship had loosened. The zemstvos had been granted expanded authority. And the redemption payments—the forty-nine-year mortgages that the liberated serfs owed for the land they had received—had been reduced by imperial decree, first by ten percent, then by another fifteen.

It was not enough. Volkonsky knew this with the certainty of a man who had walked through villages where the weights and measures had revealed the truth that no official report could capture. The peasants were not lazy, as the landlords claimed. They were not drunk, or improvident, or resistant to modern methods. They were simply paying more for their land than the land could yield. The mathematics were inexorable. A family could not extract from thirty desiatinas of mediocre soil enough grain to feed themselves, pay their taxes, meet their redemption obligations, and still have seed for the following year. Something had to give. Usually it was the children who gave first.

He picked up the pen again.

*It is the considered judgment of this office that the reforms instituted by His Imperial Majesty following the events of March 1881, while representing significant amelioration of the burdens borne by the agricultural population, have not fully addressed the structural inadequacies inherent in the original redemption framework.*

There. He had written it. *Structural inadequacies*. The phrase would be understood. It was as close as a ministry official could come to stating that the great emancipation, the Tsar's crowning achievement, the act that had earned him the title of Liberator, had been built on a foundation of mathematical impossibility.

The door opened without a knock.

"Volkonsky." State Councillor Durnovo stood in the doorway, his broad face flushed from the cold of the corridor. He was a large man, running to fat now in his sixth decade, but there was nothing soft about his small pale eyes. "You're still here."

"As you see, Excellency."

"The Minister wants to know when you'll have the agrarian memorandum ready."

Volkonsky gestured at the papers before him. "I am working on it presently."

Durnovo advanced into the room, leaving the door open behind him. The draft stirred the documents on Volkonsky's desk. "He wants it by Thursday. The Tsar has asked for a briefing on the question before his meeting with the zemstvo delegation."

"Thursday." Volkonsky kept his voice neutral. This was Tuesday. "The matter is complex."

"The matter is simple." Durnovo leaned over the desk, close enough that Volkonsky could smell the tobacco on his breath. "The Tsar wishes to be seen as continuing his program of reform. The Minister wishes to provide him with evidence that reform is proceeding. You will provide that evidence. The question of whether the evidence reflects actual conditions in the provinces is, I assure you, of secondary concern."

Volkonsky looked up at the older man. They had known each other for fifteen years. Durnovo had been his superior when Volkonsky first joined the Ministry, had written the recommendation that secured his current position, had stood as godfather to his youngest daughter. None of this would matter if Volkonsky pushed too hard.

"The Tsar," Volkonsky said carefully, "has on several occasions expressed his desire to receive accurate information regarding the state of his realm. He has said that he would rather hear unpleasant truths than comfortable lies."

"The Tsar has said many things." Durnovo straightened. "He also said, if you will recall, that he would rather give up his throne than betray the principles of the emancipation. This was very noble. It was also very nearly fatal, since it was precisely those principles that inspired young Grinevitsky to throw his bomb." He paused, letting the name settle in the air. Grinevitsky, the assassin who had thrown the second device, the one that had nearly succeeded. "There are those who believe that continued reform will only encourage further violence. The Tsar does not share this view. But the Tsar is not immortal, despite his remarkable survival, and the Tsarevich holds rather different opinions on the matter."

The Tsarevich. Alexander Alexandrovich, the heir apparent, built like a bear and possessed of a bear's temperament. He made no secret of his contempt for his father's reforms, his conviction that liberalization was weakness and weakness invited destruction. When the old Tsar finally died—and he was sixty-seven now, visibly aged by his wounds, increasingly frail—everything would change.

"I understand," Volkonsky said.

"I wonder if you do." Durnovo moved toward the door, then paused. "The memorandum should emphasize the successes of the existing program. It should propose modest extensions rather than fundamental restructuring. It should, above all, avoid any language that might be interpreted as criticism of the original emancipation framework. The Tsar is proud of his legacy. He does not wish to hear that his greatest achievement was built on—what was your phrase? I happened to glance at your draft as I came in."

Volkonsky felt the blood drain from his face. "Structural inadequacies."

"Yes. That." Durnovo smiled thinly. "Find a different phrase, Dmitri Alexandrovich. Or better yet, find a different argument entirely. Thursday."

He left, pulling the door closed behind him.

Volkonsky sat motionless for a long moment. The stove ticked. Outside, a sleigh bell jingled faintly, the sound carrying through the frozen air with crystalline clarity. He looked down at the memorandum, at the words he had written, at the careful evasions and the single moment of near-honesty that had already betrayed him.

He could do as Durnovo suggested. It would be easy enough. He had written such documents before, documents that praised progress where there was stagnation, that found hope where there was only despair, that translated the suffering of millions into the bland language of acceptable administrative discourse. He was skilled at it. It was, in many ways, what he had been trained to do.

Or.

He thought of the village of Novaya Kalitva, in Voronezh province, where he had stopped during his inspection tour. A woman had approached him there, thinking he was someone important, someone who could help. She had held out her hands to show him the calluses, the cracked skin, the nails broken down to the quick. *We work*, she had said. *Tell them in St. Petersburg. We work until we fall down. And still they take everything. Still my children go hungry. Tell them.*

He had told her he would try.

Volkonsky dipped his pen. He drew a line through *structural inadequacies* and wrote, in the margin, *circumstances warranting attention*. It was weaker. It was safer. It would pass Durnovo's scrutiny.

But as he continued drafting, as the afternoon light faded and the clerks in the outer offices began packing up their things and the great machinery of imperial administration wound down toward evening, he found that his pen kept returning to the dangerous territory. Kept inserting, between the acceptable phrases, the shadow of what he had actually seen. A reference to *nutritional deficiencies* here. A mention of *accumulated arrears* there. Small truths, buried in bureaucratic camouflage, but truths nonetheless.

Perhaps they would be noticed. Perhaps, in the right hands, they would matter.

Or perhaps they would simply vanish into the archive, one more memorandum among thousands, while the empire continued its slow grinding progress toward whatever future awaited it. The Tsar would receive his briefing, would make his gestures toward reform, would present his aging face to the zemstvo delegation as evidence that the spirit of liberation still lived. And in the villages of the Black Earth provinces, the mathematics would continue their inexorable work.

Volkonsky wrote until the lamp oil ran low and the stove grew cold. When he finally set down his pen, the memorandum was complete—or as complete as such things ever were. It said less than he knew. It proposed less than was needed. It would satisfy Durnovo and the Minister and perhaps even the Tsar himself, who wanted so badly to believe that his sacrifices had meant something.

But buried in the middle of page four, in a subordinate clause that a careless reader might easily overlook, Volkonsky had written: *The peasant, having received his freedom, now awaits the means to exercise it.*

It was not much. It was almost nothing.

He sealed the memorandum, addressed it, and left it on his desk for the morning courier.

The snow had stopped when he finally emerged from the Ministry. The Winter Palace loomed white against the darkening sky, its windows glowing with lamplight. Somewhere within those walls, the Tsar-Liberator was dining, or resting, or perhaps reading reports of his own—reports that told him his empire was progressing, his reforms were succeeding, his people were grateful.

Volkonsky pulled his coat tight against the cold and began the long walk home.
