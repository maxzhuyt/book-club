# The Weight of Paper

The courtyard maple had begun its slow surrender to autumn, and Minister Chen Yiming watched three leaves descend past his window before he trusted himself to look again at the papers on his desk.

The dispatches had arrived at the Hour of the Snake, carried by a relay rider whose horse had died beneath him at the final post station. Chen had heard this detail from his secretary and found he could not stop thinking about it—the animal's heart giving out within sight of the capital, its legs folding, the rider rolling free with the leather case clutched to his chest. Such details anchored a man when larger truths threatened to sweep him into currents he could not navigate.

He read the first dispatch again. The calligraphy was hurried, the brush strokes of a man writing by inadequate light, but the seal was genuine. Vice Commissioner Zhao's seal, which meant Zhao had survived. Which meant the walls had held.

*The Mongol bombardment ceased on the fourteenth day of the eighth month. Their new weapons, which hurl stones by means of a counterweight mechanism, proved unable to breach the western fortifications despite seventeen days of continuous assault. The garrison's losses are significant but not catastrophic. We await resupply and further instructions.*

Seventeen days. Chen set the paper down and pressed his fingertips against his eyelids until colors bloomed in the darkness. For seventeen days, stones had fallen on Xiangyang while he had attended poetry gatherings and reviewed tax assessments and listened to the Emperor discuss the placement of artificial hills in the new garden extension. He had done these things because they were required of him, and because hope was a commodity the court could not afford to display, and because if he had allowed himself to feel the full weight of those seventeen days he would have been useless to everyone.

The second dispatch was longer, more detailed. It described the Mongol withdrawal—orderly, unhurried, ominous in its lack of desperation. They had not been defeated so much as... delayed. Chen understood the distinction. A man did not serve thirty years in the Bureau of Military Affairs without learning to read the silences between words.

He thought of his cousin's son, Wei Tan, who had been assigned to Xiangyang's garrison three years ago as a junior officer. The boy had written to him twice yearly, formal letters full of filial respect and careful observations about supply logistics and troop morale. Chen had written back with advice he suspected was outdated and encouragements he feared were hollow. Wei Tan's name did not appear in the casualty lists appended to the third dispatch, but the lists were acknowledged to be incomplete. The dead were still being counted, the letter explained. Some bodies had been buried by collapsed walls and would not be recovered until spring.

Until spring. As if spring were a certainty now, as if the calendar would simply continue its ancient progression regardless of what moved beyond the mountains.

Chen rose and walked to the window. Lin'an spread below him in its familiar tapestry of tiled roofs and canal bridges, the afternoon light catching the distant pagoda of the Temple of the Soul's Retreat. Somewhere in the city, the relay rider was likely sleeping, or drinking, or weeping into his hands—Chen did not know the man and could not guess which response survival had demanded of him. The horse was certainly dead. That much was definite.

He would need to present these dispatches to the Council of State before evening. There would be celebration, carefully measured—the Emperor preferred his ministers to exhibit composure in both triumph and disaster. There would be credit to distribute, and Chen already knew how that distribution would unfold. Prime Minister Jia's faction would claim the garrison's resilience as validation of their defensive strategy. The war advocates would argue that now, with Mongol momentum broken, was the moment to counterattack. Both arguments would be made with great eloquence and complete sincerity, and both would be primarily concerned with positioning for the winter appointments.

Chen did not blame his colleagues for this. He had done the same, would do the same again before the day ended. The court was a mechanism, and mechanisms required their operators to behave predictably. What troubled him was not the politics but the silence beneath it—the awareness, shared by everyone and spoken by no one, that Xiangyang's survival changed nothing fundamental. The Mongols had ruled the north for generations. They had absorbed kingdoms,eli­minated dynasties, crossed deserts and mountains that should have been impassable. They did not abandon campaigns; they refined them.

The maple released another leaf. Chen watched it spiral downward and disappear behind the courtyard wall.

Vice Commissioner Zhao's second letter, the personal one that had been folded inside the official dispatch, contained a single line that Chen had not yet decided how to interpret. *The walls held because good men died holding them. Remember this when the court decides what their deaths have purchased.*

It was an accusation, or perhaps a plea. Zhao had always been more direct than the capital preferred. It was why he had been posted to Xiangyang in the first place—too valuable to dismiss, too inconvenient to promote. Now he would return a hero, and the court would need to find new ways to manage his inconvenience.

Chen returned to his desk and began drafting his memorandum to the Council. He wrote slowly, choosing each character with care, aware that these words would be preserved in the imperial archives and read by historians who would know how the story ended. He wondered what those future readers would think of him—whether they would understand that he had felt relief and dread in equal measure, that he had celebrated Xiangyang's survival while mourning what survival had cost and fearing what it had merely postponed.

He wondered if Wei Tan was alive, and if the boy would still write to him twice yearly, and if the letters would still be full of careful observations or whether they would now contain the kind of silences that could not be translated into brushstrokes.

Outside, the autumn afternoon continued its slow diminishment toward evening. The temple bells would ring soon, marking the hour, measuring out the time that remained. Chen finished his memorandum and set down his brush and sat for a long moment with his hands folded, looking at nothing, allowing himself to feel—briefly, privately—the full weight of a survival that was not yet victory and might never become one.

The maple had nearly finished its surrender. A few leaves clung to the highest branches, stubborn or oblivious, waiting for a wind that had not yet arrived.
