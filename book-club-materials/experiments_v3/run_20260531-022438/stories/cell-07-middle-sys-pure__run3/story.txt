# The Last Window

## Nagasaki, 1880

The fan moved slowly in the official's hand, each motion precise as a clock pendulum. Willem van der Berg had counted seven oscillations since presenting his proposal. Seven small eternities in which the afternoon light shifted imperceptibly across the tatami mats and his left foot began its familiar descent into numbness.

"You understand," Magistrate Hayashi said finally, "that the Shogun's government has received similar requests before."

"I do not doubt it, Hayashi-sama." Willem kept his eyes appropriately lowered. Twenty-three years on Dejima had taught him the geometry of submission. "Yet the Dutch East India Company has maintained its friendship with Japan for nearly three centuries. We ask only that this friendship be permitted to deepen slightly."

Through the paper screens, Willem could hear the harbor sounds that had scored his entire adult life. The slap of water against Dejima's stone walls. The calls of inspectors examining every barrel and crate for forbidden Christian materials. Somewhere beyond the guarded bridge, Nagasaki carried on its business, a city he had glimpsed only in fragments despite decades of residence.

Hayashi set the fan across his knee. He was perhaps fifty, with the soft hands of a bureaucrat and the watchful eyes of a man who had risen through the Tokugawa administration by making no mistakes. His Dutch was excellent, far better than Willem's Japanese, which they both pretended not to notice.

"Deepen," Hayashi repeated, tasting the word. "An interesting choice. You wish to expand beyond Dejima. To establish merchant houses in Nagasaki proper. Perhaps eventually in Osaka, in Edo itself."

"Nothing so ambitious." Willem produced a folded document from his sleeve, laying it on the mat between them with both hands. "Merely permission for two additional Company representatives. And perhaps—" He hesitated, knowing how much weight the next words carried. "Perhaps quarterly passage to Nagasaki for medical supplies. Our physician grows old. His instruments require maintenance we cannot provide on the island."

The fan resumed its motion. Hayashi did not reach for the document.

"Your physician. Doctor Brouwer, yes? He has trained six Japanese students in Western surgical techniques over the past decade. The Shogun's government is grateful for this knowledge."

Willem felt the familiar tightening in his chest. Gratitude, in Hayashi's mouth, was never simple. It was a ledger entry, an accounting of debts that flowed only one direction.

"Doctor Brouwer considers it an honor to share what he knows."

"Indeed." Hayashi's gaze drifted toward the screened window, beyond which the masts of Dutch ships rose like bare winter trees. Only three vessels this year. Willem remembered when there had been seven, eight, when Dejima had hummed with commerce rather than merely persisting. "Tell me, Van der Berg-san. What news from Europe?"

The question was delivered lightly, almost carelessly. Willem recognized the weight beneath it.

"I am a merchant, Hayashi-sama. Politics holds little interest for me."

"And yet merchants travel. They listen. They write letters." The fan stilled. "The British and French, I understand, continue their quarrel over African territories. The Germans consolidate. The Americans—" A ghost of a smile crossed Hayashi's face. "The Americans remain occupied with their own continent. Their great civil war settled certain questions, but raised others."

Willem said nothing. He understood now why his request had been granted an audience at all. The document on the mat between them was merely pretext.

"Twenty-six years ago," Hayashi continued, "American warships entered this harbor. Their commodore made demands. Threats." He spoke as if describing ancient history, something from a scroll rather than living memory. "The Shogun's government considered these demands carefully. We observed how the Chinese had suffered when they opened their doors to Western commerce. The opium. The missionaries. The gunboats that followed when merchants were unsatisfied."

"The Dutch have never—"

"No." Hayashi's agreement was swift, a door closing. "The Dutch have been good guests. You stay on your island. You observe our laws. You do not bring priests or Bibles. This is why you remain when all others were expelled." He picked up the document, unfolding it with ritual care. "Two additional representatives. Quarterly medical passages."

Willem waited.

"The Company's trade revenues have declined considerably," Hayashi observed. "Copper exports alone cannot sustain your operations. Your shareholders in Amsterdam grow restless, I imagine. They see the British profits in China, the French profits in Indochina, and they wonder why Dutch profits in Japan remain so modest."

"The Company values stability over expansion."

Hayashi laughed softly. It was, Willem realized, the first genuine expression he had witnessed from the man in four years of annual negotiations.

"You are a poor liar, Van der Berg-san. It is perhaps your most useful quality." He refolded the document. "I will recommend to my superiors that your requests be granted. Two additional representatives, subject to the usual restrictions. Quarterly passages for medical supplies, with inspection."

The relief that flooded Willem's chest was immediately tempered by suspicion. Nothing came freely through the Last Window, as the Dutch called their tenuous aperture into Japan.

"The Shogun's government is generous."

"The Shogun's government is practical." Hayashi tucked the document into his sleeve. "In exchange, you will provide a detailed accounting of current European naval capabilities. Armaments, ship designs, fleet deployments. Doctor Brouwer will prepare a written summary of recent Western medical advances, particularly in the treatment of battlefield injuries." He paused. "And you will explain to me, clearly and completely, how the British manufacture their new steel."

Willem's mouth went dry. The rangaku scholars, the Japanese students of Dutch learning, had been pressing for technical knowledge for decades. But this was different. This was specific. This suggested purpose.

"I am a merchant," he said again, hearing how weak it sounded. "I know little of metallurgy."

"Then you will learn." Hayashi rose, signaling the audience's end. Willem struggled to his feet, his left leg blazing with returning circulation. "Your next ship arrives in October, yes? Four months should provide sufficient time for research. The Company maintains archives. Correspondents. Resources a simple merchant might access, if properly motivated."

They faced each other across the afternoon light. Outside, a bell rang the changing of the harbor watch.

"May I ask," Willem said carefully, "what purpose this information serves? Japan has refused Western weapons for generations. Your government has maintained peace by maintaining isolation."

Hayashi moved toward the door, then paused. When he turned, his expression held something Willem had never seen there before. Not quite respect. Perhaps recognition.

"Twenty-six years ago, we turned away American warships. Do you know how, Van der Berg-san?"

Willem shook his head.

"We did not defeat them. We simply demonstrated that defeating us would cost more than victory was worth. Our coastal batteries. Our willingness to burn Nagasaki ourselves rather than see it occupied." The fan reappeared, resumed its measured arc. "The Americans were not prepared for such resolve. They left. They did not return. But we have never forgotten how close the matter was. How few cannon separated independence from subjugation."

He slid open the screen, revealing the guarded corridor beyond.

"The world does not stand still, Van der Berg-san. Even for those who wish it would. Japan has chosen isolation, but isolation is not ignorance. We watch. We learn. We prepare." A slight bow, perfectly calibrated. "Four months. I look forward to your report."

Willem returned the bow, holding it until he heard the screen close. When he straightened, he was alone with the harbor sounds and the slanting light and the weight of what he had agreed to carry.

On the desk where Hayashi had sat, a small porcelain dish held three Dutch coins. Payment for tea that had never been offered. Willem pocketed them automatically—waste was a sin on Dejima, where everything arrived by ship and nothing could be replaced easily.

The walk back to the Dutch factory took him across the single bridge connecting the island to the city. Guards checked his papers, examined his sleeves, patted the lining of his coat for contraband. He submitted to it all with the patience of long practice. Twenty-three years. His hair had been brown when he first crossed this bridge. Now it was gray, and the wife he had left in Rotterdam had remarried, and his children had grown into strangers who wrote dutiful letters about lives he could not imagine.

He had chosen this. Chosen the Last Window over the wider world, chosen the certainty of restriction over the chaos of freedom. Some nights he could not remember why.

Doctor Brouwer was waiting in the factory's small courtyard, tending the roses he had somehow coaxed into blooming in Dejima's salt air. He looked up as Willem approached, reading the outcome in his posture.

"They agreed?"

"They agreed to everything." Willem sank onto a wooden bench, suddenly exhausted. "And demanded everything in return."

Brouwer set down his pruning shears. At seventy-three, he moved carefully, but his mind remained sharp as his surgical instruments. "What do they want?"

"Steel manufacturing. Naval specifications. Medical advances." Willem closed his eyes. "They want to know how the West makes war."

Silence. The roses swayed in the harbor breeze.

"Interesting," Brouwer said finally. "After all these years. Why now?"

Willem thought of Hayashi's face in the slanting light. The ghost of a smile when he spoke of American warships turning away. The careful emphasis on preparation.

"Because they're afraid," he said. "They won. They kept us out. But they know it was closer than anyone admits, and they know it won't get easier." He opened his eyes, staring at the roses. "They're still fighting Perry's war. They just don't want anyone to know it."

Somewhere beyond the walls, a temple bell marked the hour. The sound drifted across the water, across the guarded bridge, across all the distances that Willem had spent his life trying to cross.

Four months to gather secrets. Four months to become something other than a merchant.

The Last Window remained open. But Willem was beginning to understand that windows let things pass in both directions, and the view from inside was never the same as the view from without.
