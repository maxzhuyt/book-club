# The Weight of Paper

The chrysanthemums in Minister Shen Quyin's garden had opened three days past, and already their scent carried the first suggestion of decay. He could smell them through the latticed window of his study, sweet and funereal, as the afternoon light stretched long across his writing desk. Autumn had come early to Lin'an this year. The scholars in the Bureau of Astronomy had noted it, adding the observation to their endless records of portents and patterns, as if heaven's movements might be read like a memorial to the throne.

Shen Quyin was sixty-one years old. He had served four emperors and survived three purges of the court. He knew better than to trust portents.

The dispatches had arrived at the Ministry of War shortly after the noon bells, carried by a relay rider whose horse had died beneath him at the last post station. The rider himself had collapsed in the courtyard, his lips cracked and his eyes showing white. Someone had given him water. Someone else had taken the leather satchel from his shoulder and brought it, with appropriate ceremony and inappropriate haste, to the Senior Minister of the Left.

Now the satchel sat on Shen Quyin's desk, its brass clasps dull with road dust and river damp. He had not yet opened it.

This was weakness, he knew. A minister of the Song should approach all matters with equanimity, his brush hand steady whether recording triumph or catastrophe. But the satchel had come from Xiangyang, and for six years Xiangyang had been a wound in the empire's side that would not close. Six years of siege. Six years of watching the Mongol noose tighten around the twin cities on the Han River, knowing that when they fell—*when*, not *if*, the court's whispers had long since abandoned hope—the river road to Lin'an would lie open.

Six years of writing letters to garrison commanders who might already be dead by the time his brush lifted from the paper.

He had known Lu Wenhuan, the defender of Xiangyang, when they were both young men sitting for the examinations. Lu had placed third in their year. Shen Quyin had placed nineteenth. They had drunk wine together once, in a garden not unlike this one, and Lu had recited a poem about geese flying south that Shen Quyin still remembered, though he had forgotten the occasion that prompted it.

The afternoon light moved across the desk.

Shen Quyin opened the satchel.

---

There were seven documents inside, rolled and sealed with the garrison's chop. He arranged them by the dates marked on their outer casings, oldest to newest, though the oldest was barely three weeks past. The relay system had performed better than he might have hoped. Or perhaps the riders had understood what they carried and had ridden accordingly, killing horses and risking their own lives for paper and ink.

Paper and ink. The empire ran on nothing else.

He broke the first seal.

*To the Ministry of War, from the Garrison Command of Xiangyang, in the eighth month of the ninth year of the Xianchun reign.*

*This memorial respectfully reports on the disposition of forces following the enemy's third assault on the western wall...*

The language was formal, almost serene, the words of a man who had learned to write about death the way a physician learns to cut flesh—with precision, without trembling. Shen Quyin read of the Mongol siege engines, the great trebuchets that the enemy had deployed from counterweight designs, hurling stones larger than any Song fortification had been built to withstand. He read of the section of wall near the River Gate that had collapsed on the afternoon of the fourteenth day, burying eighteen men beneath it. He read the names of those men, listed in order of rank, and then the names of their commanding officers, and then an accounting of the arrows and crossbow bolts expended in repelling the assault that followed.

The second dispatch described the famine.

*The civilian population within the walls has been reduced to approximately eleven thousand souls. Daily rice rations have been further decreased to four ounces per adult, two ounces per child. The garrison itself maintains six ounces per soldier to preserve fighting capacity. This office regrets to report that incidents of unauthorized slaughter of horses and mules have increased despite the penalties prescribed...*

Shen Quyin set the paper down. He looked at his garden through the window, at the chrysanthemums nodding in the slight breeze, at the ornamental stones arranged to suggest mountains he had never climbed. His cook was preparing dinner somewhere in the household. He could smell ginger and pork.

Four ounces of rice. He tried to imagine it cupped in his palm.

He picked up the third dispatch.

---

The fifth document was different.

It was not written in the hand of a secretary. The brushwork was rougher, the characters formed by someone writing quickly, or in poor light, or both. It bore Lu Wenhuan's personal seal alongside the garrison chop.

*Old friend*, it began, *if you are reading this, then the riders have done their duty and I have done mine. I will write what the formal memorials cannot say.*

*The Mongols broke through the River Gate on the morning of the twenty-second day. They had breached the wall the night before, working through darkness with a determination I have never seen in any army. By dawn there were perhaps three hundred of their horsemen inside the city, and our reserves were already committed to the western district where a fire had spread from their incendiary bombardment.*

*I believed we were finished. I had already written my final memorial and given it to my adjutant with instructions to burn it if by some miracle we survived. I did not expect miracles.*

Shen Quyin's hand was trembling. He steadied it against the edge of the desk.

*What happened next, I cannot fully explain. The Mongol cavalry had formed up in the market square, preparing to sweep through to the garrison headquarters. They outnumbered our remaining mobile forces three to one. And then—*

*The granary beside the square caught fire. I do not know if it was set deliberately or if it spread from the western district. In either case, the smoke that rose from it was black and thick, and the wind—the wind changed, old friend. It had blown from the north all morning, driving the flames toward us. And then it shifted, suddenly, completely, and drove the smoke directly into the Mongol formation.*

*Horses fear fire above all things. In the confusion, in the blindness of that smoke, their cavalry broke apart. Some rode into walls. Some trampled each other. Our crossbowmen on the remaining towers found their marks.*

*By noon, the Mongols inside the walls were dead or captured. By evening, we saw their main force withdrawing to the north.*

*I do not know if heaven intervened. I do not know if some clever soldier set that fire at precisely the right moment. I know only that Xiangyang stands, and that I am alive to write these words, and that eleven thousand civilians and four thousand soldiers share my astonishment.*

*The formal reports will tell you about supplies needed, walls to be rebuilt, reinforcements required before winter. This letter tells you only what I could not say in any document that might be read aloud in court: we are alive. The river road remains closed to the Khan's armies. And I remembered, in the hour when I thought I would die, that poem about the geese flying south. I could not recall who I had recited it to, all those years ago. Now I remember. It was you.*

*Your friend in survival,*
*Lu Wenhuan*

---

The sixth and seventh dispatches were addenda: lists of the dead, assessments of structural damage, formal requests for grain and timber and iron and men. Shen Quyin read them carefully, making notes on a separate sheet, his brush moving with the automatic discipline of forty years' practice. Three hundred and twelve soldiers killed in the final assault. Forty-seven civilians dead from starvation in the week preceding it. The western wall would require six months to rebuild properly, three months if they cut corners that would need to be repaired within the year. The garrison's arrow supply was down to four thousand shafts. They had no more naphtha for fire weapons. They had no more horses.

But they had walls. They had the river. They had time.

The afternoon light had faded to amber, then to grey. Someone had lit a lamp in the corridor outside his study; he could see its glow beneath the door. His dinner would be cold by now, or his servants would be keeping it warm over coals, waiting for the minister to emerge. He did not emerge.

Instead, he sat in the gathering darkness and thought about Kublai Khan.

The Great Khan was not a fool. He had spent six years on Xiangyang because he understood, as clearly as any Song strategist, that the twin cities controlled the Han River, and the Han River controlled access to the Yangtze, and the Yangtze was the empire's spine. With Xiangyang fallen, Mongol armies could have swept down the waterways to Lin'an itself within a year. Without Xiangyang, they would have to find another path—through mountains that had broken armies before, or across the river at points where Song fortifications and Song ships could bleed them for every li of progress.

The siege had failed. But the Khan had other armies, other engineers, other years of patience. He was younger than Shen Quyin by a decade, and his empire stretched from the western seas to the Korean peninsula. He could afford to wait, to probe, to try again.

This victory was not an ending. It was a reprieve.

Shen Quyin thought of the eleven thousand civilians inside Xiangyang's walls, eating their four ounces of rice, watching the smoke rise from the granary fire that had saved them. Did they understand that they might face another siege within five years? Within three? Did they know that their survival depended on the continued health of an aging emperor, on the competence of generals not yet tested, on the treasury's ability to fund the rebuilding that the dispatches so meticulously requested?

Did Lu Wenhuan know, when he wrote his letter about heaven's intervention and remembered poems, that he might die in the same city he had just saved, in another siege, in another year?

Of course he knew. He was not a fool either.

---

The lamp in the corridor went out. Someone had given up waiting for him.

Shen Quyin lit his own lamp, a small clay dish of oil with a cotton wick that his wife had given him years ago, before her death. The flame was weak but steady. He positioned it beside his inkstone and began to grind fresh ink, the circular motion as familiar as breathing.

He would need to write three documents tonight.

The first would go to the Ministry of War, a summary of the dispatches suitable for distribution to the relevant bureaus. It would be factual, restrained, appropriate for bureaucratic circulation. It would note the successful defense of Xiangyang, the withdrawal of Mongol forces, the garrison's supply requirements. It would not mention wind or fire or poems about geese.

The second would go to the Grand Councilor, for the Emperor's attention. It would be more delicate, praising the garrison's valor without suggesting that the throne's policies had ever been in doubt, recommending reinforcement without implying that current defenses were inadequate. It would walk the careful line that all communications to the throne must walk, conveying truth through the elaborate courtesy that made truth speakable in the halls of power.

The third would go to Lu Wenhuan.

Shen Quyin did not yet know what he would write in that one.

He began with the first document, because it was easiest, because the forms for such reports had been established centuries ago and required only that he fill in the relevant details with an appropriate style. He wrote quickly, pausing only to re-ink his brush, producing three pages of elegant administrative prose that said everything necessary and nothing more.

The second document took longer. He revised his opening sentence four times before achieving the proper balance of humility and confidence. He spent twenty minutes on a single phrase describing the Khan's withdrawal, seeking language that acknowledged the enemy's strength without suggesting that the Song had merely been lucky. When he finished, he read it through twice, made three small corrections, and set it aside to dry.

The third document remained unwritten.

His lamp had burned down to a fingernail's width of oil. The autumn night had turned cold; he could feel it through the walls, through the latticed window that still stood open to his darkened garden. The chrysanthemums would be closing now, folding their petals against the chill. In the morning they would open again, a little more faded than before, a little closer to the end of their season.

Shen Quyin pulled a fresh sheet of paper toward him.

*To my old friend Lu Wenhuan*, he wrote, *from his old friend who placed nineteenth to his third, and who never forgot the poem about the geese, though he forgot for many years that it was you who recited it.*

*I have read your dispatches. I have written my reports. The court will send you grain and timber and men, because that is what the court does when a garrison has proven its worth. You will rebuild your walls, and you will wait, and in a year or three years or five years, the Khan will try again, because that is what the Khan does when an obstacle remains in his path.*

*I cannot tell you that the next siege will end as this one did. I cannot tell you that heaven will send another wind. I can tell you only that today, in this hour, Xiangyang stands, and you are alive, and somewhere in the north a great Khan is reconsidering his plans.*

*This is what we do, you and I. We hold what can be held. We write what must be written. We remember poems from our youth and hope that someone will remember them after we are gone.*

*The geese fly south each autumn. They do not know if they will return in spring. They fly anyway.*

*Your friend in paper and ink,*
*Shen Quyin*

He set down his brush. The lamp guttered and died, leaving him in darkness, the ink still wet on the page, the smell of chrysanthemums drifting through the open window.

In the morning, he would seal all three letters and send them to their destinations. The empire would turn on his words, as it always turned on words, and men would live or die according to the decisions those words prompted. This was the weight of paper. This was the burden of the brush.

But for now, in the darkness, he allowed himself a moment of something that was not quite hope and not quite grief, but something in between—the feeling of a man who has received news that the worst has not yet happened, and who knows that the worst is still coming, and who chooses, despite everything, to light another lamp.
