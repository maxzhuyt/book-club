# Structural Beats for "Dispatches from Xiangyang"

## 5-Point To-Do List

1. **Establish the Minister's Physical Context** — Open with the minister in his study or office within Lin'an, surrounded by details that convey both the administrative weight of the Southern Song bureaucracy and the autumn season (fading light, cooling air, perhaps chrysanthemums), grounding the reader in place and time before the emotional content arrives.

2. **Layer the Reading with Accumulated Tension** — Structure the dispatch-reading as a gradual reveal, moving from formal military language through increasingly personal details of the siege's final days—the starvation, the failed Mongol assault on the walls, the moment the northern cavalry withdrew—allowing the minister (and reader) to absorb the magnitude of what almost was lost.

3. **Counterpoint Relief with Survivor's Accounting** — Complicate the victory by including costs within the dispatches: names of fallen commanders, reports of civilian deaths, descriptions of the garrison's depleted state. The siege failed, but Xiangyang paid in bodies, creating moral weight rather than triumphalism.

4. **Introduce the Strategic Uncertainty Ahead** — Have the minister's thoughts turn from what happened to what comes next: Will Kublai Khan regroup? Can the river defenses hold if tested again? Plant the seed that this reprieve may be temporary, maintaining narrative tension despite victory.

5. **Close on Personal-Political Intersection** — End with the minister composing his response or report to the court, forcing him to translate grief, relief, and strategic anxiety into careful bureaucratic language—revealing character through the gap between private feeling and public duty.
