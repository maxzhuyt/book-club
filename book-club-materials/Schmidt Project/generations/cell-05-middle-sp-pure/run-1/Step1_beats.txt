# Key Structural Beats for Original Scene: "The Agrarian Memo" (Winter Palace, 1885)

Based on the counterfactual premise of Alexander II's survival and continued reforms, here are the 5 most important structural elements this scene requires:

1. **Establish the Bureaucrat's Position and Stakes** - Ground the reader in who this civil servant is within the imperial hierarchy. Their rank, ambitions, and personal investment in reform (or self-preservation) should color every word they consider putting to paper. The memo is not just policy but career gamble.

2. **Convey the Alternate Political Landscape** - Through the drafting process, reveal how four additional years of reform have changed Russia. References to earlier successes, failures, or compromises in the memo's background section can efficiently show this altered world without halting the narrative.

3. **Create Tension Through the Act of Writing** - The physical and mental process of drafting becomes the action. Show hesitation over word choices, crossed-out phrases, awareness of who will read this document. Each revision reveals both character psychology and political danger.

4. **Layer Conflicting Pressures** - The bureaucrat must navigate between progressive reformers pushing for acceleration, conservative nobility resisting further change, and the aging Tsar's own uncertain temperament. These opposing forces should feel present even in a solitary office.

5. **End with Consequential Commitment** - Conclude with a decisive act—signing the memo, sealing it, or perhaps destroying a draft—that signals both personal risk and historical momentum toward the next chapter of this alternate Russia.
