# The Agrarian Memo

**Winter Palace, St. Petersburg**
**February 1885**

The ink had frozen in its well again.

Dmitri Alexeyevich Voronov held the small porcelain vessel near the lamp's flame, watching the black surface slowly liquify. Beyond his window the Neva lay entombed in ice, and the February wind found every gap in the ancient casements. He had stuffed paper into the worst of them. The paper was a draft of last year's zemstvo proposal, which had pleased no one and offended everyone, which meant it had probably been correct.

He set the ink down and flexed his fingers. Forty-three years old, and already his knuckles ached in winter like his father's had. His father had been a serf on the Sheremetev estates until the Emancipation. Now Dmitri sat in the Winter Palace drafting memoranda for the Ministry of State Domains. The arc of one generation, bent by a Tsar's signature.

That Tsar was still alive. This fact shaped everything.

Dmitri pulled a fresh sheet toward him and wrote the date in his careful ministerial hand. Below it: *On the Question of Communal Land Tenure and Individual Allotment in the Central Agricultural Provinces: Recommendations for the Next Phase.* He considered the title. *Next Phase* suggested inevitability, momentum. Perhaps *Proposed Considerations* was safer. He crossed out nothing yet. First the full thought, then the pruning.

*His Imperial Majesty's wisdom in extending the redemption payment period*, he began, *has materially improved the condition of the peasant cultivator while maintaining social stability in the rural districts.*

True enough. The reduction from forty-nine years to thirty had been announced in 1883, on the twenty-second anniversary of Emancipation. Dmitri had been in the crowd outside the Cathedral of Our Lady of Kazan when the decree was read. He remembered weeping. Around him, others had wept too, or stood in stunned silence, or hurried away to send telegrams to the provinces. The conservative papers had predicted chaos. Chaos had not come.

But neither had prosperity.

He continued: *However, the communal system of land redistribution, while serving important functions of social cohesion and mutual obligation, has in practice created impediments to agricultural improvement that merit examination.*

Here was the knife's edge. The *obshchina*, the village commune, was holy to some and hateful to others. The Slavophiles saw in it the Russian soul itself, proof that Holy Russia need not follow the individualist path of the corrupt West. The liberals saw a relic that trapped peasants in inefficiency, preventing the emergence of a proper yeoman class. His Majesty had never declared himself fully for either camp. This was perhaps why he remained alive.

Dmitri thought of Sophia Perovskaya's face in the newspapers four years ago, after the trial. She had been hanged with the others, the last conspirators of the People's Will. The bomb that March day had torn the Tsar's carriage apart but somehow left the man himself merely wounded. Shrapnel in the leg, a ruptured eardrum, three months of recovery. And then he had emerged not chastened into reaction, as his advisors urged, but strangely liberated. *They have tried to kill the reform*, he reportedly told Loris-Melikov, *and so the reform must live.*

Whether the quote was real or apocryphal, it had become true through repetition.

Dmitri dipped his pen. *The undersigned respectfully submits that a pilot program permitting voluntary separation from communal tenure, limited initially to three provinces, would provide empirical evidence—*

He stopped. *Voluntary separation.* Count Tolstoy would read those words and see the dissolution of Russia. Konstantin Pobedonostsev would see godlessness, Western contamination, the abandonment of the peasant to the mercies of capital. And they would not be entirely wrong to see these things, which was what made the work so difficult.

He thought of his father again. Pyotr Voronov had died in 1879, having spent eighteen free years working the same land he had worked as a serf, making the same redemption payments that felt indistinguishable from the old obligations, drinking himself into his grave with the same vodka the state monopoly provided. Freedom had been an abstraction to him. The land remained another man's. The debt remained.

*This is why*, Dmitri thought. *This is why it must be more than words.*

He wrote: *—would provide empirical evidence upon which future policy might be rationally founded, while containing any disruption within manageable boundaries.*

The lamp flickered. He had been at this desk since four o'clock. Outside, the early darkness of northern winter had long since fallen. Somewhere in the palace, the Tsar was dining, or receiving ministers, or simply sitting with the Empress in whatever private exhaustion a twice-targeted man permitted himself. Dmitri had seen Alexander Nikolayevich once, at a ministerial reception. The Emperor had looked tired. His beard was whiter than in the portraits. His right hand trembled slightly—nerve damage from the bombing, it was said. But his eyes, when they passed over Dmitri's face, had been sharp and searching, as if looking for something he could not name.

*Does he know what we are asking him to do?* Dmitri wondered. *Does he understand that we are asking him to finish what he started, knowing it may finish him?*

The conservatives whispered that the reforms had made the assassination attempts inevitable, that loosening the bonds of authority only invited further demands. The radicals whispered that the reforms had not gone far enough, that the Tsar remained a tyrant in liberal costume. Between these two positions lay a narrow space in which men like Dmitri tried to do useful work.

He read over what he had written. It was cautious. It was measured. It would offend no one so grievously that it could not be discussed, but it pointed in a direction, however timidly.

Was that enough?

He added: *The undersigned is aware that any alteration to communal tenure will excite strong sentiment among those who regard the obshchina as essential to Russian identity. He respectfully suggests that Russian identity has proven itself capacious enough to absorb the transformation of twenty million souls from bondage to legal personhood. It may perhaps accommodate this smaller adjustment.*

That was too much. He knew it as soon as the words dried. The sentence was argumentative, even faintly mocking. Pobedonostsev would take it as a personal affront. Even Dmitri's own minister, the generally sympathetic Nikolai Bunge, would wince at the tone.

And yet he did not cross it out.

He thought of the peasants in Voronezh province whom he had interviewed last spring, part of a ministerial fact-finding tour. One old man had invited him into a hut that smelled of cabbage and damp earth. The man had ten children living; four had died. He farmed strips of land scattered across three fields, as the commune's redistribution system dictated, walking miles between plots. When Dmitri asked if he would prefer consolidated holdings, the man had looked at him with an expression of profound wariness. *And if the harvest fails?* he asked. *Who helps me then?*

The commune was not merely inefficient. It was insurance. It was the only safety these people had ever known.

But insurance against what? Against the very risks that kept them poor. Against the possibility of improvement itself.

Dmitri rubbed his eyes. The hour was late. The lamp was burning low. He should finish the draft, sleep, revise tomorrow with fresh perspective. That was the sensible course.

Instead he wrote: *The question before His Imperial Majesty's government is not whether Russia shall change—she is changing, irrevocably, with every verst of railroad laid and every factory chimney raised. The question is whether that change shall be directed by deliberate policy or abandoned to accident and crisis. The undersigned believes the former course preferable. He submits these recommendations in that spirit.*

He set down the pen. The ink was cooling again, thickening toward uselessness. Outside, a church bell struck the hour—ten o'clock. In seven hours the palace would stir. In eight, the ministers would begin their meetings. In nine, perhaps, the Tsar would review papers brought to him by various secretaries, and this memorandum might or might not be among them.

Dmitri read the document from the beginning. It was neither bold nor cautious, neither revolutionary nor reactionary. It was the work of a man trying to thread a needle in poor light. Perhaps that was all any of them could do.

He thought of signing it. His name, affixed to these words, would follow him forever. If the pilot program was approved and succeeded, he might rise. If it failed or was rejected with prejudice, he might fall. If it simply disappeared into the ministerial archives, as so many documents did, he would remain exactly where he was: a middle-ranking bureaucrat in a cold office, doing work that might mean something or might mean nothing.

His father would have found this incomprehensible. Pyotr Voronov had wanted only to own the land he worked, to pass it to his sons, to die knowing his labor had purchased something solid. He had achieved none of these things. The land remained the landlord's, legally; the redemption payments continued after his death; and what he passed to Dmitri was only the memory of wanting.

*I am trying*, Dmitri thought, as if his father could hear him. *I am trying to make the next generation's wanting smaller.*

He signed the memorandum. Dmitri Alexeyevich Voronov, Collegiate Assessor, Ministry of State Domains. The ink gleamed wetly in the lamplight, then dulled as it dried.

He folded the document, sealed it with wax, and wrote the address of his minister's office on the exterior. Tomorrow he would carry it through the palace corridors himself. He would place it on the appropriate desk. And then he would return to this room and begin drafting the next memorandum, or the one after that, each small document a small stone laid in a foundation that might support something or might simply sink into the Russian mud.

The lamp guttered and went out. Dmitri sat in the darkness for a moment, listening to the wind, feeling the cold seep through the inadequate paper stuffed into the casements.

Then he rose, gathered his coat, and went out into the frozen city to find his way home.
