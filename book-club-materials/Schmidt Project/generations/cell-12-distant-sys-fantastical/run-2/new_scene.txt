# The Sibyl's Instruction

The debate over Crassus's Parthian grain contracts had dragged into its second hour when the lictors parted at the eastern entrance. Gaius Scribonius Curio, mid-sentence and red-faced, fell silent. Every head turned.

The Keeper of the Sibylline Books walked barefoot on the cold marble, as protocol demanded when bearing active prophecy. Behind him came two priests of Apollo, their presence confirming what the chamber already understood: this was no routine consultation, no convenient divination produced to settle a factional squabble. The Keeper carried a sealed bronze cylinder, and even from across the chamber, Marcus Porcius Cato could see the faint luminescence bleeding through its seams.

The words were still burning.

"Conscript fathers," the Keeper announced, his voice carrying the flat affect required of his office, "three days past, the Sibyl at Cumae spoke unprompted. The sacred flame turned white. The prophecy has been verified by the college of augurs and confirmed by signs at three separate temples." He paused, letting the weight settle. "Rome has received instruction."

Cato watched his colleagues arrange their faces into masks of pious attention. Pompey sat motionless in the front benches, his expression unreadable, though his hand had moved to the small gold talisman he wore beneath his toga—Cato had noticed him clutching it more frequently these past months. Two rows behind him, Caesar leaned forward with what appeared to be genuine interest, though with Caesar, appearances were their own form of currency.

The Consul Metellus Celer rose from the curule chair. "Let the instruction be read into the record."

The Keeper broke the seal. Light spilled out, not dramatically, but with the steady glow of heated iron. He unrolled the transcription—the original words could never leave Cumae, of course, but the copies retained enough divine residue to discourage creative interpretation. For a generation, at least.

"'The son of the sea has offended the son of the sky,'" the Keeper read. "'Let Rome send the offender from Italia before the summer stars rise, or the Tiber will remember its old bed. So speaks Apollo through his servant.'"

Silence.

Cato felt the calculation rippling through the chamber like wind through wheat. The son of the sea. Neptune's lineage, then, or some claimed connection to it. His mind was already sorting through the possibilities when Lucius Domitius Ahenobarbus rose from the benches opposite.

"The instruction is clear," Ahenobarbus declared, not bothering to hide his satisfaction. "Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus traces his family's divine patronage through Neptune. He has spoken publicly of this connection on no fewer than—"

"The Domitii claim Mars," Pompey interrupted, still seated, his voice dangerously mild. "Should we exile half the Senate every time the war god quarrels with another Olympian?" He gestured lazily toward the cylinder, still glowing in the Keeper's hands. "The prophecy says 'offended.' I have undertaken no action that might give Jupiter cause for anger. I have performed the prescribed sacrifices. I have—"

"The prophecy does not require your acknowledgment to be valid."

"Nor does it name me."

Cato rose. He had not intended to speak—had, in fact, planned to let the Pompeian and Caesarean factions tear at each other while he watched for advantage—but the argument was already sliding toward the familiar grooves of factional warfare, and that would not serve. Not with the words still burning.

"Conscript fathers." He waited until the murmuring subsided. "We debate interpretation as though this were a contract dispute with a Campanian grain merchant. It is not. The gods do not negotiate. They do not clarify upon request. We may send to Cumae, and the Sibyl will say what she has already said, or she will say nothing, or she will say something worse." He turned to face Pompey directly. "The question before us is not whether Gnaeus Pompeius has given offense. The question is whether Rome will stake the city itself on his certainty that he has not."

"You would have me exile myself on the strength of a phrase that might apply to a hundred men?"

"I would have you demonstrate the piety you have so often claimed."

"Piety." Pompey's voice hardened. "I stood in the Temple of Neptune during the Mithridatic campaign and received his blessing before the fleet. The priests confirmed the signs. I have that god's favor, Cato, written in victories across three continents. Does Jupiter quarrel with his brother over my service? Then let Jupiter say so plainly."

"He has."

The voice came from the back benches, thin with age but carrying the particular weight of a man who had survived too much to fear the room's displeasure. Lucius Aurelius Cotta, eighty years old, consul twice, and one of the few living senators who had witnessed the last time Rome had ignored a direct Sibylline instruction.

The chamber went quiet.

"I was seventeen," Cotta said, not rising, letting his colleagues crane their necks to see him, "when the oracle demanded we abandon the Sicilian campaign against Spartacus's naval allies. The Senate debated for six days. Interpretation. Faction. Who benefited, who suffered. The usual questions." He coughed, a dry rattle that echoed in the silence. "On the seventh day, the Tiber rose thirty feet in an hour. No rain. No storm. The water simply... remembered, as the prophecy said it might. The Sublician Bridge was torn away. The granaries in the Velabrum flooded. Four thousand people drowned in the streets, and we could hear them screaming from the Temple of Jupiter, where the Senate had fled to debate further."

No one spoke.

"We exiled the admiral that same afternoon," Cotta continued. "Gaius Verres. Some of you remember him. The water receded before sunset. The bodies took longer to clear." He fixed his rheumy eyes on Pompey. "I do not know if you have offended Jupiter, Gnaeus Pompeius. Neither do you. That is rather the point of piety—it does not require certainty. It requires only the wisdom to recognize what we cannot know and the humility to act accordingly."

Pompey's face had gone carefully blank, the expression of a man reassessing the terrain of a battle he had expected to win easily. Cato watched him glance toward Caesar, a flicker of eye contact that confirmed what everyone suspected about their private alliance.

Caesar rose with fluid grace, as though he had been waiting for precisely this moment. "The esteemed Cotta speaks with the authority of experience, and no man here would discount the tragedy he describes. But I must ask the Senate to consider a practical matter." He spread his hands, the gesture of a reasonable man addressing reasonable colleagues. "The prophecy specifies 'before the summer stars rise.' We have, by the most generous calculation, nine weeks. Gnaeus Pompeius currently oversees the grain supply for Italia, commands the loyalty of six legions, and administers three provinces through his legates. If he departs—and I am not yet convinced he must—who assumes these responsibilities?"

"The Senate," Cato said flatly.

"The Senate is not a military commander. The Senate does not inspect granaries or negotiate with Sicilian merchants. If we exile Pompey without arranging for the transfer of his duties, we may satisfy the prophecy only to starve the city through our own incompetence." Caesar smiled, the expression that had charmed half the noble families in Rome. "I propose a compromise. Let us appoint a committee to determine the proper interpretation of 'son of the sea'—there are, after all, several possible candidates—and simultaneously prepare contingency measures for the transfer of Pompey's responsibilities should the committee conclude that he is indeed the subject of the prophecy. We can accomplish both within the nine weeks allotted."

"A committee," Cato repeated. "To interpret divine instruction."

"To implement it responsibly. The gods, I think, prefer competent obedience to hasty chaos."

Cato looked at the bronze cylinder in the Keeper's hands. The glow had not diminished. If anything, it seemed brighter than when the seal was broken, though that might have been his imagination, or his fear.

"I move," he said slowly, "that we vote on the question directly. Does the Senate believe Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus to be the subject of the Sibylline instruction? Those who vote yes commit to his exile within the prescribed period. Those who vote no accept responsibility for whatever consequences follow." He paused. "Let each man here decide how much he trusts his own interpretation—and how much he is willing to wager on it."

The Consul Metellus Celer looked profoundly unhappy, which was appropriate for a man caught between a divine ultimatum and the two most powerful men in Rome. "The question has been moved. Is there a second?"

"Seconded," said Cotta from the back benches.

"Then we shall vote. But first—" Metellus hesitated, glancing at the Keeper. "Is there precedent for requesting clarification from Cumae? Any procedure by which—"

"The Sibyl speaks when Apollo moves her," the Keeper said. "She does not take questions."

"No," Metellus murmured. "No, I suppose not."

The voting began. Cato watched his colleagues approach the urns, each man dropping his tablet into one vessel or the other, and tried to read the outcome in their faces. Some looked certain. Most looked afraid.

The glow from the bronze cylinder painted their shadows long against the marble walls, and outside, somewhere beyond the chamber's ancient stones, the Tiber flowed quietly toward the sea, remembering nothing.

Yet.
