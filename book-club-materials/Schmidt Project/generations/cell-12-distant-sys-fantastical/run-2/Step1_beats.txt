# Structural Beats for Original Scene: Roman Senate Debates Divine Oracle

## Premise Context
Classical Rome, 60 BCE — the gods demonstrably intervene in human affairs, and oracular pronouncements carry the weight of verified supernatural authority.

---

## To-Do List for Scene Structure

1. **Open with procedural tension disrupted by sacred obligation** — Begin mid-session with familiar Senate business (military appointments, provincial matters) interrupted by the formal presentation of the oracle's pronouncement, establishing that divine instruction legally supersedes normal debate yet still requires senatorial interpretation for implementation.

2. **Reveal the oracle's specific demand and its political impossibility** — Introduce the instruction itself (perhaps demanding a specific commander be recalled, a temple built on contested land, or a sacrifice that benefits one faction), making clear how divine will cuts across existing alliances, ambitions, and the fragile Caesar-Pompey-Crassus dynamic.

3. **Stage the interpretive battle through competing speeches** — Allow two or three senators to argue not whether to obey but how to obey, each proposing implementation that conveniently serves their faction while claiming pious fidelity, exposing how human politics persists even under divine certainty.

4. **Introduce the cost of defiance through historical memory** — Have a speaker invoke a specific past instance when Rome ignored or delayed divine instruction and suffered verifiable consequences, raising the stakes beyond political maneuvering to existential civic fear.

5. **Close on unresolved procedural deferral masking deeper crisis** — End with the Senate voting to consult further augurs or delay until additional signs appear, a technically pious postponement that satisfies no faction and leaves the divine demand hanging over Rome like a sword.

---

These beats maintain historical texture while exploring how demonstrable divine power complicates rather than simplifies human governance.
