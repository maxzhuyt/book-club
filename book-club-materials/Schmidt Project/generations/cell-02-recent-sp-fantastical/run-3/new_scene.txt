# The Broadcast

The bunker smelled of damp concrete and cigarette smoke, same as every night. Major Arthur Crane descended the seventeen steps with his evening coffee balanced in one hand, the saucer rattling against the cup in a way that would have mortified his mother. Two months ago he'd have steadied it. Now he didn't bother.

Corporal Westin snapped to attention at the security door.

"At ease. Anything unusual?"

"No sir. Quiet shift."

Quiet. As if the device ever made noise on its own. It simply sat there on its table in the converted storage room, looking like a field radio that had mated with a grandfather clock, all brass dials and vacuum tubes and that one component nobody could identify, the dark cylinder at its heart that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it.

Crane dismissed Westin and sealed the door behind him. He'd argued against the armed guard initially. Seemed theatrical. But Colonel Ashford had insisted, and Crane had learned not to argue with Ashford about the device. Not since Normandy. Not since they'd pulled it from that bunker outside Saint-Lô and Weber, the German technician they'd captured with it, had put a pistol in his mouth rather than explain what he'd helped build.

Crane settled into the wooden chair they'd requisitioned from a bombed-out schoolhouse and set his coffee aside. The logbook lay open on the table, his handwriting filling pages in neat columns. Date of access. Target date selected. Duration of signal. Content summary.

Two months of summaries. Two months of baseball scores and weather reports and soap advertisements and news broadcasts from times that hadn't happened yet. Intelligence had been skeptical at first. Then they'd tested it. Tuned to a date three days forward, listened to a BBC announcer read casualty figures from an operation that was still in planning stages. When those figures matched exactly, the skepticism evaporated. In its place came something worse. Hunger.

Now Crane received his target dates from London via encrypted cable. Once weekly, sometimes twice. Always specific. Always unexplained.

He checked tonight's orders. November 22, 1963.

Nineteen years from now. Further than they'd ever attempted. The device seemed to struggle with distance, the transmissions growing fainter and more distorted the further one reached. Anything past 1950 had been largely static and fragments. But orders were orders.

Crane adjusted the primary dial, watching the numbers roll past like a slot machine. The secondary dials he set by instinct now, finding the frequencies where voices tended to cluster. The BBC. American networks. The usual landmarks of the invisible future.

He depressed the activation switch. The dark cylinder at the device's heart began to hum, a sound felt more than heard, pressure behind the eyes.

Static. He adjusted the frequency. More static, then a squeal, then—

"—repeat, President Kennedy has been shot—"

Crane's hand froze on the dial.

"—motorcade in Dallas, Texas. The President was rushed to Parkland Memorial Hospital. We are awaiting confirmation—"

The voice was American, male, professional but shaken. Crane could hear something beneath the words he'd never encountered in these future broadcasts before. Genuine shock. The sound of a man delivering news he didn't want to believe.

"—Vice President Johnson was in the motorcade but is reported unharmed. Again, if you are just joining us, President John F. Kennedy has been shot while traveling through Dallas—"

Crane fumbled for his pencil, nearly knocking over his coffee. President Kennedy. He wrote the name carefully, then stopped. Kennedy. Irish, most likely. Catholic? An Irish Catholic president of the United States. The idea seemed almost as strange as the device itself.

"—Governor Connally of Texas also wounded. The President's wife was with him in the car. We have no information on her condition—"

Shot. A president shot. Crane thought of Lincoln, of Garfield, names from history books. This was different. This was a president who didn't exist yet, killed in a city Crane had never visited, in a year when he himself would be—he calculated quickly—fifty-one years old. If he survived the war. If any of them did.

"—crowds gathered outside the hospital. Dallas police are searching for the gunman—"

The transmission crackled, wavered. Crane adjusted the dial with desperate precision, chasing the signal.

"—Zapruder, a local businessman, may have captured the shooting on his personal film camera. Police are attempting to locate—"

The voice dissolved into static. Crane worked the dials for another fifteen minutes but couldn't recover it. The device did that sometimes, gave you a window and then shut it. Like tuning into a conversation at a party, then having someone close a door.

He sat back. His coffee had gone cold. His cigarette had burned down to the filter in the ashtray, unsmoked.

President Kennedy. November 22, 1963. Dallas. Parkland Memorial Hospital. Governor Connally. Zapruder.

He stared at the words in his logbook. They meant nothing. They were names without faces, places without context. A president he'd never heard of, shot in a city he'd never seen, two decades before it happened.

But London had requested this specific date. November 22, 1963. Not November 21st, not the 23rd. They'd known. Someone had known, or suspected, that this date mattered.

Crane closed the logbook.

Protocol demanded he cable his summary to Colonel Ashford immediately. Content summary. Significant events. Potential intelligence value. The same dry categories he'd used to describe stock prices and election results and the outcomes of battles not yet fought.

A president would be murdered. Was that intelligence? Could you warn a man who hadn't been born yet, about a death that hadn't happened, in a country still fighting a war that this broadcast suggested it would survive?

He looked at the device. The dark cylinder had gone dormant, silent, patient.

Crane had never questioned his orders regarding the target dates. He'd assumed London had reasons, strategies, ways of using this information that a field officer needn't understand. But sitting here in the bunker's damp silence, he wondered for the first time who had chosen this particular date. And why.

He opened the logbook again. Uncapped his pen.

Then, very carefully, he began to write a different summary than the one he'd heard.
