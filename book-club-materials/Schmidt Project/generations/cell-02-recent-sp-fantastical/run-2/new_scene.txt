# The Broadcast

The candle had burned to a stub by the time Major David Ashford finished his evening cigarette. He crushed it in the ashtray—a brass dish liberated from a bombed-out hotel in Caen—and regarded the device on his desk with the weary familiarity of a man looking at his wife of twenty years.

Two months. Sixty-three nights of sitting in this requisitioned farmhouse, spinning dials, pressing his ear to the speaker cloth, transcribing fragments of impossible futures. The initial terror had long since curdled into something like boredom.

He switched it on. The valves—if that's what they were—glowed their peculiar amber, a shade he'd never seen in any British or American equipment. The casing bore scorch marks from the firefight in the Harz facility, and three of the original markings had been scratched away by someone, perhaps whoever had decided this thing should never exist.

Static. The sound of distant centuries shuffling like cards.

He turned the primary dial, the one they'd labeled "TEMPORAL" in grease pencil for lack of any better term. The secondary dial—"FREQUENCY" seemed inadequate—he left alone. Random sampling. That was the protocol now. Cast a wide net.

A woman's voice emerged from the static with startling clarity.

"—anniversary commemorations continue throughout the week, with services planned at memorial sites across Europe and the Pacific. We go now to our correspondent in Hiroshima, where survivors and their descendants have gathered—"

Ashford's pencil moved automatically. *Hiroshima*. He knew it only as a point on a map, a Japanese city of middling strategic importance. Why would survivors gather there? Survivors of what?

"—seventy-nine years since the atomic attacks that ended the Second World War, and still the debate continues among historians about whether President Truman's decision—"

The transmission wavered, dissolved into hissing, then reconstituted.

"—estimated death toll of two hundred thousand, though some scholars argue—"

Ashford's pencil stopped. He looked at what he'd written. *Atomic attacks*. *Two hundred thousand*. *Ended the Second World War*.

The words sat on the page like strangers.

He knew about atomic research, of course. Knew the boffins were working on something in America, something that Churchill discussed in hushed tones with Roosevelt. But this—two hundred thousand dead from an attack on a single city? That wasn't a weapon. That was the wrath of God.

"—many in Japan still question why Nagasaki was targeted only three days later, when surrender negotiations were already—"

*Nagasaki*. He wrote it down. Another city he couldn't picture. Three days later. Two of these weapons deployed in the span of a week.

The woman's voice shifted into a lighter register, the verbal equivalent of turning a page.

"—in other news, veterans of the Normandy invasion gathered today at the American Cemetery in Colleville-sur-Mer, where over nine thousand servicemen remain buried. The youngest attendees, now in their late nineties, spoke of comrades who never—"

Late nineties. Ashford did the arithmetic slowly, not trusting himself. If a man landed at Normandy this past June at age twenty, he would be ninety-nine in... 2024. Seventy-nine years after the atomic attacks would place them in 1945.

Next year.

He stared at the device. The amber glow illuminated nothing.

"—President Harrison's remarks at the ceremony drew criticism from some who felt the comparison to current European tensions was inappropriate, though supporters praised—"

President Harrison. Ashford didn't recognize the name. He wrote it down anyway, in the margin, beside the other names he'd collected over the weeks. Names of leaders, of countries, of alliances that meant nothing to him. A vocabulary for a world that hadn't learned to speak yet.

The transmission degraded. The woman's voice stretched and yawned into frequencies beyond human hearing. He adjusted the dial, chasing her, but she was gone.

Ashford sat back. His cigarette case lay within reach, but he didn't take one. His eyes moved over the transcript, over the neat blocks of handwriting that recorded the unthinkable with clerical precision.

*Ended the Second World War*.

If this broadcast spoke true, the war would end next year. His war. The war that had consumed five years of his life, that had killed his brother at Dunkirk, that had reduced London to rubble and ash. It would end.

With two hundred thousand dead in cities he couldn't find on a map.

He should feel relief. He should feel something. Instead he felt only the particular numbness of a man handed an envelope he wasn't certain he wished to open.

The protocol was clear. Transcripts went to Colonel Whitmore in the morning. Whitmore decided what went further up the chain, to the shadowed men who never gave their names. Ashford was a filter, nothing more. A clerk with a security clearance and a device that whispered secrets from tomorrow.

He looked at the page again. *Atomic attacks. Hiroshima. Nagasaki. Two hundred thousand.*

Then he opened his desk drawer and removed the folder where he kept the transcripts that troubled him—the ones he reviewed twice before passing along, the ones that kept him awake. The folder was thicker than it had been in September.

He added tonight's pages to the stack, closed the folder, and returned it to the drawer.

In the morning he would give Whitmore the standard report. Fragments received. Nothing operationally significant. The war continued.

Ashford switched off the device. The amber faded. In the darkness of the farmhouse, surrounded by the distant rumble of artillery softened by miles of French countryside, he sat with his hands flat on the desk and tried to imagine a weapon that could kill two hundred thousand people.

He couldn't.

He reached for his cigarettes after all, and smoked two more before the candle finally guttered out.
