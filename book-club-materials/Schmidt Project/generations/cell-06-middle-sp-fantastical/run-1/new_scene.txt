# Chapter Seven

The fire had burned down to a steady glow, sufficient for the room but no longer commanding it. Reverend Josiah Whitmore sat at the desk his father had used for forty years before him, the surface scarred by decades of ink spills and the circular ghosts of coffee cups. His pen moved, then stopped. Moved again.

*We are told that eye hath not seen, nor ear heard—*

He crossed out *told* and wrote *assured* above it. Then crossed out *assured* and wrote *promised*. The nib hovered.

Across the room, in the leather chair that faced the cold window, Dr. Marcus Endicott turned a page in his notebook. The paper sound was crisp in the quiet study. He had arrived from Hartford three hours ago with a letter of introduction from the faculty at Yale Divinity School and a carpetbag that suggested he intended to stay longer than the afternoon. Whitmore had not asked how long. There had been so many visitors in the fourteen months since his return that he had stopped asking.

"You've chosen First Corinthians," Endicott said. Not quite a question.

"I return to it often."

"Paul's account of the resurrection body."

"Paul's *attempt* at an account." Whitmore set down his pen and examined his fingers, which were stained at the tips. He had developed a habit of pressing too hard. "He struggles more than people credit. Gives us metaphors rather than mechanisms. The seed that becomes the flower. The celestial versus the terrestrial."

Endicott made a note. His pencil was sharpened to a precise point, and he wrote in a hand so small and controlled that Whitmore could not read it from across the room. This was, he suspected, intentional.

"You find his struggles... sympathetic?"

"I find them honest."

The fire settled, sending up a brief spiral of sparks. Outside, February snow lay thick on the elm branches, and the late afternoon light came gray and even through the windows. Whitmore's study faced north. He had chosen it for that reason twenty years ago, preferring the consistency of indirect light for reading. Now he sometimes wondered if he had been avoiding something.

He returned to the sermon. The congregation expected him on Sunday as they expected him every Sunday, and Mrs. Alcott's boy was to be baptized, and there were announcements about the roof repair fund. The machinery of faith continued to require tending regardless of what its minister had seen in those eleven minutes when, by the sworn testimony of Dr. Edwin Cross and Dr. Samuel Barton and the four witnesses in the sickroom, his heart had produced no pulse and his lungs had drawn no breath.

*We are promised that eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, the things which God hath prepared—*

"Reverend Whitmore."

He looked up. Endicott had set aside his notebook and was leaning forward slightly, his elbows on his knees. He was perhaps fifty, with steel-colored whiskers and the pallid complexion of a man who spent his hours in libraries. His eyes, however, were unexpectedly warm. This was the thing Whitmore had not anticipated.

"I should tell you why I've come," Endicott said. "Not why the faculty sent me. Why I came."

"I assumed they were the same."

"They are not."

Whitmore waited. He had learned to wait. In the months after his return, he had spoken too quickly and too much, trying to fill every silence with description, as though repetition might construct a bridge between what he had experienced and what language could carry. He had learned that silence served better. Let the visitor arrive at his own purposes.

"My daughter," Endicott said. "Caroline. She died in the spring of sixty-seven. She was nine."

"I'm sorry."

"Diphtheria. It took her in six days." Endicott's voice was level, practiced. He had spoken these words before, Whitmore understood, many times, until they had worn smooth as river stones. "I sat with her at the end. I told her she was going to see the Lord. I told her there would be no pain and no fear and that her grandmother would be waiting."

"Yes."

"I told her these things, and I did not know if they were true. I believed them. I *wanted* them. But I did not *know*." Endicott picked up his notebook, then set it down again without looking at it. "You understand the distinction I'm drawing."

"I do."

"The faculty wishes me to examine your account for theological consistency. They are concerned about certain elements. The absence of explicit Christological imagery. The—I believe Dr. Harmon's phrase was 'troubling universalism' implied by your descriptions."

"I recall Dr. Harmon's letter."

"I am not here for that." Endicott met his eyes. "I am here because I told my daughter she would be met with love, and you have said there was love. I told her there would be light, and you have said there was light. I am here, Reverend Whitmore, because I want you to tell me that I did not lie to her."

The fire crackled. Somewhere in the house, Mrs. Phelps was preparing supper, and the faint smell of roasting meat drifted under the study door. Ordinary life, continuing. Whitmore looked down at his sermon, at the words *eye hath not seen*, and thought about the inadequacy of all seeing, all hearing, all human apparatus of perception.

"Dr. Endicott," he said slowly. "What I can tell you is what I experienced. What I cannot tell you—what I cannot tell anyone—is what my experience *means*."

"I'm not sure I understand."

"I have described a light. I have described a sense of presence. I have described the absence of fear and the presence of something I can only call welcome." He touched the edge of the page before him. "But I cannot promise you these were not the final firings of a dying brain. I cannot promise you they represent objective truth about the architecture of eternity. I was not given a tour, Dr. Endicott. I was not handed a map."

"The physicians—"

"The physicians attest that I displayed no vital signs for eleven minutes. They do not attest to the reality of what I perceived. They cannot."

Endicott was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke again, his voice had lost its scholarly distance. "Then what use is your testimony?"

Whitmore picked up his pen. He examined the nib, the small reservoir of ink, the simple mechanism by which thought became word became mark on page. An inadequate technology for an impossible task.

"I have asked myself that question every day for fourteen months," he said. "I have asked it while I write these sermons that dance around the center of what happened to me because I do not have words for the center. I have asked it while theologians send letters and physicians send letters and newspaper editors send letters and I answer them all with the same circling, inadequate language."

"And?"

"And I think perhaps the use is simply this." He looked at Endicott. "You told your daughter she would be met with love. You did not know. You believed and hoped and wanted, but you did not know."

"Yes."

"Neither did I, Dr. Endicott. Before. I preached resurrection for twenty-three years without knowing. I offered comfort at a hundred deathbeds without knowing." He set down the pen. "Now I have seen something. I cannot prove it was real. I cannot construct a theology from it that will satisfy your colleagues in Hartford. But I can tell you this: I no longer stand at deathbeds and offer comfort I am uncertain of. What I offer now is different."

"How is it different?"

"It is still uncertain. But the uncertainty has changed shape." He struggled for the words, as he always struggled. "Before, I wondered if there was anything at all. Now I wonder only if what I glimpsed was representative. Do you understand? The question has moved. It used to be *whether*. Now it is only *what* and *how* and *for whom*."

Endicott stared at him. The fire had burned lower still, and the room was growing dim. Neither man moved to light a lamp.

"That is not the same as proof," Endicott said finally.

"No."

"It is not even the same as faith. Not as we teach faith."

"No." Whitmore gestured at the page before him, at the half-written sermon with its crossings-out and its marginal queries. "And so I struggle, as Paul struggled. I reach for metaphors because the thing itself will not fit in my hands. The seed and the flower. The earthly house and the heavenly. I have spent a year trying to find better metaphors, and I have failed."

"Perhaps there are no better metaphors."

"Perhaps not."

Endicott picked up his notebook, looked at it, and then closed it without adding anything. He tucked his pencil into his coat pocket. When he spoke, his voice was rough.

"She asked me, at the end. Caroline. She said, 'Papa, will it hurt?' And I said no. I said it would be like falling asleep, and when she woke, she would be somewhere wonderful." He cleared his throat. "I do not know if it hurt. I was holding her hand and I do not know if those last moments were agony or peace. I will never know."

"No."

"But you have said—in your account, you said there was no pain."

"I experienced no pain."

"You said the transition was—I have your words here, I've read them so many times—you said it was 'as gentle as setting down a heavy burden.'"

"Yes."

"Did you mean it?"

Whitmore looked at this man, this stranger who had come from Hartford with his notebook and his sharpened pencil and his grief worn smooth by repetition. He thought about all the things he could not say, all the things he did not know, all the ways his eleven minutes might have been illusion or misperception or the desperate confabulations of oxygen-starved tissue.

"I meant it," he said. "Whatever it was—whether it was real in the way this desk is real, or real only in the way a dream is real—I experienced it as gentleness. I experienced it as homecoming. I cannot give you more than that."

Endicott nodded slowly. He did not speak.

"Will that be sufficient for your report?"

"I am not certain I will write a report."

"The faculty will expect one."

"The faculty expects many things." Endicott stood, moved to the window, looked out at the snow and the gray light and the bare branches of the elms. "They expect me to evaluate your account against doctrinal standards. They expect me to identify heresies or confirm orthodoxies. They have sent me as a kind of theological physician, to diagnose whether your experience is healthy or diseased."

"And?"

"And I find I am not interested in diagnosis." He turned from the window. "I am interested in what you just told me. That you no longer wonder *whether*. That the question has moved."

"That troubles them, I think. The faculty."

"It troubles everyone." Endicott almost smiled. "It is much easier to argue about whether heaven exists than to argue about its nature. The first question can be debated endlessly without risk. The second requires choosing. If heaven is real, then it is some particular way and not other ways, and our choices here might matter in specific rather than general terms."

Whitmore looked down at his sermon. *Eye hath not seen.* But his eye had seen something. That was the trouble. That was the splinter that would not work free.

"I should let you return to your work," Endicott said.

"Will you stay for supper? Mrs. Phelps sets a good table."

"I would be grateful."

Endicott moved toward the door, then paused. His hand rested on the frame, and he did not turn around.

"Reverend Whitmore. What you saw. The light, the presence, the welcome." His voice was very quiet. "Was there any sense of... of individuals? Of particular souls?"

"You're asking if I saw your daughter."

"I suppose I am."

Whitmore considered the question. He had been asked versions of it many times—by grieving parents, by widows, by children who had lost siblings. He had learned that no answer satisfied, that whatever he said would be heard through the filter of desperate need.

"I perceived presences," he said carefully. "I did not perceive faces or names. I cannot tell you that I encountered any specific person, because I did not experience persons as we understand them here. I experienced..." He paused, reaching again for words that would not come. "Communion. Welcome. Recognition without identification."

"I see."

"I'm sorry."

"No." Endicott turned finally, and his eyes were bright. "No, that is—that is something. Recognition without identification. I will think about that. I will think about what it might mean that the dead recognize us without needing to know our names."

He left the study. Whitmore heard his footsteps in the hall, then on the stairs as he went up to the guest room to wash before supper.

The minister sat alone with his half-written sermon. The fire had burned to embers now, and the room was cold enough that he could see his breath. He should add wood. He should light a lamp. He should finish the paragraph he had started, find a way to bridge Paul's ancient struggles to the needs of his congregation, to Mrs. Alcott's boy who would be held over the baptismal font and promised entry into a mystery that Whitmore had touched and still could not explain.

Instead he sat still, pen in hand, and looked at the words he had written.

*We are promised that eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, the things which God hath prepared—*

After a long moment, he crossed out *promised* and wrote *told*. Then crossed out *told* and wrote *given to believe*.

It was not right. None of it was right. But it was closer, perhaps, to the shape of what he did not know.

He began, again, to write.
