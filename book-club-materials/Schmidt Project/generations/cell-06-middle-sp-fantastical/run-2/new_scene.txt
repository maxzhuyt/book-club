# Chapter Seven

The pen had been still for some minutes now. Reverend Josiah Crane watched a droplet of ink gather at its nib, threatening the half-finished sentence beneath. He ought to blot it. He ought to do many things.

*And what shall we say of that country from which no traveler returns?*

The words sat on the page like an accusation. He had been that traveler. He had returned. And yet Sunday would arrive as it always did, and the congregation would sit in their pews with their hungry, frightened faces turned upward, and he would have to say something. Before the event—he still could not call it death, though the physicians insisted—sermons had come to him like water from a spring. Now he sat in his own study feeling like a man asked to describe color to the blind while doubting he had ever seen it himself.

Across the desk, Dr. Marcus Webb of the Andover faculty turned a page in his notebook. The soft sound landed in the silence like a stone in a well.

"You've stopped writing," Webb observed.

"An astute theological insight."

Webb smiled without warmth. He was a trim man of perhaps fifty, with the kind of neat gray beard that suggested disciplined habits of mind and body. His dark coat was brushed immaculate. On his lap sat a leather portfolio thick with papers, and Crane had watched him add to his notes throughout the afternoon—small, precise markings in a hand too controlled to read at this distance.

"I don't mean to disrupt your process," Webb said. "Though I confess I find it remarkable that you can work at all with a visitor present. I would find it impossible."

"One grows accustomed to observation."

This was not quite true. The year since his return had brought physicians, journalists, fellow ministers, and curiosity-seekers of every description to this room. He had grown not accustomed but resigned, which was a different thing. The study itself bore witness to the transformation. Where once a modest bookshelf had sufficed, towers of correspondence now colonized every surface—letters from grieving widows, from defiant atheists, from earnest young men at seminary, from a professor of mesmerism in Philadelphia who had written nine times without receiving a reply.

"Shall I withdraw for a time?"

"No." Crane set down the pen and rubbed his eyes. Through the window the October light was failing, the maples along Charter Street gone to rust and gold. "No, I find myself unable to continue regardless. The machinery has seized."

Webb leaned forward slightly. "May I ask what specifically troubles you? The sermon's theme, or—"

"Job." Crane gestured at the Bible open beside his papers. "The comforters. I am preaching on the comforters."

"Ah." Webb's pen moved. "And you find that text difficult now."

"I find it impossible. Eliphaz, Bildad, Zophar—they speak with such confidence about the architecture of divine justice. Who can read them without wincing? And yet I sat in this chair for nineteen years and preached with that same confidence. The same certainty that suffering had meaning, that the righteous would be rewarded, that I understood the shape of God's providence." He laughed, a dry and humorless sound. "What authority had I? What had I seen?"

"And now you have seen."

The words hung between them. Crane studied the theologian's face and found it carefully neutral, the expression of a man who had trained himself to receive information without revealing judgment.

"You have my testimony," Crane said. "You have Dr. Hartwell's attestation, and Dr. Pierce's. Four minutes without respiration or pulse. You have read the accounts."

"I have."

"Then you know what I claim to have seen."

Webb set down his pen and folded his hands over the portfolio. The gesture had a quality of ritual, a professor preparing to deliver a difficult assessment to a student.

"What you describe," he said slowly, "presents certain complications. You understand this. The landscape you report—the boundaries, the presences, the quality of light and sound—these do not map easily onto our eschatological traditions. Neither the Reformed consensus nor the older Catholic schemas quite accommodate your account."

"I am aware that I have proved a poor witness for established doctrine."

"That is not my concern. Doctrine must yield to truth, if truth can be established." Webb opened the portfolio and withdrew a particular sheaf of pages. Crane recognized his own handwriting—the formal deposition he had prepared for the Ministerial Association in February. "What concerns me is here, in your description of what you call 'the envelope.' You write that you perceived yourself surrounded by—I have your words—'a membrane of purpose, intelligent and entire, which knew me as a man knows his own hand.'"

Crane felt his throat tighten. Of all the things he had struggled to render in language, this had resisted most completely.

"Yes."

"You felt known."

"I was known. There was no separation between the knowing and the being known. I cannot explain it except through inadequate analogy. When you dream, Dr. Webb, you are both the actor in the dream and the author of it, though you forget this upon waking. It was something like that recognition—the discovery that one has always been held within a larger mind—except that I did not forget. I have not forgotten."

Webb made no note. His eyes remained fixed on Crane's face.

"And this mind," he said. "This intelligence you perceived. Did it communicate with you? Did it speak?"

"Not in words."

"Then how?"

The light through the window had grown amber and strange. Crane thought of Sarah in the kitchen below, preparing a supper he would barely touch. He thought of the congregation on Sunday, the Widow Pearson who had lost three children to diphtheria, old Samuel Trent who was dying by inches and knew it, young Abigail Foster who had confessed to him last month that she could no longer pray. What could he say to them? What right had he to stand in that pulpit and speak of comfort?

"It did not need to speak," he said at last. "I understood that I was temporary. That this—" he gestured at the room, the desk, his own body, "—all of this was temporary. A single breath in a conversation already underway. I understood that the conversation would continue and that I would continue within it, though not as this." He touched his chest. "Not as Josiah Crane of Salem. That was the mercy and the terror of it, Dr. Webb. To know that what I am is not what I will be. That what any of us are—"

He stopped. The words had run out again, as they always did when he approached the center.

Webb was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice had lost some of its careful neutrality.

"Dr. Pierce's report states that you showed no brain activity for the duration. No response to stimuli. By every clinical measure, you were deceased."

"Yes."

"And yet here you sit, composing sermons on Job."

"Failing to compose sermons on Job."

"The materialist interpretation—you are aware of it, of course. The brain in extremis produces hallucinations. Oxygen deprivation creates false experiences of transcendence. There is a literature developing."

"I have read Dr. Hammond's paper. And the German—Richter, I believe."

"What is your response to their position?"

Crane picked up the pen again, turned it in his fingers. The ink had dried on the nib. He would need to clean it before continuing, if he continued at all.

"My response," he said slowly, "is that I cannot argue with them. I cannot prove what I experienced. I cannot produce the membrane of purpose and display it for scientific examination. I can only tell you that I was dead, and that I was not alone, and that I am more certain of what I encountered in that country than I am of anything I see in this room. More certain than I am of you, Dr. Webb. More certain than I am of this desk, this pen, these inadequate words." He met the theologian's gaze. "And I can tell you that certainty is worthless. It convinces no one. It does not even comfort me."

The clock on the mantel chimed five. In the kitchen below, something clattered—Sarah dropping a pot, perhaps, or the servant girl fumbling with the stove.

"There is one thing more I must ask," Webb said. His voice had gone quiet, almost reluctant. "In your testimony, in all the interviews and depositions, you have not addressed it directly. Perhaps because no one has asked."

Crane waited.

"When you were there—in that country, within that membrane, known by that intelligence—" Webb paused, and Crane saw something flicker across the composed face, something that might have been fear or might have been longing. "Did you wish to return?"

The question opened a door Crane kept locked. Behind it lay the memory of Sarah's face when he woke, the tears streaking her cheeks, the way she had clutched his hand and thanked God aloud while he lay silent, unable to tell her that gratitude was not what he felt. That what he felt was closer to grief. That some part of him had wanted to stay in that place where he was known, where the questions that tormented him now did not exist because the answers were not separate from the asking.

"No," he said. "I did not wish to return."

Webb closed the portfolio.

"And yet you did."

"And yet I did." Crane looked down at the half-finished sermon, the failed sentence, the congregation waiting for words he could not find. "And now I must write about Job's comforters, and stand before people who are suffering, and tell them—"

He stopped.

"Tell them what?" Webb asked.

The room was growing dark. Soon Sarah would come to light the lamps, and he would have to compose his face into something she could bear, and the evening would proceed as evenings did. But for this moment the dusk held them both, and the question hung unanswered, and Crane felt the weight of every sermon he had yet to write, every grieving face he had yet to meet, every Sunday morning stretching out before him like a road with no visible end.

"I don't know," he said. "That is the trouble, Dr. Webb. I have seen what lies beyond that country from which no traveler returns. And I do not know what to tell them. I do not know if anything I say can matter. I only know that I must say something, and that the something will be inadequate, and that I must say it anyway."

Webb rose, gathering his portfolio and his hat. At the door of the study he paused.

"Your testimony is being read in seminaries from Boston to Edinburgh," he said. "There are men who believe it will transform our understanding of providence. There are men who believe it is the most dangerous heresy since Servetus. I am not yet certain which camp will claim me." He settled the hat on his head. "But I will tell you this. Whatever you say on Sunday—however inadequate you find it—there will be someone in that congregation for whom it is enough. Perhaps that is all any of us can offer. Inadequate words, in the direction of an adequate truth."

He left. Crane listened to his footsteps on the stairs, the murmur of voices as Sarah showed him out, the sound of the front door closing.

Then he cleaned the nib, dipped the pen in fresh ink, and crossed out the sentence he had begun.

*What shall we say*, he wrote instead, *of those who insist they understand our suffering?*

It was not a sermon yet. It was barely a beginning. But the pen was moving, and the page was filling, and somewhere in the text of Job a man sat in ashes and refused the comfort of friends who thought they knew the shape of God's justice.

Outside the window, the last light faded from the maples. Crane wrote on into the dark.
