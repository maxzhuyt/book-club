# Structural Beats: "The Mammoth Question"
**London Expedition Planning Meeting, 1872**

1. **Establish the Institutional Stakes** — Open with the meeting already in tension; the Natural History department faces pressure from rival European museums (Berlin, Paris) who are mounting their own Siberian expeditions, creating urgency around Britain's imperial scientific reputation.

2. **Introduce Competing Factions Through Debate** — Surface the central conflict through character positions: one faction argues for live capture and transport (spectacle, zoological gardens), another insists on specimen collection (scientific rigor, preservation), while a third raises ethical objections emerging from recent animal welfare movements.

3. **Complicate with Practical Realities** — Ground the fantastical premise in logistical authenticity; someone must raise the problems of Siberian permits from an uneasy Tsar, the challenge of keeping a mammoth alive through Baltic shipping routes, the astronomical costs, and the question of native Yakut cooperation.

4. **Plant the Human Story Within the Imperial Machine** — Introduce or reference the expedition's proposed leader, giving them a personal stake (redemption from a failed previous expedition, a scientific rival, a secret motivation) that will drive narrative beyond institutional concerns.

5. **End on a Provisional Decision with Embedded Conflict** — The committee votes or defers, but the resolution contains the seeds of future complications: funding is conditional, an unwanted member is imposed on the team, or troubling news arrives from a advance scout already in Irkutsk.
