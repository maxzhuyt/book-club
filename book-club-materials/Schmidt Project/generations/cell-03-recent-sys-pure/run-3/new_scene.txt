# The Neutral Victory

The teletype machines had been clattering since before dawn, and now at half past ten the newsroom of the *Boston Herald* smelled of cigarette smoke, cold coffee, and the peculiar electricity that accumulated when history arrived faster than men could type. Every few minutes another wire boy tore a fresh sheet from the Morse printer and ran it to the city desk, where it joined the drifts of paper already threatening to avalanche onto the floor.

Martin Carey stood at the window with his back to the chaos, watching the crowds gathering on Tremont Street below. Office girls streamed out of buildings. Someone had found a Union Jack and was waving it from a third-floor window across the way. He could hear car horns and what might have been church bells, though it was hard to tell through the glass.

"Marty." The voice belonged to Lou Phelps, the night editor who had refused to go home. "We need to make a decision."

Carey turned. The conference table was surrounded: Phelps with his loosened tie and the gray stubble of a man who'd been awake for twenty hours, young Connolly from the national desk, Helen Wardwell who handled cable news and had sources in London that put the wire services to shame, and at the far end, half-hidden behind a cloud of pipe smoke, old Saul Bernstein, who had been with the paper since before any of them were born.

"The first edition goes to press at noon," Phelps continued. "That's ninety minutes. We need a lead."

"The lead writes itself." Connolly was twenty-six and had the confidence of a man who had never been seriously wrong about anything. "VICTORY IN EUROPE. The war is over. Hitler is dead. The Reich has surrendered unconditionally. That's the story."

"Is it?" Helen Wardwell tapped her pencil against the table. She had pinned a small photograph above her desk three years ago, a snapshot of her younger brother in the uniform of the Royal Canadian Air Force. The photograph was still there. The brother was not. "Is that really our story? Britain's story, certainly. France's story, Russia's story. But ours?"

"We supplied half the steel that built those tanks," Connolly said. "Half the aluminum in those planes. American money won this war."

"American money." Saul Bernstein spoke for the first time. His voice was quiet, but the room went quieter still. "Yes. We were very generous with our money."

Connolly's jaw tightened. "What would you have us print, Saul? An apology?"

"I would have us print the truth." Bernstein removed his pipe and examined the bowl as though it contained something fascinating. "The war in Europe is over. That is true. The Allies have won. Also true. America watched." He looked up. "That is the truth as well, is it not? We watched. For six years we watched. We sold weapons to the British on credit and called ourselves neutral. We turned away ships full of refugees and called ourselves humane. Now the camps are liberated and the photographs are coming out and we will print them, and our readers will be horrified, and they will say how terrible, how could anyone let such a thing happen." He set the pipe down. "And we will not remind them that we let it happen. That Lindbergh let it happen. That we elected him to let it happen, twice, because he promised to keep our boys safe."

The silence that followed was the kind that has weight.

"That's not a newspaper headline," Connolly said finally. "That's an editorial. And an irresponsible one."

"The *New York Times* will lead with straight victory coverage," Phelps said, reaching for a cigarette. "So will the *Tribune*, the *Inquirer*, every paper in the country. If we go out there with something that reads like we're bitter, like we're second-guessing—"

"Like we have a conscience," Helen said.

"Like we're out of step with the public mood." Phelps lit his cigarette and exhaled a long stream toward the ceiling. "People want to celebrate. They've had six years of war news and rationing and worry. They want to feel like the good guys won."

"The good guys did win." Connolly spread his hands. "Look, I understand the sentiment, I do. But Lindbergh kept us out of a war that killed fifty million people. Fifty million. My father fought in the last one. He came back missing a lung and coughing blood for the rest of his life. I'm not going to apologize because this president decided American boys didn't need to die in European trenches again."

"Your father had a choice." Helen's voice was steady. "He enlisted. The people in those camps didn't choose anything. The children who—" She stopped. Took a breath. "There are photographs coming out of a place called Buchenwald. I've seen the descriptions. I haven't seen the photographs yet and I'm not certain I want to. Do you know what the liberating troops found?"

"Helen." Phelps held up a hand. "We all know. We've all read the wires."

"Then how do we print VICTORY IN EUROPE in letters three inches tall like we had anything to do with it? Like we didn't spend four years pretending it wasn't our concern?"

Martin Carey walked to the table and placed both hands flat on its surface. He had been editor-in-chief for eleven years. He had endorsed Lindbergh in 1940, as had nearly every paper in New England except the Jewish weeklies. He had genuinely believed the man would keep America safe. He had not thought very much about what America would be kept safe from, or who would pay the price of that safety.

"There's another problem," he said. "One we haven't addressed."

The room waited.

"The war in Europe is over. The war in the Pacific is not." He pulled a wire report from his jacket pocket and dropped it on the table. "The Joint Chiefs are briefing Congress this week. Invasion of the Japanese home islands. They're estimating a million American casualties. Maybe more."

Connolly went pale. "A million?"

"We stayed out of Europe. We were never going to stay out of the Pacific, not after they took the Philippines. Lindbergh knew it. Everyone knew it. We've been fighting our way across those islands for three years now and the worst is still ahead." Carey looked around the table. "So do we lead with European victory? With the camps? Or do we remind our readers that their sons and brothers and husbands are still dying in places they can't pronounce, and the war they actually care about is nowhere close to over?"

"You can't bury V-E Day below the fold," Phelps said. "It's the biggest story since Pearl—" He caught himself. Shook his head. "Since the fall of France."

"I'm not saying bury it. I'm saying we need to decide what this paper thinks this moment means." Carey straightened. "Is it a celebration? A reckoning? A warning? Because we can't do all three, and we have"—he checked his watch—"eighty-three minutes to choose."

Bernstein had picked up his pipe again. He was looking at the window, at the revelers visible on the street below. "In 1938," he said, "I wrote a letter to my cousin Yitzhak in Warsaw. I told him he should leave. I told him it was not safe, that there were programs available for refugees, that I would sponsor him and his family. He wrote back that I was being hysterical. That he had lived in Warsaw his whole life. That Poland was his home." He turned to face the room. "I never heard from him again. I have made inquiries through every agency I can find. No one has any record of him after 1942."

No one spoke.

"I do not know how to celebrate today," Bernstein continued. "Perhaps that is my limitation. Perhaps the public is wiser than I am and they understand something I do not. But if this paper prints a headline that says VICTORY IN EUROPE without any acknowledgment of what that victory cost, and who paid the price while we looked away—" He shook his head slowly. "Then I am not certain I can put my name on the masthead tomorrow."

"Saul." Phelps stubbed out his cigarette. "Nobody's saying we ignore—"

"What are we saying, Lou?" Helen leaned forward. "What's the headline? ALLIES WIN, AMERICA HELPED? EUROPE FREE, PACIFIC NEXT? What words do we use to describe a victory we refused to fight for?"

"How about EUROPE'S WAR ENDS." Connolly's voice was quiet now, the confidence drained out of it. "Factual. Neutral. Like us."

The bitter laugh came from Helen. "My God. You're actually serious."

"I don't know what I am." Connolly ran a hand through his hair. "I thought I knew how I felt about all this. My whole life my father told me about the trenches, about the gas, about watching his friends drown in mud. He made me promise I'd never let anyone talk me into fighting a European war. And I haven't. And now I'm looking at Saul and I'm thinking about those photographs coming out of Buchenwald and I don't—" He stopped. Swallowed. "I don't know what we should have done. I don't know what we should print. I just know we have to print something."

Martin Carey closed his eyes. He could hear the bells now, definitely bells, rising from the streets of Boston. In an hour the bars would open early and the celebrations would begin in earnest. By tonight there would be dancing in the Common and fireworks over the harbor. America would celebrate the end of a war it had declined to fight, and tomorrow morning his newspaper would be on every doorstep, telling people what it all meant.

He opened his eyes.

"Front page, above the fold: WAR ENDS IN EUROPE. Not victory. The war ends. Factual, like Connolly said. Below that, two columns. Left column: the surrender, the celebrations in London and Paris, the Allied triumph. Right column: the liberation of the camps, the photographs if we can get them cleared in time, the full scope of what was found. Equal weight. Equal space. The reader can decide which story they want to focus on."

"And the Pacific?" Phelps asked.

"Below the fold. PACIFIC WAR CONTINUES; INVASION PLANNING UNDERWAY. We don't bury it, but we don't let it overshadow. Today is about Europe, for better or worse."

"The editorial page," Helen said. "What do we say there?"

Carey looked at Bernstein. "Saul, I want you to write it. Whatever you think needs to be said. I won't cut a word."

The old man was quiet for a long moment. Then he nodded once, slowly, and reached for his pen.

"That's the decision then." Carey looked around the table. No one was smiling. No one looked satisfied. Outside, the bells kept ringing and the crowds kept cheering, and somewhere across an ocean the chimneys of Buchenwald had finally stopped smoking, and in the Pacific young men who had never heard of any of it were cleaning their rifles and writing letters home and trying not to think about the beaches ahead.

"Get to work," Carey said. "We go to press in seventy-five minutes."

The room emptied slowly. Helen paused at the door and looked back at him.

"It's not enough," she said. "What we're doing. It's not nearly enough."

"No," Carey agreed. "It isn't."

She left. He stood alone in the conference room, listening to the celebrations below, and thought about all the things that would never be enough, and the newspaper that had to be printed anyway, and the country that would read it tomorrow and feel whatever it was going to feel.

The teletype machines kept clattering. History kept arriving. And Martin Carey, who had endorsed Charles Lindbergh for president five years ago and would live with that decision for the rest of his life, went back to his desk to put out the morning edition.
