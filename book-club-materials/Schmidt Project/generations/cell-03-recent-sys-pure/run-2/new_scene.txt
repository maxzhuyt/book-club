# The War We Watched

The wire machine had been chattering for six hours straight and nobody had changed the paper roll. It spooled onto the floor now, accordion-folding against Tommy Flanagan's shoe, and still he didn't move. He stood reading the latest from Reims with a cigarette burning forgotten between his fingers.

"It's done," he said. "Jodl signed twenty minutes ago."

Arthur Kessler looked up from the layout table where he'd been standing for so long his lower back had fused into a single aching knot. Around him the newsroom of the *Boston Standard* held a moment of perfect stillness—twelve men and three women suspended between their telephones and typewriters, caught in the strange amber of history happening elsewhere.

Then Dickie Plowman whooped from the sports desk and the spell broke. Someone produced a bottle of Old Forester from a filing cabinet. Paper cups appeared. There was movement toward the windows overlooking Washington Street where, four floors below, car horns had begun their tinny chorus.

"To victory," Dickie said, raising his cup.

Arthur didn't reach for one. He watched the others drink, watched the performing of celebration, and thought that he had never seen joy sit so uneasily on human faces. They were toasting a war won five thousand miles away by other men's sons.

"We need the front page locked in forty minutes," he said.

The reminder landed like a stone in a pond. The little clusters of staffers began drifting back toward their desks, their cups still in hand, the holiday mood curdling into Monday obligation.

"I've got the mock-up here." Tommy crossed to the layout table and spread his draft across Arthur's street maps of liberated Paris, which had been sitting there since August awaiting some piece that justified their use. "AP photo of the signing above the fold, obviously. Eisenhower's statement runs down the left column. We've got reaction from Governor Tobin, from Cardinal Cushing. Celebration in London, celebration in Moscow."

Arthur studied the page. The headline stretched across all eight columns in the largest type they owned: **VICTORY IN EUROPE**.

"What about the subhead?"

"I was thinking 'Hitler's Thousand-Year Reich Dies at Twelve.'"

"That's good."

"I thought so."

Arthur pulled his reading glasses off and pressed his thumb and forefinger into the corners of his eyes. He had been awake for thirty-one hours. They all had. But it was not fatigue that made him hesitate.

"What else is there to say?" he asked.

Tommy's expression flickered with something Arthur couldn't quite read. "What do you mean?"

"I mean what else should the paper of record say on the day the war ends? What are we telling people?"

"We're telling them Germany surrendered. We're telling them the boys are coming home." Tommy paused. "The boys who went, anyway."

There it was. Arthur felt the sentence land in his chest and sit there. He looked around the newsroom at his staff bent over their typewriters, feeding paper into the machines, and he wondered how many of them were thinking the same thing. How many had brothers or cousins who'd gone to Canada in '41 and joined up there. How many had stopped writing letters to old addresses in Kraków or Vilna because the letters came back or, worse, didn't.

"I want to add a second subhead," Arthur said.

"There's no room."

"Make room."

Tommy's eyes narrowed. "What do you want it to say?"

Before Arthur could answer, the door to the editor's office swung open and Marshall Burke emerged. He was the *Standard*'s managing editor, fifteen years Arthur's senior, a man who had covered the last war from a trench in the Ardennes and come home with a piece of German steel in his hip that made him limp when the weather turned. He crossed the newsroom now with two cups of bourbon, handed one to Arthur, and looked down at the layout.

"Clean," he said. "Simple. I like it."

"Arthur wants to add something," Tommy said.

Marshall's gaze slid sideways. "Add what?"

Arthur realized he hadn't formulated the words yet, only the feeling. He reached for language the way he had been reaching for it in editorials for four years, always pulling back, always softening, always finding the euphemism that would let the paper speak without saying.

"I want to acknowledge what we didn't do."

The silence that followed had a different quality than the one that greeted the surrender news. This was the silence of men who understood each other too well.

"Arthur." Marshall's voice was gentle in a way that made Arthur's shoulders tighten. "It's V-E Day."

"I know what day it is."

"People want to celebrate."

"Do they? Or do they want us to give them permission to feel something they haven't earned?"

Tommy took a step back from the layout table, physically removing himself from the argument. Smart boy. He'd go far if he kept doing that.

"We stayed out," Arthur said. "We watched the whole thing from the cheap seats. Four years, Marshall. France fell and we watched. The Blitz and we watched. Stalingrad and we watched. And now it's over and we get to print 'Victory' in letters four inches tall like we had anything to do with it."

"We sent materiel. Lend-lease kept Britain alive."

"We sold them guns and collected the receipts. That's not the same thing and you know it."

Marshall set his cup down on the layout table, a careful gesture that told Arthur the man was working hard to keep his temper. They had known each other for twenty-two years. They had disagreed about a thousand things, from Roosevelt's court-packing to the designated hitter. But they had never disagreed about the war, because the war was the one subject neither of them could bear to discuss directly.

"What would you have the headline say?" Marshall asked. "Victory in Europe, No Thanks to Us?"

"I'd have it tell the truth."

"The truth." Marshall laughed, but there was no humor in it. "The truth is Lindbergh kept us out and most people were glad. The truth is we're the only industrial nation that didn't get bombed flat and that's going to make us the richest country in the history of the world. The truth is our boys are alive and their boys are dead and if you put that on the front page people will say thank God and turn to the sports section. Is that the truth you want to tell?"

Arthur said nothing. He was thinking about his brother Edward, who had joined the Royal Canadian Air Force in the summer of 1941, who had sent letters full of British slang and complaints about the food, who had died in a Lancaster bomber over a city Arthur couldn't even picture properly because he had never been to Germany, would probably never go.

"My sister-in-law got a telegram from the Air Ministry," he said quietly. "Not from our government. From theirs. Expressing condolences for the loss of Flight Lieutenant Edward Kessler in service to the Crown. She keeps it in a drawer because she doesn't know whether to be proud or ashamed."

Marshall's face changed. The combativeness drained out of it, replaced by something older and more tired. "Arthur. I'm sorry. I didn't know."

"I never told anyone here. What was there to say? He went and did what I couldn't convince this paper to support, and it got him killed, and I wrote editorials about the wisdom of neutrality while my brother burned alive over Dresden."

Tommy had retreated all the way to his desk. The staffers nearby were pretending very hard not to listen, their typewriters clacking at a performance of productivity.

"If we say something now," Marshall said, "if we publish some kind of mea culpa on the very day of victory, do you know what happens? We look like we're trying to take away people's happiness. We look like scolds. And Garrison will have our heads."

Garrison was the publisher. His family had owned the *Standard* since 1886 and his friends had dined with Lindbergh at the Copley Plaza.

"Garrison," Arthur repeated. "Yes. Garrison and his friends. The businessmen who made sure we never ran anything too critical of the America First position. The advertisers who threatened to pull out if we gave too much space to interventionist arguments. Four years of that, Marshall. Four years of trimming and hedging and calling it editorial judgment."

"That's not fair."

"Isn't it?"

The telephone on Marshall's desk began to ring. They both looked at it.

"That'll be Garrison now," Marshall said. "Wanting to know what the front page looks like. Wanting to make sure we strike the right tone."

"The right tone."

"You know what he means. Celebratory but dignified. Forward-looking. None of this dwelling on what might have been."

Arthur looked down at the mock-up again. **VICTORY IN EUROPE**. The words sat there in their massive black letters, asserting something that was technically true and spiritually false. We had not achieved victory. We had waited out the clock. We had placed bets on both sides and cashed our tickets at the window, and now we wanted to stand in the winner's circle and accept the garland.

"I had a letter from my cousin Esther," he said. "In 1941. She was in Warsaw. She was trying to get a visa, any visa, anywhere. She asked if I could help, if I knew anyone, if there was anything the paper could do. I wrote back and said I would try. I made some calls. I wrote a letter to our congressman. Nothing came of it. The quotas were what they were." He paused. "I stopped hearing from her in 1942."

Marshall closed his eyes. "Arthur."

"I don't know what happened to her. I have theories. Everyone has theories now. But I don't know, and I will never know, and tonight there will be parties in the streets and confetti and young people kissing strangers, and my cousin Esther is probably dead in a place I couldn't find on a map, and the front page of the *Boston Standard* will say 'Victory in Europe' as though we had any right to that word."

The phone was still ringing. Marshall ignored it.

"What do you want to do?" he asked quietly.

"I want to add a line. Below the subhead. Something that acknowledges—" Arthur stopped. The words wouldn't come. Four years of not saying this thing, and now that he had permission to say it, he found that the muscles of his mind had atrophied. "Something that acknowledges we have questions to answer. Debts to pay. I don't know how to say it. But the paper should try."

Marshall picked up his bourbon and drained it. He set the cup down next to the layout and stared at the headline for a long moment.

"Tommy," he called out. "Get over here."

Tommy approached warily, a man walking toward a dogfight.

"We're adding a line," Marshall said. "Below 'Hitler's Thousand-Year Reich Dies at Twelve.' Something about—" He looked at Arthur. "How would you phrase it?"

Arthur's throat felt thick. He had not expected Marshall to yield. He was not prepared.

"'As the world celebrates, America must ask what role it failed to play,'" he said. "Something like that. Something that opens the question without presuming to answer it."

"That's an editorial, not a headline."

"Then let's write the editorial."

"For tomorrow?"

"For tonight. Run it on page one, below the fold. 'The Victory We Watched.' 'The Price of Neutrality.' I don't care what we call it. But we should say something, Marshall. If we don't say it now, when everyone's looking at us, we'll never say it at all."

Tommy looked between them like a child watching his parents negotiate a peace he didn't understand. "The presses roll at midnight," he said. "That's—"

"Four hours." Arthur took off his jacket and draped it over a chair. "I've written editorials in less time than that. Get me coffee. Get me the wire reports on the camps. Get me everything we didn't print."

"Arthur." Marshall's voice had a warning in it. "Garrison will call. He'll want to see the page before we print."

"Then don't answer the phone."

"He'll come down here himself."

"Then lock the door."

Marshall laughed, a real laugh this time, with something wild in it. "You're going to get us both fired."

"Maybe. Or maybe we'll find out this city still has a few readers who want their newspaper to tell them the truth." Arthur sat down at the nearest typewriter and rolled a fresh sheet into the carriage. "Either way, we'll have said something. For once in four years, we'll have said something that mattered."

The phone stopped ringing. The newsroom was silent except for the wire machine, still chattering, still spilling its endless ribbon of history onto the floor. Outside, the car horns continued their celebration, and somewhere in the distance a church bell began to ring, and the presses waited in the basement like patient animals.

Arthur put his fingers on the keys.

*On the day the war in Europe ended*, he typed, *the* Boston Standard *owes its readers an apology. We have told you the news of this conflict faithfully and accurately for six years. What we have not told you is the truth about ourselves.*

He paused. He could feel Marshall reading over his shoulder, could feel Tommy hovering at the edge of his peripheral vision, could feel the weight of the headline waiting upstairs in the composing room.

*We stayed out*, he continued. *We had reasons that seemed good at the time, and we had fears that were real, and we had leaders who told us that American blood was too precious to spend on Europe's ancient quarrels. And so we watched. We watched from the safety of our neutrality while the world burned, and now that the fire is out we would like to join the celebration. But first we must say plainly what we failed to say for four years: we were wrong.*

Marshall's hand landed on his shoulder. Arthur stopped typing and looked up.

"Keep going," Marshall said. "I'll handle Garrison."

He walked toward his office, toward the telephone that had begun to ring again, and closed the door behind him. Through the glass, Arthur could see him pick up the receiver, could see his mouth moving, could see him shake his head.

Arthur turned back to the typewriter.

*We were wrong*, he typed again, and the words looked different now, heavier, more true. He thought about Edward's Lancaster falling through the dark sky over Dresden. He thought about Esther's letters from Warsaw, their handwriting growing more desperate with each envelope. He thought about all the editorials he hadn't written, all the headlines he'd softened, all the ways he had told himself that prudence was not the same as cowardice.

The bell on the wire machine dinged. Another dispatch from Europe, another piece of the story that would fill tomorrow's paper and the paper after that, the long reckoning that was only beginning.

Arthur kept typing. The presses would roll at midnight, and the city would have its celebration, but the front page of the *Boston Standard* would carry something else as well—a small crack in the edifice of national self-congratulation, a few hundred words that admitted what most papers would not.

It wasn't much. It wasn't nearly enough. But it was something, and in four years of saying nothing that mattered, Arthur had learned to value something.

He wrote until his fingers ached, and the coffee grew cold at his elbow, and the city's church bells rang their borrowed victory into the spring night.
