# The Third Shift

Margaret Holloway walked the inspection line with her clipboard pressed against her chest, the familiar weight of it steadied her as much as any ritual ever had. The morning shift wouldn't arrive for another hour, and the third shift workers moved like ghosts between the machinery, their faces grey with exhaustion and copper dust.

The furnaces at the far end of the building threw orange light across the concrete floor, catching the silver inlay of the primary binding circle. Someone had refreshed the salt lines recently—she could smell the purification still hanging in the air, that peculiar scent like lightning and chalk that she'd never been able to adequately describe to her sister in letters home.

She stopped at the first inspection station and lifted a shell casing from the output bin. Standard twenty-five pounder, brass gleaming dully under the work lights. Margaret turned it in her hands, checking the seams first—metallurgical before metaphysical, always, that was the order. The brass was good. Welsh copper, Cornish tin, proper alloy ratios. She scratched a small mark near the base with her inspection awl and watched how the metal responded. No brittleness, no hidden fractures singing back at her through the tool.

Then she closed her eyes and pressed her thumb to the casing's base where the primer would seat.

The binding felt intact. She sensed the familiar layers of intention worked into the metal during its forging—the targeting compulsions that would help the shell find flesh and bone, the secondary fragmentation charm that ensured the shrapnel would resist medical extraction. Standard issue. Unpleasant to touch, if she was honest with herself, but war was unpleasant and Margaret had long since made peace with her role in it.

She set the shell down and marked her clipboard. Batch 7445-C, acceptable. Moved on.

The floor supervisor found her at the third station. Doris Blackwood was a heavyset woman in her fifties whose family had worked metal in Birmingham for four generations, though none of them had ever imagined working it quite like this. She had chalk dust in the creases of her knuckles and shadows carved beneath her eyes.

"Mrs. Holloway." Doris's voice was carefully neutral. "Didn't expect you until seven."

"Couldn't sleep." Margaret lifted another shell casing, began her examination. "Thought I'd get ahead. How was the night's run?"

"Quota and a half. Minister's pushing for more output before the spring offensive."

"Minister's not the one whose hands shake after eight hours of inscription work." Margaret set the shell down. This one was good too. She was almost disappointed. "Your engravers holding up?"

"Well enough."

There was something in the way Doris said it. Margaret had learned to listen for those small hesitations, the places where words carried more weight than their syllables suggested. She made another mark on her clipboard without looking down at it.

"Show me the enchantment line."

The enchantment line ran along the factory's eastern wall, separated from the main floor by a shoulder-high barrier of cold iron. This was where the real work happened—where plain ammunition became something more. Eight stations, eight engravers, each one responsible for inscribing the microscopic bind-marks that turned simple shells into instruments of magical violence.

At half five in the morning, only three stations were occupied. The engravers sat hunched over their workbenches, magnification lenses strapped to their faces, their styluses moving in patterns too small for the naked eye to follow. The air here tasted different. Thicker. Margaret's teeth ached with proximity to so much accumulated intention.

"We're down five engravers?" she asked.

"Rotation. They work four hours on, four off. Otherwise the bleed-through gets dangerous."

Margaret nodded. She knew the regulations—she'd helped write half of them after the Leeds incident in '39, when an exhausted engraver had accidentally bound a piece of his own soul into a mortar shell. The resulting explosion had killed twelve people and left a wound in the world that still wept during thunderstorms.

She walked along the barrier, observing each engraver in turn. Mrs. Chen at station two, her stylus moving in precise spirals. Young Thomas Aldridge at station five, barely eighteen and already developing the permanent squint that marked their profession. And at station seven—

Margaret stopped.

The woman at station seven was working too fast. Her stylus danced across the shell casing with a fluid certainty that should have been impossible at this hour of the morning, after a full shift of delicate work. Margaret watched her complete an inscription in the time it would take a normal engraver to finish half of one.

"Who is that?"

Doris appeared at her shoulder. "Vera Malik. Transferred in three weeks ago from the Coventry plant, after the bombing."

"Her output?"

"Triple the others. Maybe more."

Margaret felt something cold settle in her stomach. She'd seen this before—workers who'd found shortcuts, who'd discovered that you could skip certain steps in the binding process if you were willing to accept the consequences. Sometimes the consequences were immediate. Sometimes they waited. Sometimes they followed the shells all the way to their targets and manifested there instead, turning straightforward kills into something far worse.

"I need to inspect her work directly."

She didn't wait for Doris's response. Margaret opened the gate in the cold iron barrier and walked to station seven, her clipboard forgotten in her hand.

Vera Malik didn't look up as Margaret approached. Up close, she was younger than Margaret had expected—late twenties, perhaps—with dark hair pinned back beneath a factory kerchief and hands that moved with eerie, mechanical precision. Her stylus never hesitated, never wavered. The inscriptions flowed from its tip like water finding its natural course downhill.

"Mrs. Malik."

The stylus stopped. Vera looked up, and Margaret saw that her eyes were wrong. Not dramatically so—nothing that would show up in a medical examination—but there was a quality to her gaze that made Margaret think of mirrors reflecting mirrors, depth suggesting more depth beneath.

"Inspector." Vera's voice was pleasant and empty. "Is there a problem?"

"I'd like to examine your work."

"Of course."

Vera set down her stylus and pushed her current project—a shell casing half-covered in spiraling inscriptions—toward Margaret. Then she folded her hands in her lap and waited with a patience that felt rehearsed.

Margaret picked up the shell. The metal was warm from handling, and the inscriptions were flawless. She could see that even without her instruments—each line perfect, each curve precise, the kind of work that master engravers produced on their best days after decades of practice. She pressed her thumb to the base and reached for the binding underneath.

What she found made her breath catch.

The standard enchantments were there. Targeting, fragmentation, all the ugly necessities of modern magical warfare. But beneath them, woven through them like threads of ice through water, was something else. A pattern she didn't recognize. A purpose she couldn't read.

Margaret set the shell down very carefully.

"Where did you learn to do this?"

Vera smiled. It didn't reach her eyes—but then, Margaret suspected very little reached those eyes anymore.

"The Coventry plant had excellent training programs."

"Coventry didn't teach this. I've inspected Coventry's output. I know their methods." Margaret kept her voice level with an effort. "This is something else. Something additional."

"Is the work satisfactory? I've exceeded all quotas."

"That's not the question I'm asking."

A long moment passed. Around them, the factory hummed and clanked, the ordinary sounds of industrial production continuing as though nothing was wrong. Mrs. Chen's stylus scratched steadily at station two. Thomas Aldridge coughed quietly and kept working. The furnaces roared in the distance.

Vera's smile faded. When she spoke again, her voice had changed—still pleasant, but with something harder underneath.

"The shells will do what shells are meant to do, Inspector. They'll find their targets. They'll kill Germans. What does it matter if they do something else as well?"

"What else?"

"Something that will end the war faster."

Margaret thought about the reports from the Eastern Front. The rumors of Russian experimental battalions, of German sorcerers who'd traded pieces of themselves for power, of weapons that left wounds in reality itself. She thought about Leeds, and the way the sky still screamed there when the wind blew from the east.

"Who taught you this? Who's behind it?"

"Does it matter?" Vera's hands unfolded from her lap. Her fingers were steady—no tremor, no shake, no sign of the exhaustion that should have claimed her hours ago. "We're losing, Inspector. Everyone knows it, even if no one says it. The Germans have capabilities we can't match. Unless someone changes the rules."

"And you're changing the rules."

"I'm making shells. You're the one who decides what happens next."

Margaret looked down at her clipboard. The checklist was straightforward: metallurgical quality, ritual integrity, output quantity, worker safety. Four categories, four boxes to tick, a system designed to catch defective ammunition and ensure consistent production. Nothing in the regulations covered this. Nothing in her training had prepared her for a shell that was perfect and wrong at the same time.

She could report it. Flag the entire night's output, call in the Ministry inspectors, watch them dismantle Vera's work and probably disappear the woman herself into one of those quiet facilities where inconvenient magic-workers went to answer questions they couldn't refuse. The war effort would lose three weeks of production while new engravers were trained. More British soldiers would die in the interim, killed by German shells that carried no secret additions, no hidden purpose.

Or she could mark the batch acceptable and walk away. Let whatever Vera had woven into the metal find its way to the front. Trust that "something that will end the war faster" was worth the cost of not knowing what that something was.

Margaret thought about her nephew David, eighteen years old and somewhere in North Africa with a rifle in his hands and ordinary ammunition in his pack. She thought about the letters he sent home, carefully cheerful, carefully vague about everything that mattered. She thought about her sister's face when she read them.

She thought about what she was willing to accept, and what she was willing to become, and whether there was any meaningful difference anymore.

The furnaces roared. The engravers worked. The night shift shuffled toward its end, and somewhere across the Channel, German factories were producing their own shells, their own horrors, their own desperate innovations.

Margaret Holloway looked at her clipboard, and made her choice, and marked the box.
