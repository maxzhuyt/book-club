# The Directive

## Berlin, December 1942

---

The summons had arrived at half past seven, while Kriminalinspektor Paul Metzger was still scraping frost from his kitchen window. A motorcycle courier, breath steaming in the early darkness, had waited exactly long enough to confirm Metzger's signature before disappearing into the grey murk of Friedrichshain.

Now Metzger stood in an anteroom on Prinz-Albrecht-Straße, his hat in his hands, watching a secretary type with mechanical precision. She had not acknowledged him beyond an initial nod toward a wooden chair. He had not sat. Twenty minutes now. His feet ached from the cold walk and his lower back announced its familiar complaint, but one did not sit without invitation in this building. Not at his level.

He studied the door to the inner office. Oak, heavy, with brass fittings polished to a military shine. Somewhere beyond it, decisions were being made about his immediate future. Perhaps about whether he would have a future at all.

Metzger was not an anxious man by temperament. Twenty-three years with the Kripo had weathered him against most provocations. But Prinz-Albrecht-Straße was different. The Reich Security Main Office operated by its own physics. Men entered with careers and exited without them. Sometimes they did not exit at all.

He inventoried the room to calm himself. Two filing cabinets, one slightly newer than the other. A portrait of the Führer in the standard heroic pose, somewhat larger than regulation required. A smaller photograph beside it—Heydrich in SS black, his pale eyes finding the camera with an expression of faint amusement, as though he had just finished telling a joke whose punchline remained private. Metzger looked away from it.

The typing stopped.

"Kriminalinspektor." The secretary's voice was colorless. "You may enter."

---

The inner office was smaller than Metzger had expected. A desk, two chairs for visitors, bookshelves bearing legal volumes and bound files. Winter light fell weakly through gauze curtains. The room smelled of cigarette smoke and, beneath it, something chemical—cleaning solvent, perhaps, or developing fluid.

The man behind the desk did not rise. SS-Sturmbannführer. The rank insignia registered before the face did. Mid-forties, Metzger guessed, with the lean features and receding hairline of a career bureaucrat. Wire-rimmed spectacles. A fountain pen held between two fingers like a conductor's baton.

"Metzger." Not a question. The Sturmbannführer consulted a file open before him. "Paul Friedrich. Born Düsseldorf, 1899. Kripo since 1919. Currently assigned to Abteilung Five, serious crimes. Divorced. One daughter, deceased. Son in the Wehrmacht, currently posted to—" He turned a page. "—Stalingrad."

Metzger said nothing. His son's location was not information he had shared with anyone.

"Sit."

He sat. The chair was hard and slightly too low, positioning him at a disadvantage. Intentional, certainly.

"You are wondering why you are here."

"Yes, Sturmbannführer."

The pen tapped twice against the desk. "The Reichsführer-SS has taken personal interest in a matter requiring investigation. Your name was put forward."

"I'm honored."

"You are not honored. You are selected." The correction carried no particular malice. "There is a difference. Honor implies you have done something to deserve attention. Selection implies only that you possess certain useful qualities."

"May I ask what qualities, Sturmbannführer?"

"You may ask." A thin smile. "You are competent. Your case closure rate is above average. You have no significant political affiliations, which in present circumstances is preferable to having the wrong ones. And you are—" The pen circled vaguely in the air. "—suitably obscure. If you fail, no one of consequence will notice. If you succeed, credit may be distributed appropriately."

Metzger absorbed this. He was, it seemed, expendable enough to be trusted.

"The matter concerns a death," the Sturmbannführer continued. "A body discovered four days ago in Grunewald. The forest, not the district. Male, approximately sixty years of age. No identification documents. Cause of death appears to be exposure, possibly combined with cardiac failure."

"Grunewald falls within my section's jurisdiction," Metzger said carefully. "I was not informed of any such discovery."

"No. You were not." The Sturmbannführer turned another page in the file. "The body was found by a forestry worker and reported to the Orpo. A patrol officer made an initial assessment and filed a standard report. That report was intercepted before it reached your office."

"By whom?"

The pale eyes lifted from the file. "By the appropriate authorities. The question is not relevant to your assignment."

Metzger felt the familiar tightening in his chest. The space between what he was being told and what he was being permitted to know—that space contained the danger.

"What is my assignment, Sturmbannführer?"

"You will identify the deceased. You will establish his movements during the seventy-two hours preceding his death. You will determine whether any criminal act contributed to that death. And you will report your findings exclusively to this office."

"Not to my own superiors?"

"Your superiors have been informed that you are seconded to a special investigation. They will not expect reports, and you will not provide them." The Sturmbannführer extracted a single sheet of paper from the file and turned it to face Metzger. "This document confirms your temporary authority. You will note that it permits you to requisition files, compel interviews, and enter premises as necessary for your investigation. You will also note the signatures."

Metzger leaned forward. The letterhead bore the seal of the Reich Security Main Office. Below the typed text were two signatures. The first he did not recognize. The second was a name he had seen only in newspapers and official communications, rendered in a slanted, precise hand.

*R. Heydrich.*

"The Reichsführer-SS," the Sturmbannführer said quietly, "does not often attach his name to investigative authorizations. When he does, it is because he wishes the investigation to succeed. And also because he wishes to know precisely who is responsible if it does not."

Metzger stared at the signature. His mouth had gone dry.

"There are conditions," the Sturmbannführer continued. "You will not discuss this matter with colleagues, family, or any person not directly connected to your inquiries. You will not commit findings to writing except in reports submitted to this office. You will not contact or interview any individual whose name appears on this list—" He produced a second sheet, this one bearing perhaps a dozen typed names. "—without prior written approval from me."

Metzger scanned the names. Most meant nothing to him. But one, near the bottom, caught his eye. He had seen it recently, though he could not immediately place where.

"You recognize someone."

It was not a question. Metzger looked up. The Sturmbannführer was watching him with the expression of a man who already knew the answer.

"The name Brenner," Metzger said slowly. "I have encountered it. In what context, I cannot—"

"You will remember eventually. When you do, you will also remember that Doktor Brenner is not to be approached." The Sturmbannführer retrieved the paper, aligning it precisely with the authorization. "These documents will remain in my possession. You will work from memory."

"That is—" Metzger stopped himself.

"Unusual? Yes. These are unusual circumstances, Kriminalinspektor. The Reichsführer is not a man who tolerates loose papers. Everything you learn in this investigation exists only in your head until you report it to me. This is for your protection as much as ours."

The logic was impeccable and entirely false. Metzger understood this clearly. Nothing about this arrangement was designed for his protection.

"You mentioned a deadline," he said. "The documents specify that the investigation must be concluded by—"

"January twentieth." The Sturmbannführer capped his pen and placed it beside the file with geometric precision. "Five weeks. The date is not arbitrary, but its significance need not concern you. What concerns you is only that the Reichsführer expects results by that date. Comprehensive results."

January twentieth. Metzger filed the date away. It would mean something, and discovering what might be the only leverage he would ever possess in this room.

"I understand, Sturmbannführer."

"Do you?" The thin smile returned. "I wonder. You are a policeman, Metzger. A good one, by the evidence of your file. Good policemen ask questions. They follow evidence. They pursue truth." He pronounced the last word with a slight emphasis, as though it were a technical term requiring definition. "In this investigation, you will do all of these things. But you will also understand that some truths are not yours to possess. You will find the edges of this matter, and when you reach them, you will stop. The Reichsführer wishes to know certain things. He does not wish to know everything. Your task is to understand the difference."

"And if I cannot?"

"Then you will have ceased to be useful." The Sturmbannführer opened a drawer and produced a small envelope, which he slid across the desk. "Credentials confirming your temporary status. A requisition authorization for a vehicle. The address where the body is currently being held. You will begin today."

Metzger took the envelope. It was lighter than he expected, as though its contents were provisional, easily revoked.

"One final matter." The Sturmbannführer had risen. The interview was ending. "The body in the forest was wearing a coat. In the pocket of that coat was a photograph. A woman and two children. The photograph is not among the evidence you will be given access to, because it is no longer considered evidence. But I mention it because you should understand something about this investigation from the beginning."

He moved to the window, his back to Metzger, silhouetted against the winter light.

"The woman in the photograph is alive. The children are alive. They reside currently at an address in Charlottenburg. The Reichsführer is aware of their existence. He has not ordered any action regarding them. This restraint is, you might say, conditional."

Metzger stood. His legs felt distant, mechanical. "Conditional on what?"

"On the investigation proceeding correctly." The Sturmbannführer did not turn. "On the right questions being asked. On the wrong ones being avoided. Do you understand, Kriminalinspektor?"

The silence in the room had weight. Metzger could hear his own breathing, the distant clatter of the secretary's typewriter resuming beyond the oak door.

"I understand," he said.

"Good." Now the Sturmbannführer turned, and his expression was almost pleasant. "Then we have an agreement. Heil Hitler."

"Heil Hitler."

Metzger walked to the door. His hand was on the brass handle when the voice reached him again.

"The woman's name is Brenner. Margarete Brenner. The children are six and four. I mention this only so that you have complete information."

Metzger did not turn around. He opened the door, passed through the anteroom where the secretary continued typing without acknowledgment, and descended the stairs to the street.

Outside, Berlin was grey and bitter. The first snow of December had turned to slush overnight, and the gutters ran with black water. Metzger stood on the pavement, the envelope in his pocket, and breathed the cold air until his thoughts slowed.

*Brenner.* The name on the list. The name he was forbidden to approach. And now a woman and two children in Charlottenburg, their safety conditional on his compliance.

He had agreed. He had signed nothing, but he had agreed. In that room, with that signature on the authorization, agreement was the only possible response. And now he was bound to an investigation whose boundaries had been drawn before he entered the building, by men who knew exactly what they expected him to find.

Or not find.

Metzger began walking. The address for the morgue was in Moabit. An hour's walk in the cold, but he needed the time to think.

*January twentieth.* Five weeks. The date repeated in his mind, seeking connection. Something was scheduled for that day, something significant enough to constrain an investigation ordered by the Reichsführer-SS himself.

And somewhere in Grunewald, a dead man lay waiting to be named, while in Charlottenburg, a woman named Margarete Brenner went about her morning with her children, unaware that her continued existence had become a variable in an equation Paul Metzger did not yet understand.

He turned up his collar against the wind and walked faster.
