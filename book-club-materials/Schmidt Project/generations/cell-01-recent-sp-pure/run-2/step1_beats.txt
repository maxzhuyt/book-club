# Structural Beats for Original Scene: The Directive

## Setting: Berlin, December 1942 — Heydrich's Office Issues Orders

---

## 5-Point To-Do List

1. **Establish the Investigator's Vulnerable Position** — Open with the protagonist receiving the summons, using their journey to (or arrival at) the meeting location to convey their middling status within the hierarchy. Show through internal reaction and physical details (adjusting uniform, rehearsing phrases, noting exits) that an audience connected to the Reichsführer-SS represents both career opportunity and mortal danger.

2. **Characterize Heydrich's Presence Through Proxy** — Rather than meeting Heydrich directly, have the directive delivered by a trusted subordinate who channels the Reichsführer's authority. This intermediary should embody Heydrich's reputation—cold precision, intellectual superiority, implied violence—while establishing that the investigator is being watched and evaluated even now.

3. **Deliver the Directive with Strategic Ambiguity** — Present the assignment with surface legitimacy (investigating a death, locating a missing person, verifying credentials) while layering in details that unsettle: unusual jurisdictional authority granted, specific individuals placed beyond questioning, a deadline tied to an unnamed upcoming event. The investigator should recognize gaps they dare not probe.

4. **Force a Compromising Agreement** — Require the investigator to verbally accept terms or sign documentation that implicates them in the assignment's outcome, eliminating any future possibility of claiming ignorance or reluctance. This creates immediate personal stakes beyond professional duty.

5. **Plant the First Moral Fracture** — End with a small detail that troubles the investigator's conscience—a familiar name, a reference to children, a location they recognize—seeding doubt about what they have agreed to pursue and establishing the internal conflict that will drive subsequent scenes.
