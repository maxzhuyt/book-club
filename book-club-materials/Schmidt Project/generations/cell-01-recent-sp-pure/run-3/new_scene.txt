# The Directive

## Berlin, December 1942

Kriminalinspektor Werner Ehlers was reviewing the Spandau warehouse burglaries when Feldmann appeared in his doorway. The younger detective's face had gone the color of old bread.

"There's a man from Prinz-Albrecht-Straße downstairs."

Ehlers set down his pen. The address required no elaboration. Eight months ago, Heydrich had returned from Prague with Czech bullets still lodged near his spine. The surgeons had worked fourteen hours. Since then, the Reichsführer-SS had consolidated his recovery into something the whisper networks called *die neue Ordnung*—the new order within the new order. His office now cast a shadow that made the Gestapo of old seem almost quaint.

"Did he give a name?"

"Sturmbannführer Lange. He asked for you specifically."

Ehlers examined this information the way he might examine a suspicious package. In seventeen years with the Kripo, he had maintained careful anonymity. He solved cases. He filed reports. He did not attract the attention of Prinz-Albrecht-Straße.

"Send him up."

Feldmann vanished. Ehlers used the brief interval to lock the Spandau file in his desk and remove a half-eaten sandwich from his blotter. The gesture felt absurd even as he performed it—tidying his modest office for a representative of an organization that tidied entire populations.

The man who entered wore the black uniform with the ease of someone born to it. Lange was perhaps forty, with a long pale face and the attentive stillness of a physician listening for an irregular heartbeat. He carried a leather document case but made no move to open it.

"Kriminalinspektor Ehlers. Thank you for receiving me without appointment."

The courtesy was itself a message. Men from Prinz-Albrecht-Straße did not require appointments, and both of them knew it.

"Please sit, Sturmbannführer. May I offer you coffee? I should warn you it's barely worthy of the name."

"I won't be staying long." Lange remained standing, positioning himself near the window where gray December light fell across the rooftops of Alexanderplatz. "You investigated the Kranzler matter in September, yes? The accountant found in the Landwehr Canal."

Ehlers felt the conversation shift beneath him like ice on a winter lake. The Kranzler case had been routine: an embezzler who owed money to unforgiving people, weighted with bricks, three weeks in the water. He had closed it in six days.

"I did."

"Your report was thorough. The Reichsführer's office appreciated its clarity." Lange turned from the window. "We have a similar matter requiring attention. A body discovered this morning in Grunewald. The circumstances are delicate."

"Grunewald is outside my usual district."

"Your usual district is wherever we require it to be."

The words carried no particular menace. They didn't need to. Ehlers waited.

Lange finally opened his document case and withdrew a single photograph, which he placed on the desk with the care of a man handling something radioactive. Ehlers looked down at the image: a corpse in winter woods, male, well-dressed, face obscured by what appeared to be a contact wound to the temple.

"Suicide?"

"That is what you will determine." Lange's tone suggested the determination had already been made elsewhere. "The deceased is Friedrich Tessmann."

The name meant nothing. Ehlers kept his expression neutral, but Lange read the ignorance anyway.

"Ministerialdirektor Tessmann. He served in the Reich Main Security Office. Section IV B 4."

Section IV B 4. Ehlers knew the designation. Everyone in Berlin's investigative services knew it, even if they pretended otherwise. Jewish Affairs and Evacuation. Eichmann's domain—though Eichmann himself had become merely a functionary since Heydrich's return, since the Reichsführer had begun consolidating all such operations under his personal oversight.

A senior official from that section, dead in the forest, and Prinz-Albrecht-Straße wanted a Kripo detective to call it suicide.

"I see," Ehlers said, though he did not see at all.

"You will conduct a thorough investigation. You will interview witnesses, examine evidence, and produce a comprehensive report. The Reichsführer's office expects nothing less than complete professionalism." Lange paused. "You will also coordinate your findings with my office before filing any official documentation."

There it was. The box drawn around him, elegant and inescapable.

"And if my investigation suggests conclusions other than suicide?"

Lange smiled. It was not a pleasant expression. "Then I'm confident you will investigate further until the evidence clarifies itself. Ministerialdirektor Tessmann was under considerable strain. The demands of his work were significant. It would not be surprising if such strain produced a tragic outcome."

"And if there are witnesses who saw something inconsistent with that outcome?"

"Then you will interview them thoroughly. Document their statements. Assess their reliability." Lange buttoned his coat. "Witnesses in times of war often suffer from confusion. Shock. Misremembered details. I trust your experience will help you evaluate such testimony appropriately."

He placed a card on the desk beside the photograph. A telephone number, nothing else.

"You will contact me directly with any developments. Not your superiors here. Not the Gestapo district office. Me." Lange moved toward the door, then paused. "The Reichsführer takes personal interest in matters affecting his former colleagues. He will be grateful for a resolution that honors Ministerialdirektor Tessmann's service to the Reich."

"And if I am unable to provide such a resolution?"

Lange considered the question as though it were purely hypothetical. "The Reichsführer's gratitude is considerable, Kriminalinspektor. His disappointment is equally so. I'm certain a man of your experience understands the distinction."

He left without waiting for a response.

Ehlers sat motionless for a long moment. Outside his window, Berlin continued its wartime rhythms: the rumble of military vehicles, the distant wail of a siren, the muted conversation of pedestrians hurrying through the cold. Ordinary sounds. The ordinary machinery of a city that had learned to function around the extraordinary.

He picked up the photograph again. Tessmann's body lay in a clearing, snow disturbed around it, right hand extended toward something beyond the frame. The contact wound was precise. Professional, almost.

Section IV B 4.

A man who knew things. Who had seen things. Who had perhaps, in the end, known or seen too much.

Ehlers understood now why they wanted a Kripo detective rather than their own people. When the report was filed, when the suicide was confirmed, it would carry the weight of an independent investigation. Clean. Professional. Beyond reproach.

And if the investigation went wrong—if Ehlers found something he was not meant to find, spoke to someone he was not meant to question—then the failure would also be his alone. A Kripo detective who overstepped. Who proved unreliable. Who might himself succumb to the strains of wartime service.

He locked the photograph in his desk alongside the Spandau files. The burglaries could wait. Everything could wait.

Werner Ehlers put on his coat and hat. He had a body in Grunewald to examine, a suicide to confirm, and a very narrow path to walk between the gratitude and disappointment of the most dangerous man in the Reich.

The December air hit him like a slap as he stepped outside. Somewhere in the gray sky, snow was gathering. By nightfall, it would cover the city in white—clean and smooth and hiding everything beneath.
