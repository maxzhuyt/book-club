Agent-Based Reader Response
Some of our most substantial findings came from building the agent-based reader-response system — and the route to them was instructive in itself. Differentiating reader personas proved harder than expected. We first tried two methods for injecting persona-distinctiveness directly into open-weight models: PLUS preference-learning (Nam et al., 2026) and persona contrastive-decoding (Dong et al., 2026). Both failed to produce meaningful differentiation. PLUS yielded summaries indistinguishable from untrained baselines; contrastive decoding produced surface stylistic diversity in how agents talked, but not principled diversity in the judgments they reached [examples table and votes figure]. The lesson, in short, was that current open-weight LLMs can be made to talk differently, but not to judge differently. Meaningful reader personas require a reconfiguration of what these personas attend to — engineering differentiation at the interaction layer through structured attentional probes. A probe is a standardized question that directs a reader-agent's attention to a single facet of a story — its plausibility, the knowledge it presumes, the stability of its world, its handling of convention, or simply what stands out — so that what each reader notices can be compared across agents rather than left to free response (Table 2). This is the central methodological move that organizes the rest of our work.

For each of the 36 stories we convened two synthetic book clubs of ten reader-agents each, designed to differ in one way only. Group A was matched to avid historical-fiction readers on the reader-review website Goodreads; Group B was matched to Group A on every other measurable trait — how much they read, how analytically they wrote, how long their reviews ran — but drawn from readers who rarely read historical fiction based on Goodreads records (Figure 2). The point of the matching was to let a single difference, reading disposition, stand out cleanly against an otherwise similar pair of readers. Each agent then read one of the generated stories in two passes: once responding freely, and once guided by one of five attentional probes developed alongside our earlier annotation schema. The free pass tells us what a reader notices on their own; the probed pass tells us what they notice when directed — and the gap between them is itself a finding. Finally, an independent coder, blind to story, group, and probe, scored all 720 responses on five attention dimensions and a categorical convention frame. Three of the dimensions are graded on a 0–5 scale: period specificity — how precisely a response invokes datable historical knowledge (dates, technologies, material and social facts), from none to rich expert detail; concreteness — whether the reader's observations stay vague impressions or become named and locatable; and outside knowledge — how much the reader brings to bear from beyond what the passage itself states. The remaining two are counts rather than ratings: the number of distinct passage locations the reader points to, and the number of concrete historical anchors — period objects, terms, or events — the reader names. The convention frame is categorical, recording which repertoire of craft a reader reaches for: historical (the conventions of period fidelity, how the past is rendered), generic-genre (the beats of thriller, romance, or noir), mixed (both at once), or none (no conventions invoked).

Two significant findings emerged. First, the probes shape what readers notice. Statistical analysis shows that the choice of probe explains roughly 31% of the variance in the two most meaning-bearing dimensions of attention — what knowledge a reader's response draws on, and how specifically it engages period detail. Each probe elicits a distinct attention signature, and the effect holds within each reader group, not only in aggregate (Figures 3–4). One wrinkle proved telling: the probe effect is stronger in Group B than in Group A. The avid historical fiction readers bring period attention no matter what they are asked; the non-specialist readers are far more responsive to the prompt itself. Disposition and instrument, in other words, are not independent — what a probe can elicit depends on who is being probed, a point that sets up our second and more surprising finding.

Second — and this we did not anticipate — reader disposition surfaces precisely where the probes do not force it. The difference between Group A and Group B concentrates not on the probes that invoke period knowledge directly, but on the two control probes that ask only about the prose and what catches a reader's eye (Figures 5–6). Consider the Song-dynasty story (#9). When a probe asks directly what the text assumes about Chinese Song-dynasty (960–1279 CE) history, both groups deliver: even a matched non-specialist reader supplies, on demand, the 1273 siege of Xiangyang and the minister Jia Sidao, historical anchors the story names only in passing. But when the probe asks merely whether the writing calls attention to itself, the two groups diverge sharply. Reading the same flagged sentence, Group A reaches for the period — "a real minister in 1273 wouldn't be worrying about how historians would judge his feelings" — while Group B reads through generic craft — "autumn = decay and loss. It's pretty, but it's a little heavy-handed."

Two reader types, the same sentence, two opposite frames. This is a disposition effect, not a prompt-following effect. When the apparatus forces a frame, both groups follow it; the difference between them emerges in the silence — where the prompt leaves readers to their own framings, the avid reader's historical priors leak in unprompted. This is exactly the pattern reader-response theory has long predicted: that reading is socially situated, that priors shape what we notice and how we frame it, and that this shows up most clearly when nothing is telling us what to look for. As far as we know, this is the first computational demonstration of the pattern at scale.
The agent-based reader-response system thus measurably differentiates two reader populations across a corpus of counterfactual historical fiction, in ways consistent with the theory that motivated the project. We take this as evidence that an LLM-based architecture can produce socially situated reader feedback rather than the generic response a single model returns on its own — a direction the field has not previously had the instruments to pursue.
Much remains open. The most important next step is to close the loop between reader response and generation: to turn differentiated feedback into a signal the narrative machine — or a human writer working with it — can actually revise against. The accompanying interface is a first step toward this, presenting the differentiation to a human user: readers' two-phase responses are displayed lens by lens, the axes of disagreement the consolidator extracts are surfaced as discrete decisions, and the editorial directive is editable before a revision is generated. Building it out into a working loop a writer can act on is the direction Section 4 develops. Two further questions are equally live. The first is validation: we have shown that our agents differ from one another, but not yet that those differences track real human readers rather than artifacts of the models producing them — and the same interface is the channel through which human evaluators and outside readers enter the system to test exactly that, a task we take up with a new collaborator in Section 4. The second is scope: how to extend reader modeling beyond the single avid/non-specialist axis to the fuller range of communities along which real reading divides.



**A last thread loops back to the contrastive decoding we set aside at the outset — and it may prove the most consequential. Contrastive decoding on its own moved how agents talk, not how they judge; probing moves judgment, but the groups pull apart mainly in the open — asked merely what catches the eye, the avid readers reach for period convention while the non-specialists fall back on generic-genre craft — and largely converge again whenever a probe directs them to a specific facet. Layering the two together begins to do what neither could alone. The convention probe is flat under probing alone — the two groups respond no differently — but with contrastive decoding added, in exploratory runs so far only with Qwen3-32B, a gap between them opens. Overcoming persona homogeneity in this way would unlock LLM social simulation far beyond our domain — a pressing need for the broader simulation fields.**
A final point about the collaboration itself, because it bears directly on what we were able to produce. The framing of the work — what reader response should mean, how narration relates to tacit knowledge, why counterfactual historical fiction is the right test case — came from the humanities side of the team. The means of operationalizing that framing — the open-weight persona experiments, the probe architecture, the coding pipeline that turned 720 free-text responses into analyzable data — came from the computer science side. Neither half would have arrived on its own at an architecture that captures reading as socially situated: pure LLM research has not aimed at this, and humanistic reader-response work has never had instruments at this scale. The most concrete artifact of that union is the probe set itself, which renders attention concepts reader-response theory has worked with for half a century into a form LLM agents can actually execute — without dissolving the theory into mere engineering.
